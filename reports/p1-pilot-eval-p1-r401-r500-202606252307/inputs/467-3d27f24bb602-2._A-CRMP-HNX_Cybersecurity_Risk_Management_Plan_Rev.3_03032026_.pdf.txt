management plan in accordance with the risk management procedure.
This plan includes the following:

Catalog

i) Description of how the manufacturer intends to communicate forthcoming remediations,
patches, and updates to customers.

2.1 General Description of the product
(1) General details
This portable x-ray system (Model: HnX-P1, HnX-PB) consists of an LCD display with up
and down soft-keys for controlling kV, an X-ray generator (line-powered transformer), an X-
ray tube assembly, a collimator and a cart. It can also use a stand instead of a cart. The HnX-
P1 and HnX-PB are used with a film-cassette or flat-panel detector.
This device is a battery-powered portable X-ray System, designed and manufactured by
H&abyz.
Compared with traditional X-ray products, this device has exquisite structure, compact design,
light weight and easy operation.

system control board, high-voltage tank, inverter, collimator (beam limiter), and system
control software running on the system control board. The system control software is for real-
time interaction and control with various circuit modules inside the X-ray generator. The
software responds to user operations on the control panel. The user can adjust and control the
kV and mAs parameters, and the software will display the parameters or directly load the APR
parameters. The software loads the control data from X-ray output into the high-voltage

4. Cybersecurity Risk management process
Cybersecurity Risk management process needs to be iterative, covering each risk in turn, and
returning to earlier steps if risk control measures introduce new hazards or if new information
becomes available.

4.1 Resource Allocation
Effective resource allocation is critical for a successful cybersecurity implementation, which
includes establishing dedicated cybersecurity roles and assigning specific responsibilities to each
member.
4.2 Policy
Cybersecurity management policies should outline the management strategies and protocols for
maintaining cybersecurity during the development, deployment, and maintenance of the medical
device. policies should include guidelines for secure coding practices, regular security assessments,
incident response, and compliance with regulatory requirements.
4.3 Threat modeling
Threat modeling is the process of systematically identifying potential threats and vulnerabilities
that could affect a medical device. This involves creating a comprehensive model that maps out all
possible attack vectors, entry points, and weaknesses in the device's architecture. The goal is to
understand how an attacker might exploit these vulnerabilities and the potential impact on the
device's functionality, data integrity, and patient safety. Techniques such as STRIDE (Spoofing,
Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege) can be
used to categorize and analyze threats.
* STRIDE
The STRIDE algorithm primarily focuses on the security and integrity of the product itself, rather
than its interaction with the surrounding environment. This makes it especially suitable for
medical devices, where the main concern is the device's security. By concentrating on the device,
STRIDE enables a more comprehensive analysis of its inherent security risks.
4.4 Risk assessment
Once potential threats and vulnerabilities have been identified through threat modeling, the next
step is to evaluate the impact and likelihood of each threat. Risk analysis involves assessing the
severity of the impact if a threat were to be realized and the probability of its occurrence.
As a method for risk assessment, we apply CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System).

4.5 Risk Mitigation Plan
After analyzing the risks, a risk mitigation plan is developed to outline the security controls and
measures needed to reduce the identified risks to an acceptable level. This plan should detail specific
actions to be taken, such as implementing encryption, access controls, regular security updates, and
continuous monitoring. The mitigation strategies should address both the likelihood and impact of
the risks, ensuring that vulnerabilities are minimized, and the device is protected against potential
threats.
4.6 Security requirements
Deriving security requirements is a critical step in the design and development phase. This involves
identifying both functional and non-functional security requirements based on the IEC 60601-4-5.
The requirements specify the security features that the device must have, such as user authentication,
access control, and secure communication protocols.
4.7 Security design
Designing a secure system involves creating the device and its components to meet specific security
requirements. This requires developing a detailed security architecture that specifies how data will
be protected, how secure communication will be maintained, and how access to the device will be
controlled.
4.8 Secure Coding Practices
Following secure coding practices is crucial to prevent vulnerabilities in the development of
medical devices. Developers should comply with established secure coding standards, such as those
provided by CWE, to avoid common security pitfalls.
4.9 Testing and Verification
During the Testing and Verification phase, we use a thorough approach to guarantee the security of
the medical device. We employ static analysis tools to identify code vulnerabilities early in the
development process, which helps in detecting and fixing issues before they escalate. Additionally,
dynamic analysis is conducted to test the device in a runtime environment and uncover
vulnerabilities that may not be apparent through static analysis alone.
Cyber tests are divided into cyber tests when developing new products and existing ones according
to internal guidelines.
In the case of new products, cybersecurity requirements are developed and tested in the entire
process from product design input to output and transfer, and if necessary, a third-party testing
agency will conduct it.
In the case of products currently being sold, a cyber security expert monitors cyber issues in the
market quarterly, and if there are any related cyber risks, analyze them through cooperation with
other departments such as the quality team, and develop product changes if necessary.
After the change, conduct a cybersecurity test internally and update the product to the market after
the test verification.
Additionally, cybersecurity modeling as a legal requirement is tested through a third-party agency
when work is required overall.

4.10 Deployment and operation
In the Deployment and Operation phase, the system is securely deployed with all necessary security
configurations applied to ensure a fortified environment. Continuous operational monitoring is
conducted to maintain the security status, including managing and analyzing logs for any
suspicious activity. Effective vulnerability management is critical; this involves swiftly patching
and updating the system upon the discovery of new vulnerabilities to prevent potential security
breaches. This proactive approach ensures that the medical device remains secure and resilient
against evolving threats throughout its operational lifecycle.
4.11 Maintenance
In the Maintenance phase, regular updates and patches are managed to address new security threats
promptly, ensuring the system remains secure. Continuous security monitoring is essential to detect
and respond to potential incidents swiftly, supported by a well-defined incident response plan.
Additionally, user education is crucial; providing security awareness training helps users recognize
and prevent security breaches, reinforcing the overall cybersecurity posture of the medical device
system. This comprehensive maintenance strategy ensures ongoing protection and resilience
against emerging threats.

6. Sources, methods, and frequency for monitoring and
identifying vulnerabilites
6.1 Sources
• SBOM & Component Inventories
- Maintain an up-to-date Software Bill of Materials listing all third-party libraries, firmware
modules, and hardware components.
- Track vendor support status (EOS/EOL) and subscribe to vendor advisories for any new
security notices.
• Public Vulnerability Feeds
- Check NVD (National Vulnerability Database) monthly for CVEs that affect embedded
Linux, Bluetooth stacks, MCU firmwares, and relevant third-party components.
- Review CERT/ICS-CERT bulletins and CISA advisories quarterly to capture any medical-
device–specific or industry-wide alerts.
• Internal Testing & Reports
- Collect scheduled static analysis (SAST) results, dynamic vulnerability-scanner outputs,
and periodic penetration-test reports.
- Log and review any firmware integrity failures (checksum mismatches) from
manufacturing or field updates.
• Field Feedback & Incident Reports
- Monitor customer-reported issues via support tickets for any unexpected behavior that
could indicate a vulnerability.
- Track security incidents involving similar portable X-ray or other medical imaging
devices to identify potential exploit patterns.
6.2 Methods
• Scheduled Static Application Security Testing (SAST)
- Focus on CWE-identified weaknesses such as buffer overflows, injection points, and
improper authentication.
• Dynamic Scanning
- Use a vulnerability scanner against firmware images to detect known CVEs,
misconfigurations, and open ports (e.g., Bluetooth SDP, UART debug interfaces).
• Hardware Security Assessment
- Conduct penetration tests on the MCU and peripheral interfaces (e.g., side-channel
analysis, physical tampering attempts) to detect hardware backdoors or weak protections.
- Review bootloader and firmware update routines to ensure integrity checks (AES-128–
based encryption and checksum) are enforced.
• SBOM Review & Lifecyle Management
- Cross-reference SBOM entries against public CVE feeds. Flag any component
approaching end-of-support and plan for replacement or upgrade.
• Coordinated Vulnerability Disclosure (CVD) Process
- Maintain a public point of contact for researchers to report bugs. Track all disclosures
from initial report through remediation, retest, and documentation.

6.3 Frequency
• Continuous/Real-Time
- Checksum & Integrity Monitoring: Every firmware update (via C-port), verify digital
signature and AES-128 decryption. Any failure triggers an immediate alert.
- Field Incident Monitoring: Support team escalates any security-related customer reports
immediately.
• Monthly
- Query NVD for new CVEs affecting any SBOM component.
- Update SBOM and verify component versions against known-vulnerable lists.
• Quarterly
- Review CERT/ICS-CERT and CISA advisories for industry-specific alerts.
- Run a scheduled SAST scan on the codebase at the end of each quarter, focusing on new
commits since the last scan.
• Annually
- Conduct a full penetration test (hardware + firmware) to catch any issues not covered by
automated tools.
- Reevaluate the entire risk-management file, incorporating new threat models or changes
in regulatory guidance.
• Ad Hoc
- If a critical CVE is published for a component in use (e.g., a Bluetooth stack), initiate an
urgent risk assessment and patch rollout within 30 days.
- Trigger a vulnerability review if a customer-reported anomaly suggests a potential exploit.
7. Identify and address vulnerabilities identified in CISA
Known Exploited Vulnerabilities Catalog
7.1 Overview
The CISA KEV Catalog provides a list of CVEs with confirmed exploits in the wild. Check the
software and firmware components used by the HnX-P1 and HnX-PB portable X-ray system
quarterly for vulnerabilities included in the KEV Catalog, so that you can quickly implement
patches or mitigations to prepare for real-world attacks.

exploited-vulnerabilities-catalog).

7.2.3 Vulnerability Assessment & Prioritization
• Assessment Criteria:
- CVSS Base Score: If score ≥ 9.0 → classify as “Critical” (must remediate within 30
days).
- Public PoC Availability: If a proof-of-concept exploit exists, raise the priority.
- Healthcare Exploitation History: If similar medical devices have been attacked, assign
higher priority.
• Priority Levels:
- Critical: Remediate (patch or mitigation) within 30 days.
- High: Remediate within 60 days.
- Medium/Low: Schedule for the next regular software release or continue monitoring.
7.2.4 Remediation Planning
• Patch Verification & Integration:
- Check if the vendor (e.g., STMicroelectronics, KISA) has released an official patch or
version upgrade for the affected component.
- If so, update the component in our firmware build (for example, upgrade
stm32h7xx_hal_uart.c from v1.2.3 to v1.2.4). Then perform an AES-128 encryption +
Checksum integrity check on the new firmware.
• Backport & Mitigation:
If no official patch exists, choose one of the following:
- Backport: Extract and integrate only the security fix into our codebase, then rebuild.
- Feature Disablement: Temporarily disable the vulnerable functionality (e.g., disable a
specific serial interface or wireless feature).
• Verification:
- Build the patched firmware and run AES-128 encryption + Checksum verification.
- Perform internal testing (including results from penetration tests and static analysis) plus
regression testing to ensure all major features (APR save/load, SID distance measurement,
X-ray output control, etc.) still operate correctly.

7.2.5 Deployment & Field Verification
• Deployment:
- The Customer Support (CS) team uses C-Port (USB-C) to install the patched firmware or
dispatches a field engineer to update on-site.
- After updating, verify on the device’s LCD that the firmware version number matches the
new build.
• Field Verification:
- CS monitors logs from the field (Checksum checks, error logs, unexpected reboots).
- If no issues arise, mark the CVE item as “Closed.” If problems appear, either roll back or
apply additional measures immediately.
7.2.6 Documentation & Internal Reporting
• Risk Management Report Updates:
- CVE ID – Mapped Component – Current Version
- Assessment Result (Priority Level)
- Patched Version & Date Applied
- Verification Results (Test Cases & Summary)
- Deployment Method (C-Port or Field Engineer)
- CS Team Field Verification Results
• Internal Reporting:
- Submit a summary report to the Security, Development, and Regulatory Affairs (RA)
teams, including all relevant documentation for audits or regulatory submissions.
7.3 Post-Deployment Monitoring & Review
7.3.1 Quarterly Review Meetings
• Review KEV CVEs identified and their remediation status over the past quarter.
• For any items not yet completed, reassign priorities and allocate resources to avoid rollover
to the next quarter.
7.3.2 Annual Process Assessment
• Evaluate whether this quarterly KEV monitoring process remains practical and effective.
• Adjust documentation formats, testing procedures, or deployment workflows to improve
efficiency.
• Incorporate any updates from FDA or local regulatory guidance on cybersecurity.
7.3.3 Ad Hoc Customer Incident Response
• If the CS team or field engineers report anomalies after an update (e.g., unexpected reboots,
feature malfunctions), immediately re-run the SBOM-to-KEV mapping to see if additional
CVEs apply.
• If a new vulnerability is found, develop an emergency response plan and accelerate patch
deployment.

10. Update process
* If communication is interrupted or a failure occurs during the update, it may cause damage to the
product's hardware and update files. Therefore, the product's software and firmware updates cannot
be performed directly by users in the field, and must be performed by authorized service personnel
or by returning the product to the manufacturer or service center.
10.1 Preparation
• Obtain security patches from trusted sources
- You should download patches required for vulnerability fixes or feature improvements
from the manufacturer or an authorized software vendor. This ensures the integrity of the
patch itself and effectively eliminates known security risks.
• Patch file encryption
- Encrypt the patch file using a strong encryption algorithm such as AES-128 to prevent the
contents of the patch file from being exposed during transmission.
• Apply digital signature
- Add a digital signature to the patch file using the manufacturer's private key so that the
device can "verify that the patch has not been tampered with" before installation.
10.2 Update Deployment
• Accessing the update interface
- Run an update tool such as STM32CubeProgrammer on a PC connected to the HnX-P1,
HnX-PB product. It is provided in GUI or CLI form, and select the update mode according
to the instructions in the provided manual.
• Digital signature verification
- Just before installing the patch, the device verifies the digital signature of the transmitted
patch file to verify “whether there is a valid manufacturer signature” and “whether there
was any tampering during transmission.”
• Patch installation
- If the signature is valid, the patch file is written to the flash memory area. At this time, the
bootloader is run to replace the firmware by overwriting it.
• Integrity verification
- Once the installation is complete, AES-128 decryption is performed to check whether the
patch file is installed normally, and file integrity is guaranteed through checksum (hash)
comparison.
• Verification of version after reboot
- After the update is complete, reboot the device, and check the firmware version
information displayed on the LCD screen to verify once again “whether it is the expected
new version.”
• How to recover if an abnormality occurs
- If a communication failure or failure occurs during the update, use
STM32CubeProgrammer and ST-Link to enter “Forced Recovery Mode” and try to roll
back to the previous version or reinstall.

11. Patching capability
The device does not connect to the internet or external servers, and therefore does not support
remote or automatic updates.
A regular patching schedule has not been established. Instead, firmware updates are provided as
needed in response to identified issues or vulnerabilities.
Firmware patches are applied manually via a physical connection (e.g., USB Type-C port), and the
update file is verified for integrity prior to installation.
All patches are managed under the company’s internal software maintenance and change control
procedures, in accordance with the secure software development lifecycle (SSDLC).
12. Coordinated vulnerability disclosure and related
processes
H&abyz recognizes that early identification and remediation of security flaws—even those
discovered by external parties—is essential to patient safety and regulatory compliance. To that
end, our CVD process is designed to align with FDA guidance (“Pre- and Postmarket Management
of Cybersecurity in Medical Devices,” 2016) and IEC 81001-5-1 requirements, ensuring that
vulnerabilities are responsibly reported, analyzed, and resolved.
1. Purpose and Scope
• Goal: Provide a clear, secure channel for researchers, customers, and other stakeholders to
report potential vulnerabilities in the HnX-P1 and HnX-PB.
• Benefit: Enables H&abyz to address issues before they can be exploited in clinical
environments and to maintain transparency with regulators and users.
2. Reporting Channels
• Primary Contact: Security team email (e.g., sales@abyzr.com) or a dedicated web form on
our official site.
• Acknowledgment: Every submission receives an automated receipt with a unique tracking ID
upon arrival.
3. Triage and Analysis
• Initial Review (Day 0–1): The security team confirms receipt, assigns a preliminary severity
(Critical/High/Medium/Low), and validates whether the reported issue affects the HnX-P1’s
SBOM components.
• Prioritization Criteria:
- CVSS base score ≥ 9.0 or public proof-of-concept → Critical
- Evidence of industry-wide exploitation → High
- Potential misconfigurations or low-impact bugs → Medium/Low
