INTRODUCTION
Digital radiography was introduced in the Royal Adelaide
Hospital in September 1997 to the Emergency and general
areas of the hospital, and extended into the Intensive Care Unit
(ICU) in October 1998. The images are viewed on a picture
archiving and communication system (PACS) workstation
where available, or a personal computer monitor.
Digital radiography for X-rays and other forms of imaging
has been shown to result in improved turnaround times to the
requesting clinician. 1 This should result in more efficient patient
management and better patient outcomes.
This project assessed performance parameters of digital
radiography in the clinical context. It involved evaluation of the
feedback and opinion from clinicians by questionnaire, and a
time–motion study to assess image turnaround times.
Each parameter assessed in this research was compared
to conventional radiography to observe whether significant
differences existed.
METHODS
Time–motion study
The study was performed in the X-ray department of the
Royal Adelaide Hospital, where an observer was stationed for
5 weeks.
To assess the time difference between digital and con-
ventional radiography, standard start and end points of timing
were adopted. The recorded start time was the time at the
taking of the first image of the patient.The end time was the time
of earliest availability of the image to the requesting clinician.
For conventional images, this was as soon as the film was
released from the X-ray department, and for digital images, this
was as soon as the digital pictures were available to the clinician
on the hospital local network.
To ensure validity of data, only patients referred from
the Emergency Department who required chest X-rays (PA and
lateral) were considered for the study. These patients were then
randomized into conventional or digital groups.

SUMMARY
The time differences between digital and conventional radiography for emergency patients were investigated, and
clinician satisfaction with the two modalities was compared. Time–motion data was collected daily over 5 weeks for a
digital group and a conventional X-ray group. For standardization purposes, only emergency patients requiring chest
X-rays were selected for the study. Data were collected from 30 patients in each of the two groups in the time–motion
study, and 31 out of 50 Emergency and Intensive Care clinicians responded to the questionnaire. Results were analysed
by percentage, and χ2 analysis was used where appropriate. The time for availability of images to requesting clinicians
was 70% less for digital images compared to conventional radiography. The overall satisfaction between digital and
conventional radiography was very similar, but most clinicians expressed an opinion that digital radiography offered
significant image modification advantages.

Thirty patients were monitored for each of the conventional

Clinician questionnaire
A standard questionnaire was designed and circulated to all
clinicians in the Emergency Department and Intensive Care
Unit. All consultants, registrars and resident medical officers of
these departments were included.The questions were intended
to assess the current use of digital radiography, the level of
satisfaction with digital images (compared to conventional
images), and the effect of digital radiography on patient care.
RESULTS
Time–motion study
For the digital group, measured from the defined start to end
times, the total time for the group was 432 min. This equates to
an average of 14.4 min from the taking of the first image. The
range was 4–49 min. In the digital group, repeat X-ray for an
unsatisfactory image was required on one occasion.
For the conventional group, the total time for the group was
1436 min. This averages to 47.9 min from the taking of the first
image to the film being available to Emergency clinicians. The
range was 18–85 min. In the conventional group, repeating of
an unsatisfactory image was required on seven occasions
(Table 1).
Clinician questionnaire
Fifty questionnaires were sent to staff of the Emergency
Department and Intensive Care Unit of the Royal Adelaide
Hospital.The overall response rate was 62%.
Respondents were asked to compare digital radiography to
conventional radiography as ‘not as good as’, ‘equally good as’,
or ‘better than’. Overall, 19% (5/27 felt conventional radiography
was better, 37% (10/27) felt both radiography modalities equally
good, and 44% (12 of 27) indicated digital radiography was
better than conventional radiography (Table 2).
Respondents were asked to indicate their level of
satisfaction with both digital and conventional radiography by
placing a mark along a visual linear analogue scale from
0 to 100. The position of the mark was measured, and
‘satisfaction points’ allocated. Satisfaction with conventional
radiography was rated at 71.4, and digital radiography at
71.7 units (Table 3).
One-hundred percent of respondents said that they found
the image modifying options available with digital radiography,
such as magnification and image intensity variability, were
useful (Table 4).
Clinicians were asked if they believed quicker availability of
digital images make a difference to patient care. They were
asked to respond if they thought this occurred often,
sometimes, rarely, or if there was no difference to patient care.
Overall, 24% (7/29) said quicker digital images often made a
difference, 52% (15/29) said it sometimes made a difference,
14% (4/29) indicated it rarely made a difference, and 10% (3/29)
reported that they thought that quicker digital images did not
make a difference to patient care (Table 5).

DISCUSSION
Time–motion study
It can be seen that there is a consistent trend for much more
rapid availability to the requesting clinician of digital images
compared to conventional images, an average of 14.4 min
compared to 47.9 min.This equates to a 69.9% reduction in time
for availability of images when digital radiography is used in
preference to conventional radiography in emergency situations.
This can be extrapolated to total cost savings due to more rapid
implementation of treatment or discharge of patients. In his
study on digital X-ray systems, Strickland2 felt PACS allowed
‘real-time radiology’, enabling significant cost benefit.
As soon as images are archived, they appear on the monitor
stationed in the requesting department. This means that the
requesting clinician need not even leave their department in
order to view the radiographs of their patients, whereas they
must view conventional films in the X-ray department, or await
their arrival in their own department, a further time con-
sideration. Watkins et al.3 described time advantages where
PACS significantly reduced the time between request and
image availability in ICU for routine X-rays.
It was noted that a significantly higher number of repeated
images was required for the conventional radiography patients
compared to the digital patients (7:1). While not a focus of this
study, the digital image intensity modifying options, as well as
the automatic adjustment for exposure of the digitizer seem to
result in a greatly reduced requirement for repeated images in
digital radiography.
It is still current protocol to produce an 18 ◊ 24 cm hard-copy
film of all archived digital images, which the radiologists use
to report. Lai and Langlois4 found there was a 30% reduction in
reporting time for digital images compared to conventional
methods.
Clinician questionnaire
The results of clinicians’ comparison of digital and conventional
radiography is of great interest. Anecdotally, one finds many
clinicians who are either avid proponents or opponents of digital
radiography, mostly proponents. The reality is that PACS is now
seen as part of a full Electronic Patient Record system.5
The present study found that overall, 44% of respondents
believed that digital radiography was better than conventional,
with only 19% believing the ‘new’ digital radiography was inferior.
Thirty-seven percent believed each modality was equally as
good. The Intensive Care staff responded more favourably
towards digital radiography than the Emergency staff.
Two studies dealing specifically with pulmonary abnor-
malities revealed no significant difference between digital chest
radiographs and conventional film-screen chest radiographs,
except for a lesser ability to detect subtle pneumothoraces
when viewing digital examinations on electronic viewing
consoles. 6,7 Opinion is still proffered by emergency physicians.
It was expected that the image modifying options would
prove useful to clinicians. In a similar study by Watkins, all staff
said that they preferred PACS to the previous, conventional
radiology service.8 This is an often remarked feature of the
digital system, especially the ability to magnify sections of
images with great clarity. The image inversion feature seems
especially valued for rib lesions or fractures.
CONCLUSION
The time–motion data indicates a 70% reduction in turnaround
times for digital X-ray images compared with conventional films
to the requesting doctor. This in turn should result in cases
of improved patient care, more rapid discharge times, and
improved efficiency in medical decision making.
Fewer repeat X-rays were required for digital imaging.
Clinician satisfaction with the digital and conventional X-ray
groups was comparable, but with the image modification
options available with digital images being particularly valued.
