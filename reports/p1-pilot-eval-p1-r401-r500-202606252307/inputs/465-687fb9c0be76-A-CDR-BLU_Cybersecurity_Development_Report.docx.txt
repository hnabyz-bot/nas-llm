Overview

Scope

The Cybersecurity Development Report for the G Series Detector documents all the activities and procedures taken to ensure the cybersecurity of this specific device, in accordance with the IEC 81001-5-1 standard. This report includes detailed records of architecture analysis, threat modeling, vulnerability analysis, identification of security requirements, risk assessment, and mitigation. Additionally, it covers the security frameworks and protocols adhered to during the development of the G Series Detector as well as the security features integrated into its software and hardware components. By thoroughly documenting these aspects, the report provides a comprehensive overview of the cybersecurity measures implemented throughout the development lifecycle of the G Series Detector.

This document applies to all software under development or in progress, or which was completed, but not to the following cases:

① Research activities

② Initial technical feasibility assessment activities

③ Activities to support prototypes that are not intended to be provided to customers

Purpose

The purpose of the Cybersecurity Development Report for G Series Detector is to demonstrate compliance with the IEC 81001-5-1 standard, ensuring that this specific device meets all cybersecurity requirements. This report aims to confirm that the G Series Detector is equipped with appropriate protective measures against cyber threats, thus safeguarding patient safety and maintaining data integrity. Cybersecurity Development Plan

Resource Allocation

Effective resource allocation is critical for a successful cybersecurity implementation, which includes establishing dedicated cybersecurity roles and assigning specific responsibilities to each member.

Policy

Cybersecurity management policies should outline the management strategies and protocols for maintaining cybersecurity during the development, deployment, and maintenance of the medical device. policies should include guidelines for secure coding practices, regular security assessments, incident response, and compliance with regulatory requirements.

Threat modeling

Threat modeling is the process of systematically identifying potential threats and vulnerabilities that could affect a medical device. This involves creating a comprehensive model that maps out all possible attack vectors, entry points, and weaknesses in the device's architecture. The goal is to understand how an attacker might exploit these vulnerabilities and the potential impact on the device's functionality, data integrity, and patient safety. Techniques such as STRIDE (Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, Elevation of Privilege) can be used to categorize and analyze threats.

* STRIDE

The STRIDE algorithm primarily focuses on the security and integrity of the product itself, rather than its interaction with the surrounding environment. This makes it especially suitable for medical devices, where the main concern is the device's security. By concentrating on the device, STRIDE enables a more comprehensive analysis of its inherent security risks.

Risk analysis

Once potential threats and vulnerabilities have been identified through threat modeling, the next step is to evaluate the impact and likelihood of each threat. Risk analysis involves assessing the severity of the impact if a threat were to be realized and the probability of its occurrence.

Risk Mitigation Plan

After analyzing the risks, a risk mitigation plan is developed to outline the security controls and measures needed to reduce the identified risks to an acceptable level. This plan should detail specific actions to be taken, such as implementing encryption, access controls, regular security updates, and continuous monitoring. The mitigation strategies should address both the likelihood and impact of the risks, ensuring that vulnerabilities are minimized, and the device is protected against potential threats.

Security requirements

Deriving security requirements is a critical step in the design and development phase. This involves identifying both functional and non-functional security requirements based on the IEC 60601-4-5. The requirements specify the security features that the device must have, such as user authentication, access control, and secure communication protocols.

Security design

Designing a secure system involves creating the device and its components to meet specific security requirements. This requires developing a detailed security architecture that specifies how data will be protected, how secure communication will be maintained, and how access to the device will be controlled.

Secure Coding Practices

Following secure coding practices is crucial to prevent vulnerabilities in the development of medical devices. Developers should comply with established secure coding standards, such as those provided by CWE, to avoid common security pitfalls.

Testing and Verification

During the Testing and Verification phase, we use a thorough approach to guarantee the security of the medical device. We employ static analysis tools to identify code vulnerabilities early in the development process, which helps in detecting and fixing issues before they escalate. Additionally, dynamic analysis is conducted to test the device in a runtime environment and uncover vulnerabilities that may not be apparent through static analysis alone.

Cyber tests are divided into cyber tests when developing new products and existing ones according to internal guidelines.

In the case of new products, cybersecurity requirements are developed and tested in the entire process from product design input to output and transfer, and if necessary, a third-party testing agency will conduct it.

In the case of products currently being sold, a cyber security expert monitors cyber issues in the market quarterly, and if there are any related cyber risks, analyze them through cooperation with other departments such as the quality team, and develop product changes if necessary.

After the change, conduct a cybersecurity test internally and update the product to the market after the test verification.

Additionally, cybersecurity modeling as a legal requirement is tested through a third-party agency when work is required overall.

Deployment and operation

In the Deployment and Operation phase, the system is securely deployed with all necessary security configurations applied to ensure a fortified environment. Continuous operational monitoring is conducted to maintain the security status, including managing and analyzing logs for any suspicious activity. Effective vulnerability management is critical; this involves swiftly patching and updating the system upon the discovery of new vulnerabilities to prevent potential security breaches. This proactive approach ensures that the medical device remains secure and resilient against evolving threats throughout its operational lifecycle.

Maintenance

In the Maintenance phase, regular updates and patches are managed to address new security threats promptly, ensuring the system remains secure. Continuous security monitoring is essential to detect and respond to potential incidents swiftly, supported by a well-defined incident response plan. Additionally, user education is crucial; providing security awareness training helps users recognize and prevent security breaches, reinforcing the overall cybersecurity posture of the medical device system. This comprehensive maintenance strategy ensures ongoing protection and resilience against emerging threats.

Identification of Vulnerabilities

Vulnerability monitoring and scanning

It is crucial to monitor and scan for vulnerabilities to ensure the cybersecurity of medical devices. The process of identifying and addressing vulnerabilities depends on whether the technical asset is primarily software or hardware. Utilizing the Common Weakness Enumeration (CWE) framework can systematically help in identifying and mitigating vulnerabilities in both software and hardware components.

Cybersecurity Requirements (IEC 60601-4-5)

The requirements outlined in IEC 60601-4-5 emphasize the importance of device integrity, patient safety, and the protection of sensitive data throughout the device's lifecycle. These requirements have been thoroughly analyzed and organized according to the IEC 60601-4-5 standard, which offers guidance on safety-related technical security specifications for medical electrical equipment. This structured approach ensures that the devices comply with regulatory expectations and meet international standards.

Software components

Vulnerability assessment for software components can be effectively performed using Static Application Security Testing (SAST). SAST involves analyzing the software's source code, bytecode, or binary code to identify potential security weaknesses without executing the code. This method is aligned with the CWE framework, which categorizes common software vulnerabilities such as buffer overflows (CWE-119), SQL injection (CWE-89), and cross-site scripting (CWE-79). Integrating CWE identifiers into SAST tools helps systematically detect and address these vulnerabilities throughout the cybersecurity development lifecycle.

Hardware Components

In order to assess hardware components for vulnerabilities, a collaborative effort between developers and cybersecurity experts is necessary. This may involve working with accredited testing laboratories. Identifying hardware vulnerabilities often requires detailed discussions and evaluations, which can include physical penetration testing and firmware analysis. While the Common Weakness Enumeration (CWE) primarily focuses on software, it also includes categories for hardware vulnerabilities, such as those related to physical tampering. By using CWE identifiers, developers and testers can ensure comprehensive coverage of potential hardware weaknesses.

SBOM (Software Bill of Materials)

Managing an SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) for medical device cybersecurity involves creating a detailed inventory of all software components, continuously monitoring for vulnerabilities. Key actions include regular updates and maintenance of the SBOM, conducting risk assessments, and integrating it into incident response plans.

When End of Support (EOS) or End of Life (EOL) information for software components in the SBOM is not available, it's important to take proactive measures to ensure ongoing cybersecurity. The following steps outline a strategy for effectively managing such components:

1) Regular Vulnerability Analysis – Manual scanning and reviews

2) Risk Mitigation - Patching and Updates

3) Documentation and Justification - Risk Assessment, Justification for Use

This systematic process assesses the severity and potential impact of security flaws in our software and hardware systems. By using CVSS 3.1, we can measure risk levels through a detailed evaluation of exploitability and impacts on confidentiality, integrity, and availability. This method ensures that we accurately identify and prioritize vulnerabilities for remediation, ultimately enhancing our security posture. Specially for medical devices, this approach aligns with regulatory standards, ensuring patient safety and compliance. However, as this evaluation is for medical devices, neither Temporal scores nor environmental scores are not considered.

Risk Evaluation

In medical device cybersecurity, risk evaluation follows the NIST 800-30 guideline and ISO 14971:2019 standard. This is done by multiplying the impact and likelihood to classify risk levels. The levels include, critical, high, medium, low, and very low, and they determine the acceptability of the risk. Risks at critical, High and Medium levels are deemed unacceptable and require immediate mitigation. On the other hand, Low, Very Low are acceptable with regular monitoring. This method ensures that every risk is meticulously assessed and managed, leaving no room for cyber threats to cause harm.

Risk Control

Risk control in medical device cybersecurity involves implementing strategies to reduce identified risks. This can be achieved by taking preventative action to lower the likelihood of a threat occurring, or by taking contingency action to minimize the impact if a threat does occur. The key components of risk control include:

1) Access Control: Implement strong authentication and authorization mechanisms

2) Network Security: Use firewalls, intrusion detection/prevention systems (IDS/IPS), and secure communication protocols (e.g., TLS, HTTPS)

3) Data Encryption: Encrypt sensitive data to protect its confidentiality and integrity

4) Regular Updates and Patching: Ensure timely updates and patches to address known vulnerabilities

5) Redundancy: Design systems with redundancy

6) Security Guidelines: Step-by-step instructions on how to securely operate the device

7) Warning Labels: Include labels on how to protect the device from common cybersecurity threats

Residual Risk Assessment After Risk Control

In medical device cybersecurity, residual risk assessment involves evaluating the remaining risks after implementing risk controls. This process, based on NIST 800-30 and ISO 14971, includes reassessing the likelihood and impact of identified threats to ensure they meet acceptable levels, and it ensures patient safety and data integrity. Continuous risk management and compliance with regulatory standards are ensured through this process

Risk Arising from Risk Control Measure

In medical device cybersecurity, implementing risk control measures may introduce new risks. According to NIST 800-30 and ISO 14971, these risks may include potential system incompatibilities, performance degradation, or unforeseen vulnerabilities. It is essential to continuously monitor and reassess these emerging risks for effective management.

Risk/Benefit analysis

In the medical device cybersecurity, a risk/benefit analysis evaluates the potential risks associated with security measures in comparison to the advantages of safeguarding patient data and maintaining device integrity. Drawing from NIST 800-30 and ISO 14971, this analysis guarantees that cybersecurity controls do not bring about undue risks and that the overall benefits to patient safety and data security warrant the implementation of these measures.

Interoperability functionality

Medical devices need to consider cybersecurity due to their ability to interact with other medical or IT devices, as well as functions related to healthcare infrastructure such as networks, Electronic Medical Records (EMR), and medical imaging systems. It is crucial to implement strong cybersecurity measures in these interfaces to ensure the safety and effectiveness of the medical device system. To verify interoperability functionality, we have added the MDS2 (Manufacturer Disclosure Statement for Medical Device Security) checklist.

The MDS2 provides a standardized framework for manufacturers to disclose the security features of their devices. It helps identify and address potential cybersecurity risks associated with interoperability by detailing how devices handle data security, user authentication, and other security controls. This facilitates thorough evaluation and management of cybersecurity in medical device systems

Update Management

It's crucial to manage updates for medical devices as part of cybersecurity to promptly address vulnerabilities and safeguard patient safety and device integrity. Following guidelines from the FDA and NIST, update management includes identifying, acquiring, testing, and deploying software updates to fix vulnerabilities or enhance functionality.

Update Target

Update targets for medical device cybersecurity include software components, firmware, security patches, and configuration files to ensure the device remains secure and functional.

1) Firmware: Microcontroller firmware, device drivers, and other low-level system software.

2) Security Patches: Specific updates aimed at addressing known vulnerabilities.

3) Configuration Files: Updates to security settings and configurations.

Update process

The update can only be done by a CS Team representative on the H&abyz with permissions and can be executed by changing the configuration file during installation.

Secure patches are obtained from trusted and verified sources to address specific vulnerabilities or improvements required for the medical device. This ensures the patches are reliable and mitigate known security risks.

Security Incident Response Activities

The Security Incident Response Plan outlines a structured approach for handling cybersecurity incidents related to G SERIES DETECTOR. It has been developed in accordance with FDA and NIST guidelines, ensuring effective management of security incidents to protect patient safety and maintain device integrity. The plan incorporates the Incident Response Life Cycle, which includes preparation, detection and analysis, containment, eradication and recovery, and post-incident activities, to systematically address and mitigate cybersecurity threats.

Preparation

In order to effectively manage cybersecurity incidents, it is essential to continuously monitor. Incident detection involves automated alerts, user reports, and proactive monitoring. An initial analysis is conducted to assess the nature, scope, and impact of the incident, which includes data collection and analysis. Incidents are then categorized based on severity and their impact on device functionality and patient safety. Comprehensive documentation of all findings, including the incident timeline, initial observations, and analysis results, is also undertaken.

Detection and Analysis

Implement continuous monitoring using IDS/IPS, SIEM systems, and other security tools to detect anomalies and potential incidents. Identify incidents through automated alerts, user reports, and proactive monitoring. Conduct an initial analysis to determine the nature, scope, and impact of the incident by collecting and analyzing logs, alerts, and other relevant data. Classify the incident based on its severity and impact on device functionality and patient safety. Document all findings, including the incident timeline, initial observations, and analysis results, to ensure comprehensive understanding and effective response.

Containment, Eradication, and Recovery

To effectively contain an incident, take immediate actions to limit its spread and impact. This includes isolating affected systems or devices for short-term containment. Then, develop and execute a long-term containment strategy to fully control the incident. Eradicate the root cause by removing malware, closing vulnerabilities, and applying patches. During the recovery phase, restore affected systems and devices to normal operation and validate that they are fully operational and secure through thorough testing to ensure no residual issues remain

Post-Incident Activity

Conduct a detailed root cause analysis to identify the cause of the incident and understand how it occurred. Implement corrective actions to address the immediate issue and mitigate its impact, and develop preventive actions to prevent similar incidents in the future, including process improvements, enhanced monitoring, and additional training. Document all aspects of the incident response, including findings, actions taken, and lessons learned.
