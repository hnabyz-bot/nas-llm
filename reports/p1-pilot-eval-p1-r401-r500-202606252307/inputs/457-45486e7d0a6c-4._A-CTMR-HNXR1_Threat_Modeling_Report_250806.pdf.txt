Intended use HnX-R1 (Digital X-ray Imaging System) is indicated for digital
imaging solution designed for providing general radiographic
diagnosis of human anatomy targeting both adult and children. It
is intended to replace film based radiographic diagnostic systems.
Not to be used for mammography.
The HnX-R1 wired/wireless digital X-ray imaging system consists of Detector, Power supply box,
Battery pack, Battery charger, Other Accessories and software for image acquisition and image
processing and does not include the X-ray generator. The detector is a wired/wireless type, and
there are 8 types of G series (G1417CW, G1417CWP, G1417FW, G1417FWP, G1717CW,
G1717CWP, G1717FW, G1717FWP) and 3 types of 2/3G series (A1717MCW, A1417MCW,
F1417MCW). The name of the image acquisition and image processing software (Console
software) is HnVUE.
This system is used to capture images by transmitting X-ray to a patient’s body. The X-ray passing
through a patient’s body is sent to the detector and then converted into electrical signals. These
signals go through the process of amplification and digital data conversion in the signal process
device before being sent to the HnVUE (Console Software) and saved in DICOM file, a standard
for medical imaging. The captured images are sent to the Picture Archiving & Communication
System (PACS) server, and can be used for reading images.
The cybersecurity risk of the HnX-R1 have been addressed to assure that no new or increased
cybersecurity risks were introduced as a part of device risk analysis. These risks are defined as
sequence of events leading to a hazardous situation, and the control for their risks were treated and
implemented as proposed in the risk analysis (e.g., requirements, verification)

2. Threat modeling Summary
The threat modeling document for the Medical Device HnX-R1 (Manufacture: H&abyz Co., Ltd)
aims to evaluate potential cybersecurity threats and pinpoint vulnerabilities within the device. This
comprehensive assessment identifies possible attack vectors, evaluates the likelihood and impact
of various threats, and proposes mitigations to enhance the security posture. By systematically
analyzing the device's architecture, data flow, and interfaces, the document ensures that security
measures are robust and aligned with industry standards. In total 67 risks have been identified
during the threat modeling process:
The key findings and outcomes of each test are summarized below:
a) IEC 60601-4-5 Compliance Testing (RT): Ensures medical devices meet requirement for
cybersecurity (35 initial risks)
b) CWE Vulnerability Assessment (VT): Detects and analyzes weaknesses to prevent cyber
threats (94initial risks)
c) Penetration Testing (PT): Simulates attacks to identify and fix security vulnerabilities in
systems. (7 initial risks)
3. Data-Flow Diagram
A Data-Flow Diagram (DFD) in medical device cybersecurity is a graphical representation that
maps out the flow of data within a medical system. It highlights how data moves between devices,
systems, and users, identifying points where data is input, processed, stored, and output. By
visualizing these flows, DFDs help identify potential vulnerabilities in data handling, transmission,
and storage.
The report's diagram contains the following views: Global System View, Multi-Patient Harm View,
Updateability/Patch-ability View, and Security Use Case View(s). These views are visually
represented in the diagram and are explained in detail through the accompanying legend. The
legend helps to clarify the purpose and scope of the depicted views in the diagram.

4. hreat Identification and Methodology
Threat Methodology: The STRIDE methodology is selected for its comprehensiveness in
identifying Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and
Elevation of Privilege, which are relevant to the HnX-P1 security needs.
Rationale for STRIDE: STRIDE provides a structured approach to identifying threats that could
compromise the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the system, making it suitable for
complex systems where multiple interaction points exist, such as medical devices.

6. Risks Assessment
6.1 Likelihood
Frequent (F) Exploitation is very easy and likely to occur frequently (e.g. public vulnerability)
Very Likely (VL) Exploitation is possible and likely to be exploited by malicious attackers
Likely (L) Exploitation is possible under certain conditions, but technically difficult
Unlikely (U) Exploitation is difficult and requires advanced technical knowledge
Very Unlikely (VU) Exploitation is practically impossible (e.g. hardware access required)
6.2 Impact
Very High (VH) Critical to system safety and possible legal and regulatory violations
High (H) Major device function failure, possible direct risk to patient safety
Medium (M) Possible to degrade system performance or impact data integrity
Low (L) Possible to cause partial function failure or some inconvenience to user experience
Very Low (VL) Limitless to system operation
6.3 Risk Level
Critical (C) Extremely hazardous, requiring immediate security action. Potential direct threat to
patient safety.
