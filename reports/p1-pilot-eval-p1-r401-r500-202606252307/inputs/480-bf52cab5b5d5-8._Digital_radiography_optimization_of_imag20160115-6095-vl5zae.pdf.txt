ORIGINAL ARTICLE
Digital radiography: optimization of image quality and dose
using multi-frequency software
H. Precht & O. Gerke & K. Rosendahl & A. Tingberg &
D. Waaler
Received: 1 November 2011 / Revised: 21 February 2012 / Accepted: 1 March 2012
# Springer-Verlag 2012
Abstract
Background New developments in processing of digital
radiographs (DR), including multi-frequency processing
(MFP), allow optimization of image quality and radiation
dose. This is particularly promising in children as they are
believed to be more sensitive to ionizing radiation than
adults.
Objective To examine whether the use of MFP software
reduces the radiation dose without compromising quality
at DR of the femur in 5-year-old-equivalent anthropomor-
phic and technical phantoms.
Materials and methods A total of 110 images of an anthro-
pomorphic phantom were imaged on a DR system (Canon
DR with CXDI-50 C detector and MLT[S] software) and
analyzed by three pediatric radiologists using Visual Grad-
ing Analysis. In addition, 3,500 images taken of a technical
contrast-detail phantom (CDRAD 2.0) provide an objective
image-quality assessment.
Results Optimal image-quality was maintained at a dose
reduction of 61% with MLT(S) optimized images. Even for
images of diagnostic quality, MLT(S) provided a dose reduc-
tion of 88% as compared to the reference image. Software
impact on image quality was found significant for dose (mAs),
dynamic range dark region and frequency band.
Conclusion By optimizing image processing parameters, a
significant dose reduction is possible without significant
loss of image quality.
Keywords Digital radiography . Software optimization .
Multi-frequency processing . Dose reduction
Introduction
Digital radiography (DR) has shown great potential for
improving image quality and/or reducing radiation dose
[1] due to new post-processing techniques, detector design
and new ways of optimizing technical parameters. Unfortu-
nately, these techniques are not fully exploited because their
potential has not been sufficiently explored and because
many users remain unaware of the potential benefits in
clinical practice [2].
Multi-frequency processing (MFP) is a relatively new
software tool for DR systems [3]. The technical principle

is to decompose the image into a series of sub-frequency
images and then to fuse these into one single image with
optimized contrast. The advantages of MFP are fewer artifacts
and higher spatial resolution, however image noise will
increase if a high-frequency band is chosen [4–6]. There
are few publications on the potential benefits of MFP in
terms of dose reduction versus image quality. This is of
particular interest in pediatric imaging, as children younger
than 10 are up to four times more sensitive to ionizing
radiation compared to adults 30–40 years old [5, 6], which in
part may be due to a longer expected remaining lifespan
(stochastic effect) [7].
Pediatric radiography poses great demands on the radio-
logical team in choosing correct imaging parameters and
patient positioning, as well as knowledge of image process-
ing algorithms. Children are particularly challenging due to
constant growth and changes in tissue composition [8, 9].
As variations in both dose and image quality in pediatric
radiology are among the largest within the radiological field,
optimization is particularly important [8, 10–12].
The aim of our study was to examine whether the use of
multi-Laplacian transformation sub-band pyramidal
enhancement-technology (MLT[S]) may allow dose reduc-
tion without significant loss of image quality. The process-
ing parameters explored include brightness, contrast, edge
enhancement, frequency band, noise reduction and dynamic
range (dark/bright region).
Materials and methods
Two different image sets were produced and analyzed, one
including 110 radiographs of an anthropomorphic phantom,
and one including 3,500 radiographs of a technical phan-
tom. The images were produced with different exposure
settings and image processing parameters.
DR system, detector and software
All radiographs were produced using a Canon DR system
with the CXDI-50 C detector and MLT(S) MFP version 7.0.
According to the manufacturer, the preferred exposure index
value, Reached EXposure index (REX), should be between
100 and 450 depending on dose, region of interest (ROI)
and image processing settings. For each exposure, the ROI
was manually set to cover precisely the same anatomical
region in the image, and the REX-value was recorded to
detect any bias caused by the operator, technique or gener-
ator. The pre-processing settings were based on documented
research [4, 7] and recommendations by the manufacturer
for clinical practice [5]. For the images of the anthropomor-
phic phantom it was important to implement a combination
of MLT(S) parameters; therefore the images were combined
in accordance with theory [4, 5] and optimized according to
the preferences by seven specialized radiographers. This
part of the study included ten images processed with MLT
(S) and one image processed with MLT (M) (an earlier
software version), to allow comparison of previous and
present options in software processing. For the images of
the technical phantom each MLT(S) parameter was adjusted,
one at a time, in 4–6 different steps.
Phantoms
In previous studies, animal phantoms have been used to
simulate human anatomy [13, 14]. A lamb was used as our
anthropomorphic phantom, since the femur size of a 5-year-
old child corresponds to that of a 6-month-old lamb accord-
ing to the National Radiological Protection Board [8]. A
conventional contrast-detail radiography phantom
(CDRAD) was used as technical phantom [15]. The
CDRAD phantom experiment was conducted using a plastic
container with 15-cm depth of water placed above the phan-
tom, corresponding to the soft tissues of a 5-year-old [16,
17]. The results of the CDRAD images were registered
quantitatively, thus giving an objective assessment of con-
trast and spatial resolution [4, 7]. If using a technical phan-
tom alone, the results would be difficult to compare directly
to the anatomical structures of children in clinical practice.
This was why the combination of an anthropomorphic and a
technical phantom was considered optimal for this study.
Technical settings
The exposures of both the anthropomorphic and the techni-
cal phantoms were carried out at 60 kV with a total filtration
of 5.2-mm Al. The source-to-detector distance (SID) was set
at 100 cm and the field sizes were chosen to correspond to
the size of the femur in the anthropomorphic phantom or to
the size of the technical phantom, respectively. Because
there were no reference dose levels available, the starting
point for the dose variation was set to 16 mAs,
corresponding to the Danish national board’s pediatric radi-
ology project [18]. The dose was gradually scaled down
from 16 mAs to the lowest possible value at 0.5 mAs. At
every exposure level the skin dose of the phantom was
measured with an Unfors Xi dosimeter, as an average value
of ten exposures. All parameters, except those for dose and
image processing (Table 1) were kept constant across
exposures.

tom was carried out using relative visual grading analysis
(VGA) [19]. VGA is a way of quantifying subjective image

Statistics
Data were analyzed and displayed descriptively by type, i.e.,
continuous data and categorical data were summarized by
means of descriptive statistics (arithmetic mean and stan-
dard deviation) and frequencies with corresponding percen-
tages, respectively.
VGA scores were investigated using ordered logistic re-
gression (proportional odds model), where VGA scale scores
(negative, zero, positive) were to be explained by dose and
MLT(S) parameters, and odds ratios (OR) were given. Inter-
rater agreement was assessed by Fleiss’ κ for three raters [30],
and a respective 95% confidence interval (CI) was given using
bootstrap techniques [31, 32]. Significance level was 5%.
InvIQF values were analyzed using linear regression,
where dose and MLT(S) parameters were included as ex-
planatory factors. A non-linear B-spline fit was added for
the purpose of visualizing the dose–response relationship.
All analyses were performed using ©SAS 9.1.3 (SAS
Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA) and ©Stata/MP 11 (Stata-

Results
The images of the anthropomorphic phantom were judged to
have lower quality than the reference image in 48% by
reader 1, 77% by reader 2 and 44 % by reader 3 (Table 4).
A higher dose resulted in a higher VGA score (results not
shown here). This was in line for all radiologists, but was
most prominent for radiologists 1 and 3. With increasing
dose the percentage of assessments that were poorer than
reference decreased for all readers (Fig. 1). For readers 1 and
2 this decrease extended over the whole investigated dose
range whereas the respective percentage for reader 3
dropped dramatically as dose was increased from 4 to 5
mAs.
The image quality was judged appropriate for the diag-
nosis of fractures in 107/110 (97%), 71/110 (65%) and 105/
110 (95%) of the cases by the three readers, respectively.
The inter-observer agreement was low with a κ-value of 0
0.23 (95% CI: 0.15, 0.32).
Building a joint proportional odds model with reader as
explanatory variable failed since the score test for the pro-
portional odds assumption indicated the inadequacy of this
assumption (P00.0008). Therefore, models were applied
stratified by reader for exploratory purposes.
For reader one, three significant factors were found: dose
(mAs), dynamic range dark region and frequency. The gen-
eralized ORs (95% CI) were estimated to 1.5 (1.3, 1.7) for
dose, 0.8 (0.7, 0.95) for dynamic range dark region and 1.3
(1.003, 1.7) for frequency band (Table 5) suggesting that for
dose, the probability of observing an image quality just as
good or better than the reference image was enhanced at
higher doses. Similarly, the probability for observing an
image quality just as good as or better than the reference
image was poorer for higher values of dynamic range
dark-region settings. For readers two and three, dose was
the only statistically significant factor with an OR [95% CI]
of 1.4 [1.3, 1.7] and 1.4 [1.3, 1.6], respectively (results not
shown). To summarize, the dose parameter was the only
statistically significant factor for all readers.

Overall VGA showed that MLT(S) optimized images
supported a dose reduction from 16 to 6.3 mAs (61%)
without significant loss of image quality. Entrance Skin
Dose (ESD) was reduced from 311 to 125 μGy (61%
reduction). A further reduction to 2 mAs was also found
acceptable by all three radiologists, although image quality
was notably reduced, corresponding to 88% in dose and
reaching an ESD of 38 μGy (Fig. 2).
For the CDRAD experiments there was no significant
difference in InvIQF by adjusting contrast, brightness, dy-
namic range adjustment, frequency or edge enhancement in
the order of the flowchart of the manufacturer (the guidance
for the sequence of setting the MLT(S) parameters [5, 33].
All the MLT(S) parameters were adjusted one by one, while
keeping the other parameters constant as an average value to
the further testing. The only MLT(S) parameter that resulted
in a significant difference in InvIQF values was the noise
reduction parameter. The InvIQF increased with increased
noise reduction.
InvIQF values were analyzed using linear regression,
where dose and MLT(S) parameters were included as ex-
planatory factors. The model described our data with R 2 0
0.92, indicating that 92% of the variation could be explained
through the independent variables. In total, two significant
factors were found at the 5% level, namely dose (mAs) and
noise reduction. The point estimates (95% CI) were 0.20
(0.19, 0.21) and 0.07 (0.05, 0.10), suggesting that if dose is
increased with one mAs, InvIQF is enhanced with 0.20
units. Increased dose and reduced noise were found to have
a positive influence on InvIQF (Table 6). A positive dose–
response relationship was observed in which a non-
parametrical B-spline fit reduced to piecewise linear poly-
nomials flattening out at higher doses.
Discussion

We have shown that a dose reduction of up to 61% can be
achieved without impairing image quality by using MLT(S)
optimized images, as compared to reference radiographs. At
a reduced image-quality level, but still clinically acceptable
for diagnosis, optimized images allowed for exposures as
low as 2 mAs (compared to the standard 16 mAs), empha-
sizing the importance of optimizing digital radiographs
according to ALARA. The reduction was 88% and the
effective dose was reduced from 79 μSv to 4 μSv.
To enable this dose reduction images were optimized
with MLT(S) software. For DR of the femur the processing
parameters dynamic range, dark region and high-frequency
band, had to assume a low value to visualize the fine osseous

reduction were required to be high at high doses and low
for low doses. It can be concluded that at the dose levels used
in this study, either the noise did not influence the radiologists
or, more likely, did not cause a significant loss of detail.
Likewise it was seen that noise reduction was large at high
levels of edge enhancement, thus compensating for the loss of
detail. By optimizing the image processing parameters thor-
oughly, we showed that a heavily processed image was pre-
ferred. This supported the assumption that MLT(S) software
was capable of maintaining the natural appearance of the
image.
The VGA of the second reader differed considerably
from that of the other two (Table 4). As the VGA is a
subjective evaluation of image quality each radiologist will
perceive the quality differently. Hence, the outcome of this
study is not unexpected and may have several causes, such
as differences in age, diagnostic experience, preferences, etc.,
among the radiologists. Independent reading by three radi-
ologists was performed exactly for this reason.
The results from the CDRAD studies contradicted the
results obtained with the anthropomorphic phantom. Thus
the CDRAD phantom cannot be recommended for optimiz-
ing software parameters although it has been used for opti-
mization in other studies [29]. Despite varying the MLT(S)
processing parameters of contrast, brightness, dynamic range,
frequency band and edge enhancement, to achieve an opti-
mized image, no significant change in the InvIQF value was
observed. The main reasons for this were: (1) The compo-
sition of the CDRAD phantom, more specifically the homo-
geneous absorption of the phantom with water in front, made
it impossible to assess the dynamic range in the image
quality. The CDRAD phantom does not contain any
anatomical structures (neither normal anatomical background
nor pathology) and thus does not resemble the clinical task
of the observer. The theory behind the CDRAD phantom is
based on the Rose-model that assumes that it is the photonic
noise that is the limiting factor in detection of low-contrast
details [34]. This is true regarding the CDRAD phantom,
but not in general for detection of pathology where the
limiting factor for low-contrast detail detection will be due
to disturbing anatomical background. (2) The calculations in
the CDRAD analysis software, which are based on statistical
standard deviations comparing the small ROI in the back-
ground of every square with the ROI in the middle of the
puncture in the square, made it impossible to assess the
sharpness of structural edges, difference in the contrast and
brightness, and change in larger structures. These character-
istics are important image-quality factors with great impor-
tance in real diagnostic images. The influences of dose and of
noise reduction were the only positively significant factors
found. Image quality is a subjective phenomenon character-
ized by complex interactions between the MLT(S) parameters,
whereas in the technical tests only one parameter at a time is
adjusted. The aim for image processing is both to present the
image data for the radiologists in an optimal way and to ensure
that the quality of the diagnosis is an interaction between the
clinical task and the ability of the radiologist to use the data
correctly. This means that it is not only the technical param-
eters (spatial resolution, contrast etc) that are important, but
these were the only image-quality factors that could be
assessed using the CDRAD phantom. Based on this, any
optimization of software and dose factors requires experi-
ments on anthropomorphic phantoms including analysis of
image quality by experienced observers before implementation
in clinical practice. The technical test confirmed that radiolog-
ists’ assessments needs to be considered the true reference.
Regression techniques are commonly used to investigate
dose–response relationships, which usually are of non-linear
nature (see for example [35]). To model a non-linear dose–
response relationship several parametric (but non-linear),
semi-parametric and non-parametric techniques are avail-
able (e.g. [36, 37]) from which we chose B-splines to fit a
non-linear curve to our observations. Due to the investiga-
tion of only four discrete points on the dose range for each
of which there were 70 observations, the non-parametrical
B-spline fit reduced to piecewise linear polynomials, mean-
ing that average values at consecutive dose levels were
simply connected by straight lines.
The limitation of this study was the use of a lamb femur
as the anthropomorphic phantom. Since the processing
parameters were constructed for the lamb phantom, which
is not identical to the human anatomy, albeit comparable, it
needs to be interpreted with caution. As the size of the lamb

Conclusion
The study showed great opportunities in the MLT(S) soft-
ware as the anthropomorphic experiments of the project
supported the hypothesis that multi-frequency processing
software in DR maintain optimal image quality at lower
mAs in pediatric-equivalent phantom examinations of the
femur. Diagnostic image quality was maintained even at a
dose reduction of 61% with MLT(S) optimized images and
with an entrance skin dose of 125 μGy. Even for images of
diagnostically acceptable quality MLT(S) supported a dose
reduction of 88% compared to the reference image and a
reduction in effective dose from 79 μSv to 4 μSv. Common
to all tests was the finding of dose as a positive significant
factor. It may thus be concluded that even if the influence on
image quality by the software was vast and the possibilities
many, dose always impacts the perceived image quality.
