General Description of Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector (model name: A1717MCW / A1417MCW / F1417MCW)

The A1717MCW/A1417MCW/F1417MCW are wired or wireless digital flat panel detectors that have been designed for faster, more streamlined approach to digital radiography systems. The detector consists of TFT and Scintillator (CsI) and an electronic device that converts data read through it into digital signals. The detectors support an auto exposure detection technology that allows the detectors to be used without generator integration.

The flat panel sensors of the A1717MCW/A1417MCW/F1417MCW are fabricated using thin film technology based on amorphous silicon technology. Electronically, the sensors are much like conventional photodiode arrays. Each pixel in the array consists of a light‐sensing photodiode and a switching Thin Film Transistor (TFT) in the same electronic circuit. The amorphous silicon photodiode is sensitive to visual light, and its response curve has almost the same performance as human vision. The sensitivity of amorphous silicon photodiodes peaks in green wavelengths, well matched to scintillators such as CsI. It also has excellent linearity according to the exposure dose.

Connection

Do not connect the detector with anything other than specified. Otherwise, it may result in fire or electric shock.

To avoid the risk of electric shock, this detector must only be connected to supply mains with protective earth.

Avoid installing the system in such way that it is difficult for the operator to disconnect the appliance plug.

Do not touch Signal Input Part/Signal Output Part and the patient simultaneously. There is a risk of electric shock from leakage current.

Additional equipment connected to medical electrical equipment must comply with the respective IEC or ISO standards. Furthermore, all configurations shall comply with the requirements for medical electrical systems. Anybody connecting additional equipment to medical electrical equipment configures a medical system and is therefore responsible that the system complies with the requirements for medical electrical systems. Attention is drawn to the fact that local laws take priority over the above-mentioned requirements.

Equipment connected to the detector and in the patient, environment must be powered from a medically-isolated power source or must be a medically-isolated device. Equipment powered from a non-isolated source can result in chassis leakage currents exceeding safe levels. Chassis leakage current created by an accessory or device connected to a non-isolated outlet may add to the chassis leakage current of the detector.

The x-ray systems and console software used with the product must be CE certified.

Always be sure to keep checking the condition of the system and the patient to ensure they are normal during the use of the detector. If any problem is found, take appropriate measures, such as stopping the operation of the detector, as required.

Also, since the detector incorporates parts that may cause electric shocks and other hazardous parts, touching them may cause death or serious injury.

Do not hit or drop the detector. The detector may be damaged if it receives a strong jolt, which may result in fire or electric shock if the detector is used without being repaired.

Precautions for use

Preparation

Be sure to connect the cables to the proper connectors. Otherwise, the detector may malfunction or may be damaged.

The battery should be equipped at a specified location. Otherwise, the detector may malfunction or be damaged.

Handling

Handle the detector carefully, as it may become damaged if it is hit, dropped, or receives a strong jolt.

Be sure to use the detector on a flat place so it will not bend. Otherwise, the detector may be damaged.

Be sure to check the detector daily and confirm that it works properly. Sudden heating of the room in cold areas will cause condensation to form on the detector. In this case, wait until condensation disappears before performing exposure. If the detector is used with condensation formed on it, problems may occur in the quality of the detector. When an air-conditioner is going to be used, be sure to raise/lower the temperature gradually so that a difference in temperature in the room and in the detector does not occur, to prevent forming of condensation. Follow the recommended proper room temp.

Do not use the detector near devices generating a strong magnetic field. Doing so may produce image noise or artifacts.

Keep the connectors free from being in contact with the patient.

Connectors are intended to be connected to an external device and must follow IEC standards.

Do not apply excessive weight to the detector. Otherwise, the detector may be damaged.

Use a Weight Distribution Cap when weight of a patient is concentrated on one particular point.

Principles of operation

The X-ray output from the X-ray generator passes through the object to be photographed and is then converted into a digital signal through the detector to be output to the image signal. X-rays weaken in dense tissue such as bones, and soft tissue uses the principle of easy passage to adjust the brightness of the image to express the signal.

The indirect type detector consists of a Scintillator for converting X-rays into visible light and a TFT panel for converting them into data. A TFT array consists of covered with a thin layer of silicon that is amorphous or disordered, and silicon is engraved with millions of transistors arranged in higher order like a grid of graph paper. Each of these thin-film transistors (TFTs) is attached to a light-absorbing photodiode that makes up individual pixels, and visible light entering the photodiode is converted by intensity into a digital signal and output to the screen in a readable digital image.

Clinical background, current knowledge, state of the art

Description of historical context and developments

X-ray radiography is the most common medical imaging procedure. In the last two decades, digital radiography has emerged as a viable technology for acquiring x-ray images. Digital radiography offers a number of new capabilities such as post-processing, electronic archival, concurrent access to the images, and improved image distribution. The first of such technologies to become commercially stable was commuted Radiography (CR). More recently, advances in thin-film transistor technology have led to the development of solid-state flat-panel digital radiographic systems. At comparable resolution, these devices offer detective quantum efficiencies (DQEs) Higher than CR and analog scree-film systems.

Currently, there are two general types of flat-panel imaging detectors, direct and indirect, named for the mechanism of x-ray detection they utilize. The indirect flat-panel detectors, which are more common, use a layer of phosphor material that converts x-rays to visible photons. There are also two general types of indirect digital radiographic systems based on the phosphor screens that they employ. In one type, a structured phosphor screen is used as the scintillator, commonly in the form of crystalline CsI Grown in perpen-dicular structures. In the other type, the phosphor screen is turbid in nature with phosphor particles suspended in a layer of binding material. These kinds of indirect detectors often use rare-earth phosphor screens of the type used in screen-film radiography (e.g., Gd2O2S). In either type of detector, the visible photons are subsequently captured by a layer of photodiode pixel elements, where the energy of the light photons is converted to electronic charge. The charges are stored in the capacitors associated with individual pixels. After exposure, the stored charges are read electronically line by line via the panel’s gate and amplifier lines, and the image data are sent to a computer for immediate processing, display, storage, and/or transmission.

The introduction of flat-panel detectors in 2000 led to the development of fully digitalized x-ray systems. Due to their better dose quantum efficiency (DQE), dose reduction could be achieved as compared with conventional film–screen combinations. Further advantages were the instant availability of images and the avoidance of transport of the cassettes to the reading station. Today, digital imaging technology is realized in the majority of radiological departments, creating a filmless workflow. Technical advances and price reductions of hardware and storage media are the reasons why digital imaging now does not suffer from any disadvantages compared with conventional film-based imaging.

The state-of-the-art is the Flat Panel Detector for Digital Radiography that is our device under evaluation. TFT-based flat-panel X-ray detector is capable of achieving low-dose, real-time X-ray imaging through coupling an energy transfer layer and large-area pixelated TFT arrays. According to the difference in the pathway of converting X-ray radiation to charge carriers, flat-panel X-ray detectors are categorized into indirect conversion systems and direct conversion systems. The Indirect Conversion X-Ray Detector Indirect conversion flat-panel X-ray detector is made of a layer of scintillator thin-film on the top for X-ray energy conversion, pixelated amorphous silicon (α-Si) photodiode arrays adjacent to scintillators, and a TFT array. When X-ray irradiates the flat-panel X-ray detectors, X-ray photons are converted into visible luminescence by scintillators and subsequently converted into electric charges by the α-Si photodiode arrays. Eventually, the electric charges are recorded by a TFT array. The most widely used scintillators are CsI: Tl with a thickness of 150-600 μm and terbium-doped GOS: Tb.

Alternative treatments

Our device is an Flat Panel Detector for Digital Radiography and there are alternative treatments like Screen-film(X-ray film) for Conventional Radiography (Screen-film Radiography, Film-screen Radiography) and Imaging Plate for Computed Radiography.

The conventional X-ray film consists of a flexible polyester base and a layer of photosensitive emulsion containing silver halide microcrystals. When exposed to X-rays or visible light, these crystals can capture energy and move electrons from the valence band to the conduction band. These electrons become trapped in areas with crystal structure distortion and attract positively charged silver ions, forming metallic silver atoms and creating a latent image. During development, the metallic silver content determines the image's darkness or optical density. The final image is revealed through a photochemical processing process.

Imaging Plate (IP) is a digital replacement for conventional X-ray film. It contains a layer of photostimulable phosphor (PSP) and is used in a cassette similar to traditional X-ray cassettes. X-ray radiation is absorbed by the phosphor layer and stored as energy in metastable regions called F-centers. This stored energy forms a latent image. To reveal the image, the IP is scanned by a laser beam in a reader. The laser scans the IP, and the emitted light, generated during photo stimulation, is collected by an optical fiber. This data is processed to create a diagnostic image. The scanning process involves moving the IP in one direction while the laser beam moves perpendicular to it, covering the entire plate surface.

Clinical benefit 1 – Reduction in patient dose and high quality image

The Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector (digital radiography) provides a lower patient dose and higher quality images than imaging plate (computed radiography) and X-ray film (conventional radiography).

[Literature #6] The study found substantial dose reduction capabilities with the new digital radiography systems compared to conventional screen-film radiography. A previous dosimetry study cited in the article showed dose reductions of approximately 35% with computed radiography (CR) and 60% with flat-panel detector (FP) systems relative to screen-film. This implies the CR system operated at around 65% of the screen-film dose, while the FP system operated at around 40%. Figure 3 clearly demonstrated the FP system provided superior image quality compared to CR and screen-film at comparable exposures. This shows the FP system enables large dose reductions versus screen-film without compromising image quality. In summary, the FP system provided the lowest patient dose at around 40% of the screen-film dose, followed by the CR system at around 65% of the screen-film dose. The FP system's superior image quality at low dose facilitated these substantial dose reductions relative to the other systems.

[Literature #8] The study demonstrated improved image quality with the new DRX-1 flat panel digital radiography system compared to the previous computed radiography (CR) system. Contrast-detail analysis of phantom images showed better contrast detectability and higher IQFinv values with DRX-1. Lower dose DRX-1 phantom images (3 μGy) had comparable quality to higher dose CR images (10 μGy). Clinical image quality rated by radiologists improved from an average of 3 with CR to 4.5 with DRX-1. Automated IQFinv analysis of clinical images also showed superior quality with DRX-1 versus CR. In summary, both objective phantom and subjective clinical image assessments found quantitatively and qualitatively better image quality with the DRX-1 flat panel system compared to the previous CR system.

[Literature #9] The study optimized and compared radiation doses for chest x-ray exams using computed radiography (CR) and digital radiography (DR) systems versus traditional film-screen systems. Initial CR doses were 5 times higher than film-screen for chest PA exams, while initial DR doses were lower. Through an optimization process of adjusting algorithms and dose levels, final CR chest doses were reduced to 0.24 mGy, similar to the 0.2 mGy film-screen dose. DR chest doses remained lower than the reference film-screen dose at 0.14 mGy. In terms of image quality, radiologists preferred the lower-dose optimized CR chest images 60% of the time. Increasing the DR chest dose improved image quality by reducing noise.

Overall, the study demonstrated that both CR and DR allow chest radiation doses comparable to or lower than film-screen, while maintaining or improving image quality for diagnostic purposes through dose optimization.

[Literature #12] The study demonstrated an approximate 50.1% reduction in radiation dose with the digital flat-panel detector system compared to the conventional film-screen system, as measured by dose measurements in an anthropomorphic chest phantom. Despite this significant dose reduction, the overall image quality and visibility of anatomic structures were rated as superior with the digital system by the observer evaluation. Specifically, chest radiographs obtained with the digital system were scored significantly higher (p ≤ 0.0003) in terms of overall image quality compared to film-screen radiography. Additionally, the visibility of anatomical structures was rated significantly better (p ≤ 0.0003) on the digital images by the observers, with the exception of basal peripheral lung vessels on PA view which showed no significant difference between the two systems. The largest differences were seen for mediastinal structures on PA view and retrocardiac vessels on lateral projections. Furthermore, Spearman's rank correlation analysis indicated good interobserver agreement, suggesting reliable and objective image quality evaluation. In summary, with approximately 50% lower dose, the digital flat-panel detector system provided chest radiographs with improved overall image quality and visibility of anatomical structures compared to conventional film-screen radiography.

[Literature #14] The study found that the flat panel (FP) system had a slightly lower modulation transfer function (MTF) compared to the computed radiography (CR) system, but the MTF was still comparable between the two systems. Importantly, the noise power spectrum (NPS) of the FP system was generally lower than the CR system, and decreased at a faster rate with higher spatial frequencies. When integrating these metrics, the detective quantum efficiency (DQE) of the FP system was significantly higher, by at least 2 times, than the CR system at clinically relevant exposure levels under 1 mR. Additionally, the DQE of the FP system increased with greater exposure, while the DQE of the CR system decreased slightly. Overall, in terms of overall image quality as quantified by DQE, the study demonstrated the FP system performed markedly better than the CR system, especially at the higher exposure levels typical for chest radiography exams. The lower noise characteristics of the FP system offset its marginally lower MTF, resulting in superior DQE and overall image quality.

[Literature #15] The study found the new digital radiography system with a flat panel detector demonstrated superior image quality metrics compared to conventional screen-film systems. Measurements showed the detector had a detective quantum efficiency around twice as high as screen-film, indicating far greater efficiency in converting x-rays to an image. The detector also matched or exceeded the modulation transfer function of screen-film up to 2 lp/mm, meaning its ability to resolve spatial frequencies and contrast was equivalent or better. Additionally, the flat panel detector provided a substantially wider dynamic range versus screen-film, making it less susceptible to over- or underexposure. Initial clinical images were evaluated by radiologists as having comparable or superior diagnostic quality to screen-film images. In summary, quantitative measurements of key parameters like DQE, MTF and dynamic range showed the new flat panel digital system offers considerable advantages in image quality over conventional screen-film systems.

Clinical benefit 2 - Fast image acquisition time

The Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector (digital radiography) provides a faster image acquisition time than imaging plate (computed radiography) and X-ray film (conventional radiography).

[Literature 8] The study found a significant reduction in image acquisition time after transitioning from a computed radiography (CR) system to a new digital radiography (DR) system with flat panel detectors (DRX-1). Time measurements showed the average image acquisition time per exposure decreased by 25 seconds with DRX-1 compared to CR. This time savings was attributed to the streamlined workflow enabled by the permanently mounted flat panel detectors in each room with DRX-1, eliminating transport of CR cassettes. The faster image acquisition of DRX-1 improved radiology department efficiency and throughput. Exam room occupancy times were reduced. In summary, the switch to DRX-1 resulted in a statistically significant average decrease of 25 seconds in image acquisition time per image versus CR due to superior workflow. This demonstrated that DR systems with fixed flat panel detectors can provide meaningful gains in exam efficiency.

[Literature 10] The study found a statistically significant reduction in the average time required per image after transitioning from screen-film and computed radiography (CR) systems to a new digital radiography (DR) system. For the pre-conversion group using screen-film and CR, the average time per image was 3.27 minutes (SD 1.50 minutes). For the post-conversion group using the new DR system, the average time decreased to 2.51 minutes (SD 1.16 minutes) per image. Statistical analysis showed this difference was highly significant (p=0.000822). The switch to digital radiography resulted in a time savings of 0.76 minutes (23%) per image on average compared to the prior mixed screen-film and CR systems. In summary, adopting the new DR system led to a statistically significant reduction in the average image acquisition time versus conventional screen-film radiography, improving workflow efficiency.

[Literature 11] This study found that using digital radiography (DR) for intraoperative localization during cervical spine surgery significantly reduced the time required compared to conventional screen-film radiography. The mean localization time with DR was 100 seconds versus 823 seconds with conventional radiography, an over 80% reduction. This large difference of 723 seconds (12 minutes 3 seconds) per case was statistically significant (p<0.001). The rapid image acquisition and display capabilities of DR streamlined the intraoperative localization process. Based on these results, the authors concluded that DR provides a faster, more efficient method for obtaining critical imaging data during cervical spine surgery compared to screen-film radiography. The considerable time savings per case with DR improves surgical workflow and has the potential to enhance patient safety by reducing anesthesia exposure.

[Literature 13] This time-motion analysis found a substantial reduction in image acquisition and availability times with digital radiography (DR) compared to conventional radiography. For the DR group, the total time from initial image capture to availability for clinicians was 432 minutes, with an average of 14.4 minutes per case. For the conventional radiography group, the total time was 1436 minutes, averaging 47.9 minutes per case. Therefore, the time for images to become available was 70% less with DR versus conventional methods. The range of times was 4-49 minutes for DR cases versus 18-85 minutes for conventional cases. Repeat images were required in only 1 DR case compared to 7 conventional cases due to unsatisfactory initial images. In summary, results showed DR reduced the time for images to become available to clinicians by approximately 70% compared to conventional radiography. This considerable time savings demonstrates a clear workflow efficiency advantage of digital radiography resulting from more rapid image acquisition and display.

Indication

Musculoskeletal indication

The Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector (digital radiography) provides musculoskeletal X-ray images.

[Literature #3] The study examined skeletal surveys from 930 children under 2 years old who were suspected victims of abuse. Skeletal surveys involve taking X-ray images of the entire skeleton to check for fractures and other injuries. The surveys included 31 images of the skull, spine, ribs, pelvis, shoulders, arms, legs, hands and feet. Fractures were detected in 34% of the children, with 18% having multiple fractures. The vast majority of fractures (over 90%) were located in the long bones, ribs, skull and clavicles. Fractures of the spine, pelvis, hands and feet were very rare, occurring in less than 1% of cases. Overall, the skeletal surveys revealed a high rate of fractures, predominantly affecting the long bones, ribs, skull and clavicles, in young children suspected of being abuse victims.

[Literature #11] The study examined localization radiographs from 18 patients undergoing anterior cervical spine surgery. Prior to surgery, the cervical spine level(s) targeted for operation were exposed. A spinal needle was then inserted directly into the intervertebral disc space between the vertebral bodies requiring localization. With the needle in place within the disc space, a lateral cervical radiograph was obtained. This allowed the exact vertebral level and disc space where surgery was to be performed to be identified on the image. By placing the spinal needle directly inside the disc space of the exposed surgical level and obtaining a radiograph, the study was able to accurately localize the cervical spine level and intervertebral disc space that needed to be operated on. The localization radiographs focused specifically on imaging the disc space with the needle in place as the reference point.

Chest indication

The Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector (digital radiography) provides chest X-ray images.

[Literature #2] The study examined chest radiographs from 115 patients who underwent imaging using both conventional and digital radiography systems. Each patient had posteroanterior (PA) and lateral chest x-rays taken within 24 hours using the two techniques. On the PA views, 11 anatomical regions were evaluated - trachea, carina, main bronchi, costopleural border, azygoesophageal recess, paraspinal stripe, peripheral pulmonary vasculature, hilum, mediastinum, soft tissue, and bones. For the lateral views, 9 regions were assessed - trachea, costodiaphragmatic recess, posterior cardiac border, retrosternal pulmonary structure, retrocardiac pulmonary structure, fissures, hilum, thoracic spine, and sternum.

[Literature #9] The study recruited approximately 30 subjects in the weight range of 70 ± 10 kg who were referred for routine chest, abdomen, and pelvis x-ray exams at the hospital. It compared radiation doses and image quality across three imaging modalities - screen-film radiography, computed radiography (CR), and digital radiography (DR) - for four common projections: chest PA (posterior-anterior), chest LAT (lateral), abdomen AP (anterior-posterior), and pelvis AP. By selecting subjects solely based on a weight criteria and evaluating standard projections used in chest, abdomen and pelvis exams, the study aimed to optimize radiation doses and image quality for these routine radiographic procedures.

[Literature #12] The study examined standard posteroanterior (PA) and lateral chest radiographs from 100 asymptomatic outpatients. The PA chest x-rays were evaluated for image quality and visibility of anatomical structures including the right and left main bronchi, peripheral bronchi, lung vasculature (apical, basal, peripheral), right pulmonary artery, descending aorta, paravertebral diaphragm, and pedicles of upper thoracic vertebrae. The lateral chest x-rays were assessed for structures including retrocardiac lung vessels, hilum, lung vessels within the heart shadow, diaphragm at the heart shadow, and upper third of the thoracic spine.

[Literature #13] A study examined the workflow for acquiring standard chest x-rays, including posteroanterior and lateral views, in the emergency department setting. The study tracked time intervals for key steps in the chest x-ray workflow in 60 emergency department patients referred for chest x-rays, with 30 undergoing digital radiography and 30 undergoing conventional film-screen radiography.
