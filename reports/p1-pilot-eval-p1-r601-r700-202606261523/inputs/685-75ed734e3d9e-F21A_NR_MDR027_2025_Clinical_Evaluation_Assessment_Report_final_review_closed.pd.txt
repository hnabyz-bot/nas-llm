Contraindication
- Pregnancy: The device is contraindicated for use in the abdominal and pelvic regions of women who are pregnant or may be pregnant. X-ray exposure to the fetus
carries a risk of severe congenital defects and developmental disorders.
- Bariatric Patients: The device is strictly contraindicated for any examination other than the extremities (i.e., limb imaging) in bariatric patients (defined as BMI 40
kg/m^2). Due to the limited X-ray output capacity for thicker body parts, examinations of the trunk, abdomen, and pelvis may yield poor diagnostic quality and result
in excessive radiation dose without sufficient clinical benefit.
- Mammography: This device is not to be used for mammography.

Commentary: The portable X-ray device is designed for diagnostic imaging in adult patients while maintaining compliance with established medical device safety
standards and radiation protection principles. As the device emits ionizing radiation during normal operation, its safety profile focuses on minimizing exposure and
ensuring that radiation risks remain well below clinically significant thresholds. Deterministic radiation effects such as acute tissue injury are associated with high
radiation doses, typically around 2 Gy; however, the maximum entrance skin dose produced by this device is approximately 1.13 mGy, which is more than 1,700
times lower than the threshold at which such effects may occur. Consequently, short-term deterministic injuries such as radiation-induced skin erythema are not
expected during normal diagnostic use. Neither is cataract formation, which has a cumulative deterministic threshold of roughly 0.5 Gy. It is safe to say that it
cannot occur from a single examination with this system, as the radiation dose delivered per examination remains below 1 mGy and therefore more than 400 times
below the threshold. The primary residual risk associated with diagnostic X-ray exposure is stochastic in nature, particularly a small increase in lifetime cancer risk.
According to internationally recognized radiological protection models, diagnostic examinations delivering approximately 0.4-1.1 mSv per exposure correspond to
an estimated individual attributable cancer risk of approximately 0.002–0.006% per examination. This level of risk is considered clinically acceptable when justified
by the diagnostic benefit, consistent with the justification and optimization principles described by the International Commission on Radiological Protection.
