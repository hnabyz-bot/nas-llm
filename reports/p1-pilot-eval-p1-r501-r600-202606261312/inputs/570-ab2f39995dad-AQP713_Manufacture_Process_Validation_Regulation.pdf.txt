1. Purpose
The purpose of this regulation is to establish methods and procedures to
ensure that processes, facilities, or equipment function properly as intended
and to ensure that consistent products are produced.
2. Applied Area
This regulation applies to the methods and procedures for process
validation.
3. Terms and Definitions
No. Terms Definition
1 Special process
Processes whose results cannot be sufficiently verified by
subsequent product inspection and testing, such as when
process defects appear only after the product is used
2 Process Validation
The activity of demonstrating with objective evidence that a
process consistently produces products or results that meet
predetermined requirements
3 Protocol
A document specifying the validation method, including test
parameters, product characteristics, manufacturing
equipment, and criteria for producing satisfactory test results
4 Validation
This refers to the activity of verifying, through objective
evidence, that a specific operation, process, machinery, raw
materials, operation, or system meets the requirements for
its intended use or application. In other words, for processes
that cannot be verified through testing alone, validation is
required prior to process implementation and, where
applicable, prior to product design changes, product
additions, or process modifications. The process must be
strictly managed and monitored.
5 Verification The act of satisfying specific requirements through testing
and objective evidence

5. Work procedures
5.1 General
5.1.1 The manufacturing team determines whether there are any in-house process
validation targets and, if applicable, establishes a periodic validation plan
based on the characteristics of the equipment and facilities.
5.1.2 A protocol for three stages of qualification—IQ, OQ, and PQ—must be
developed and based on the following process or product requirements. For
equipment that does not require Performance Qualification (PQ), Installation
Qualification (IQ) and Operational Qualification (OQ) can be omitted. The
validity of this should be determined within the team and documented in the
report.
1) Decide what to verify/measure
2) Decide how to verify/measure
3) Decide how much to test/measure (statistical significance)
4) Decide when to verify/measure
5) Definition of pass/fail criteria
6) Definition of required documents

5.2 IQ, Installation Qualification
5.2.1 IQ is to verify and record on-site whether the equipment and devices have
been manufactured according to the design specifications and properly
installed to perform the intended function by comparing the actual product
with the specifications.
5.2.2 Before conducting IQ, a separate design qualification (DQ) step can be
established to confirm whether the equipment and devices were designed
with scientific basis and validity.
5.2.3 Things to consider at the IQ level are.
1) Equipment design features (performance specifications, lubrication cycles,
cleaning requirements, etc.)
2) Installation conditions (wiring, utilities, functionality, etc.)
3) Calibration cycles, preventive maintenance, cleaning cycles
4) Safety features
5) Equipment manufacturing drawings, maintenance, and operation manuals
6) Software documentation
7) Spare parts list
8) Environmental conditions (cleanroom requirements, temperature,
humidity, vibration, etc.)
5.3 OQ, Operational qualification
5.3.1 Verification of proper operation of equipment within design specifications. IQ
is a verification under stationary conditions, while OQ is an actual verification
under operating conditions.
5.3.2 The OQ stage verifies the limits of operating variables through limit testing,
etc., to ensure that process parameters produce products that meet all
specified requirements under all predictable manufacturing conditions.
Therefore, the OQ stage simulates actual production conditions, including
worst-case conditions that take into account the following.
1) Process control limits (time, temperature, pressure, line speed, setting
conditions, etc.)
2) Software parameters
3) Raw material specifications
4) Fair operation procedures
5) Material handling requirements
6) Process change management
7) Training
8) Short-term stability and capability of the process (scope study or control
chart)

5.3.3 At this stage, statistically validated techniques such as screening

5.3.4 For processes and equipment that do not require performance qualification,

5.4 PQ, Performance qualification
5.4.1 This step comprehensively evaluates the manufacturing process capability by
verifying whether the manufacturing method, manufacturing equipment, etc.
can consistently produce products of the specified quality within the range of
process conditions using equipment that has completed the OQ stage.

5.4.2 Things to consider at the PQ stage are as follows.
1) Actual product, process parameters, and procedures established in OQ
2) Product acceptability
3) Process capability assurance as established in OQ
4) Process reproducibility and long-term process stability
5.4.3 Performance qualification assessments should be conducted under realistic,
routine manufacturing conditions. Furthermore, they should include upper
and lower limits for each operating variable, based on the documented
standard operating procedures established during the OQ phase. Therefore,
PQ should be repeated sufficiently to ensure meaningful and consistent
results.
5.5 Management of special processes and automated processes
5.5.1 Any (automated) process involving special processes or computer software
must be validated prior to implementation. The validation manager must
develop a validation protocol containing the following details and obtain
approval from the manufacturing team leader.
1) Project Name
2) Purpose and Expected Results
3) Organization – Relevant Department, Personnel
4) Personnel – Competency Assessment, Qualification
5) Validation Procedure
6) List of Required Procedures and Specifications
7) List of Equipment – Layout, Operation Methods
8) List of Measuring Instruments – Layout, Calibration Plan, Calibration
Records
9) Record Format, Classification, Numbering Method
10) Process Overview
11) Specifications for Products and Raw Materials
12) Test Method Overview – Sampling Method, Inspection Plan, Accept/Fail
Criteria, Analysis Method, Evaluation Method
13) Schedule – Single/Multiple Tests, Decision Date, Report Date
5.5.2 Report Writing

the results and records of various implementations and must be approved

① Conclusion
② Attachment of external data and reports
③ Photos, references, and pages
④ Evaluation

5.5.3 Process Approval and Revalidation
1) The process must be implemented after the manufacturing team leader
approves the protocol and report.
2) The revalidation protocol and report must be prepared, reviewed, and
approved by the same organization or personnel that initially performed
the revalidation.
3) Revalidation must be performed in the following cases.
① Changes in process variables (materials, equipment, personnel, work
methods, environment)
② Changes in product performance or effectiveness
③ When validation results exceed specified limits
④ Regularly, at least once a year
5.5.4 Recordkeeping
1) Workers and manufacturing managers must continuously monitor and
control process variables, record work logs, workers, applied documents,
major equipment used, and monitoring data, and manage them in
accordance with the Standard Document and Record Management
Regulations (AQP404).
2) Manufacturing team managers must retain protocols and reports for at
least 10 years.
