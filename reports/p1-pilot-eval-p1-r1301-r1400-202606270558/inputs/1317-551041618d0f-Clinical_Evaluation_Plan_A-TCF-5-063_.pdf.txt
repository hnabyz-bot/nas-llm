2.1 Introduction
Clinical evaluation is regarded as an ongoing process conducted throughout the life cycle of a medical
device. It is first performed during the conformity assessment process leading to the marketing of a
medical device and then repeated periodically as new clinical safety and performance information
about the device is obtained during its use in the post market phase.
This clinical evaluation includes the overall results of literature review, extensive clinical data for
substantially equivalent predicate device and post market surveillance data in accordance with
MEDDEV 2.7.1 Rev.4. It is intended to evaluate the clinical benefits of the ADD in comparison to its
potential risks. Therefore, this clinical evaluation is part on the risk management process according to
EN ISO 14971:2012.

2.4 Description of the device
2.4.1 Overview
The ADD is wired or wireless digital flat panel detector that have been designed for faster, more
streamlined approach to digital radiography systems. The ADD detector utilize a combination of
propriety TFT glass and scintillators (CsI), and those and electronics are housed in one package. The
detectors support an auto-trigger signal sensing technology that allows the detectors to be used
without generator integration.
The flat panel sensors of the ADD are fabricated using thin film technology based on amorphous silicon
technology. Electronically, the sensors are much like conventional photodiode arrays. Each pixel in the
array consists of a light‐sensing photodiode and a switching Thin Film Transistor (TFT) in the same
electronic circuit. Amorphous silicon photodiodes are sensitive to visible light, with a response curve
roughly comparable to human vision. The sensitivity of amorphous silicon photodiodes peaks in green
wavelengths, well matched to scintillators such as CsI. The response has the excellent linearity of a
charge‐integrating-biased photodiode.

3 Clinical background current knowledge, state of the art
3.1 Description of historical context and developments
X-ray radiography is the most common medical imaging procedure. In the last two decades, digital
radiography has emerged as a viable technology for acquiring x-ray images. Digital radiography offers a
number of new capabilities such as post-processing, electronic archival, concurrent access to the images,
and improved image distribution. The first of such technologies to become commercially stable was
commuted Radiography (CR). More recently, advances in thin-film transistor technology have led to the
development of solid-state flat-panel digital radiographic systems. At comparable resolution, these devices
offer detective quantum efficiencies (DQEs) Higher than CR and analog scree-film systems.
Currently, there are two general types of flat-panel imaging detectors, direct and indirect, named for the
mechanism of x-ray detection they utilize. The indirect flat-panel detectors, which are more common, use a
layer of phosphor material that converts x-rays to visible photons. There are also two general types of
indirect digital radiographic systems based on the phosphor screens that they employ. In one type, a
structured phosphor screen is used as the scintillator, commonly in the form of crystalline CsI Grown in
perpen-dicular structures. In the other type, the phosphor screen is turbid in nature with phosphor particles
suspended in a layer of binding material. These kinds of indirect detectors often use rare-earth phosphor
screens of the type used in screen-film radiography (e.g., Gd2O2S). In either type of detector, the visible
photons are subsequently captured by a layer of photodiode pixel elements, where the energy of the light
photons is converted to electronic charge. The charges are stored in the capacitors associated with individual
pixels. After exposure, the stored charges are read electronically line by line via the panel’s gate and
amplifier lines, and the image data are sent to a computer for immediate processing, display, storage, and/or
transmission.
The introduction of flat-panel detectors in 2000 led to the development of fully digitalized x-ray systems. Due
to their better dose quantum efficiency (DQE), dose reduction could be achieved as compared with
conventional film–screen combinations. Further advantages were the instant availability of images and the
avoidance of transport of the cassettes to the reading station. Today, digital imaging technology is realized in
the majority of radiological departments, creating a filmless workflow. Technical advances and price
reductions of hardware and storage media are the reasons why digital imaging now does not suffer from any
disadvantages compared with conventional film-based imaging.
These proposed devices are the detectors with indirect energy conversion.
