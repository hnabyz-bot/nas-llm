1.2 Objective
This protocol applies to identify, select and collate relevant publication literature for the Digital Flat
Panel X-ray Detector to research safety and performance of the product.

1.3.2 Justification of the sources
We use three scientific literature databases.
• PubMed provides more than 36 million citations for biomedical literature from MEDLINE, life
science journals, and online books. Citations may include links to full-text content from PubMed
Central and publisher web sites.
• European Radiology is the Journal of the European Society of Radiology (ESR) and the official
journal of the following organisations. It continuously updates scientific knowledge in radiology
by publication of strong original articles and state-of-the-art reviews written by leading
radiologists.
• Medical Physics is a journal of global scope and reach. It encourages submissions describing
novel, cutting edge physics and engineering developments in medical imaging, ionizing radiation
therapy, and other image-guided therapeutic interventions.
Also, we use the label and Instruction for Use of the equivalent or benchmark devices.
We searched the information on the internet such IFUs, the summary in registered FDA 510(k) in FDA
website, brochure, EC Declaration of conformity and certificates of equivalent devices.

1.3.6 Literature selection criteria
The literatures were analyzed considering the inclusion and exclusion criteria. At first, the application
of selection criteria was limited to the literatures’ metadata (title, abstract, and keywords). The metadata
of each literature were read by the searcher(s), who also considered the application of the inclusion and
exclusion criteria. In addition, the selection criteria had regard to the content of the introduction and
conclusion of the literatures.
(1) Inclusion criteria
The literatures addressing any of the topics listed below were included:
- The literature is generated from the actual use of the equivalent devices (DRX-1, CXDI-31,
CXDI-70C, and etc.).
- The literature is related to Digital Flat Panel X-ray detector.
- The literature is related to the safety of the Digital Flat Panel X-ray detector.
- The literature is related to the performance of the Digital Flat Panel X-ray detector
(Resolution, Image Acquisition Time and etc.).
- The literature addresses the performance of the equivalent devices.
- The literature addresses the safety of the equivalent devices.
- The subject is for patient who needs to use diagnostic imaging using X-ray.
(2) Exclusion Criteria
The literatures addressing any of the exclusion criteria listed below were excluded:
- The literature is not related to Digital Flat Panel X-ray detector.
- The literature does not include a discussion of safety or performance of X-ray detector.
- There is a newer or a more complete study about the same research.
- The literature is no English, not full text, duplicate with other literatures, excluding research
period, not focus on Digital Flat Panel X-ray detector.

1.3.7.2 Criteria for selection
Literature shall be appraised according to the criteria below. (IMDRF MDCE WG (PD1)/N55
(formerly GHTF/SG5/N1R8:2007), Proposed document by International Medical Device
Regulators Forum, Clinical evaluation).
For the literature evaluation, the criteria are divided into suitability and data contribution. The
suitability criteria are evaluated by 4 categories in device, device application, patient group, and
report/data collection. As well, the criteria of data contribution are evaluated by 5 categories in data
source type, outcome measures, follow up, statistical significance, clinical significance.
For the literature, the weighting is assigned by grading. So, the category is as follows;
i. 17~22 point: Sufficient for scientific Literature databases
ii. 13~16 point: Not enough for scientific Literature databases, but available for clinical

Title Optimisation of direct digital chest radiography using Cu filtration
Authors/Journal Ernest U. Ekpo, Alishja C. Hoban, Mark F. McEntee
Abstract
Purpose:
To assess the impact of additional Cu filtration with the DRX-1 detector in optimisation
of adult chest radiography.
Methods:
A simulated anatomical phantom was imaged at different levels of Cu filtration (0 mm,
0.1 mm, 0.3 mm and 0.5 mm of Cu) at 120 kVp. Image quality was assessed using
exposure index, conspicuity index, signal-to-noise ratio and pixel density. Dose area
product (DAP) meter was used to measure dose; effective dose and risk of exposure
induced cancer death (REID) were estimated using the PCXMC software 2.0.
Results:
Exposure index increased with increasing Cu thickness; 0.3 mm Cu filtration increased
conspicuity index of simulated nodules, with a minimal decrease in signal-to-noise ratio,
and pronounced decrease in pixel density. Patient doses were reduced by 20.7%, 36.6%,
and 39.79% at 0.1 mm, 0.3 mm, and 0.5 mm levels of Cu filtration respectively.
Conclusion:
Additional beam filtration using 0.3 mm Cu filter with the DRX-1 detector reduced patient
doses by 37% without decline in image quality.
<Analysis of the literature>
Performance Aspect
Ernest et al. has conducted this study to assess the impact of additional Cu filtration with the DRX-1 detector
in optimization of adult chest radiography. The CARESTREAM DRX-1 system offers an easy conversion from
conventional to digital radiography. With the wide dynamic range and image post-processing improvement in
image quality of digital systems, the major concern in optimising the imaging procedure with the DRX-1
system is the radiation dose to the patient. This study has demonstrated that the additional beam filtration using
0.3 mm Cu filter with the DRX-1 detector reduced patient doses by 37 % without decline in image quality.
DRX-1 system has shown a 37% reduction in dose and 5.8% lower effective dose compared to the ICRP 103
standard without decline in image quality.
Through this study, the performance of DRX-1 has been proven, and the DRX-1 is a CE-licensed medical
device. There is no significant difference between the legally marketed device, DRX-1 detector and the
proposed devices, Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector, and it is considered that the proposed devices also are not
influenced by the 0.3 mm Cu filter for the image quality.

11anatomic regions in the PA, and 9 in the lateral views. Statistical analysis was

Title The Prevalence of Uncommon Fractures on Skeletal Surveys Performed to Evaluate for

Authors/Journal Boaz Karmazyn, Max E. Lewis, S. Gregory Jennings, Roberta A. Hibbard, Ralph A. Hicks
Abstract
Objective:
The objective of our study was to evaluate the prevalence and site of fractures detected on
skeletal surveys performed for suspected child abuse at a tertiary children’s hospital and
to determine whether any survey images may be eliminated without affecting clinical care
or the ability to make a diagnosis.
Materials and methods:
We identified all skeletal surveys performed for suspected abuse from 2003 to 2009 of
children younger than 2 years. Repeated studies were excluded, as were studies not
performed to evaluate for suspected abuse. From the reports, we documented the sites of

Authors/Journal Christian Fink, Peter J Hallscheidt, Gerd Noeldge, Annette Kampschulte, Boris Radeleff,
Waldemar P Hosch, Günter W Kauffmann, Jochen Hansmann
Abstract
Objective:
The objective of this study was to compare clinical chest radiographs of a large-area, flat-
panel digital radiography system and a conventional film-screen radiography system. The
comparison was based on an observer preference study of image quality and visibility of
anatomic structures.
Materials and methods:
Routine follow-up chest radiographs were obtained from 100 consecutive oncology
patients using a large-area, amorphous silicon flat-panel detector digital radiography
system (dose equivalent to a 400-speed film system). Hard-copy images were compared
with previous examinations of the same individuals taken on a conventional film-screen
system (200-speed). Patients were excluded if changes in the chest anatomy were detected
or if the time interval between the examinations exceeded 1 year. Observer preference was
evaluated for the image quality and the visibility of 15 anatomic structures using a five-
point scale.
Results:
Dose measurements with a chest phantom showed a dose reduction of approximately 50%
with the digital radiography system compared with the film-screen radiography system.
The image quality and the visibility of all but one anatomic structure of the images
obtained with the digital flat-panel detector system were rated significantly superior (p <
or = 0.0003) to those obtained with the conventional film-screen radiography system.
Conclusion:
The image quality and visibility of anatomic structures on the images obtained by the flat-
panel detector system were perceived as equal or superior to the images from conventional
film-screen chest radiography. This was true even though the radiation dose was reduced
approximately 50% with the digital flat-panel detector system.
<Analysis of the literature>
Performance Aspect
The study was to compare the image quality and visibility of anatomical structures on chest radiographs

obtained from a large-area, flat-panel digital radiography system versus a conventional film-screen radiography
system. To compare the visibility of defined anatomical structures (lung, soft tissue, bone structures) on chest
radiographs from the two systems. The comparison was based on an observer preference study, where
radiologists evaluated and scored the image quality and visibility of structures for each modality. The image
quality scores for images obtained from the digital radiography system were significantly higher than those
from the conventional film-screen system according to statistical analysis. The visibility of most anatomical
structures was rated higher for images from the digital system. The biggest differences between the two systems
were seen for mediastinal structures and structures with high contrast differences, which are medically
important.

Abstract
The time differences between digital and conventional radiography for emergency

an opinion that digital radiography offered significant image modification advantag

Title Wireless digital radiography detectors in the emergency area: An efficacious solution
Authors/Journal Xinming Liu, Chris C Shaw
Abstract
Amorphous silicon (a-Si:H) flat-panel (FP) imaging systems have recently become
commercially available for both chest and mammographic imaging applications. It has
been shown that this new detector technology offers better image quality and various
operational advantages over the computed radiography (CR) which to date has been the
most widely implemented and used digital radiography technique. However, most image
quality measurements reported on flat-panel systems have been performed on prototype
systems in laboratories while those for CR systems were typically independently
performed and reported on in separate studies. To directly compare the two technologies,
we have measured the image properties for a commercial amorphous silicon/cesium iodide
[a-Si:H/CsI(Tl)] flat-panel based digital chest system and a commercial CR system under
clinical imaging conditions. In this paper, measurements of image quality metrics,
including the modulation transfer functions (MTFs), noise power spectra (NPSs), and
detective quantum efficiencies (DQEs), for the FP and CR systems are presented and
compared. Methods and issues related to these measurements are discussed. The results
show that the flat-panel system has slightly lower MTF but significantly higher DQEs than
the CR system. The DQEs of the flat-panel system were found to increase with the
exposure while those of the CR system decrease slightly with the exposure.
<Analysis of the literature>
Performance Aspect
This study is to directly compare the image quality of a commercial flat-panel detector system and a commercial
computed radiography (CR) system. To measure and compare image quality metrics like MTF, NPS, DQE of
the FP and CR systems using the same methodology and under nearly identical exposure conditions. To
perform the measurements on commercial FP and CR systems installed in clinical environments, not prototype
systems in laboratories. The key results of this study are: MTF measurement: The FP system had slightly lower
MTFs than the CR system, but the differences were small (<12% at 1 cycle/mm). The MTFs of the FP system
remained similar at 70 and 120 kVp, while the MTFs of the CR system were affected more by kVp. NPS
measurement: The NPSs of the FP system were generally lower than the CR system and decreased faster with
spatial frequency. NPSs of both systems decreased with exposure, but more so for the FP system. DQE
measurement: The DQEs of the FP system were significantly higher (at least 2x) than the CR system within
the clinical exposure range. The difference was even greater at higher exposures. DQEs of the FP system
increased with exposure, while those of the CR system decreased slightly with exposure.

Title Digital radiography with a large-area, amorphous-silicon, flat-panel X-ray detector system
Authors/Journal M Spahn, M Strotzer, M Völk, S Böhm, B Geiger, G Hahm, S Feuerbach
Abstract
Rationale and objectives:
To investigate the image quality of a digital radiography system with an amorphous-
silicon, large-area, digital flat-panel detector.
Methods:
A flat-panel detector based on a matrix of amorphous silicon was integrated into a
projection radiography system. The scintillator consisted of a layer of structured cesium
iodide. The active matrix size of 30002 pixels together with a pixel size of 143 microm
provided a large image area of 43 x 43 cm2. Basic image quality parameters such as
detective quantum efficiency (DQE) and modulation transfer function (MTF) were
measured and compared with those obtained with conventional systems.
Results:
The measurement of DQE yielded a high value of 70% at zero spatial frequency. At a
system dose equivalent to 400 speed, the DQE of the digital system was a factor of two
larger than the DQE of a storage phosphor or screen-film system within the entire spatial
frequency range between zero and the Nyquist limit of 3.5 line pairs per millimeter. The
flat-panel detector furthermore has an MTF that is superior to that in regular screen-film
systems and also provides a substantially larger dynamic range.
Conclusions:
This new technology demonstrates its potential to provide equal or superior image quality
to conventional screen-film systems and to reduce patient exposure to radiation dose. The
advantages of digital radiography systems, based on a flat-panel detector as an instant
image display, facilitation of work flow in the radiology department, and digital
networking and archiving, are well in sight.
<Analysis of the literature>
Performance Aspect

efficiency (DQE) and modulation transfer function (MTF) of the new system. To compare the image quality
of the new system with conventional screen-film systems and storage phosphor systems. To measure and assess
the dynamic range of the new system. To evaluate the diagnostic image quality through initial clinical images.
The DQE measurements showed that the new system's DQE was about 2 times higher than conventional screen-
film systems and storage phosphor systems. The new system's MTF was similar to or higher than conventional
screen-film systems up to 2 lp/mm. The dynamic range was significantly wider than conventional screen-film
systems. The initial clinical images were evaluated to have high diagnostic value.

Title Productivity and Cost Assessment of Computed Radiography, Digital Radiography, and

Authors/Journal Katherine P. Andriole
Abstract
An objective assessment and comparison of computed radiography (CR) versus di
gital radiography (DR) and screen-film for performing upright chest examinations
on outpatients is presented in terms of workflow, productivity, speed of service, a
nd potential cost justification. Perceived ease of use and workflow of each device
is collected via a technologist opinion survey. Productivity is measured as the ra
te of patient throughput from normalized timing studies. The overall speed of ser
vice is calculated from the time of examination ordering as stamped in the radiol
ogy information system (RIS), to the time of image availability on the picture arc
hiving and communication system (PACS), to the time of interpretation rendered
(from the RIS). A cost comparison is discussed in terms of potential productivity
gains and device expenditures. Comparative results of a screen-film (analog) dedic
ated chest unit versus a CR reader and a DR dedicated chest unit show a higher
patient throughput for the digital systems. A mean of 8.2 patients were moved th
rough the analog chest room per hour, versus 9.2 patients per hour using the CR
system and 10.7 patients per hour with the DR system. This represents a 12% in
crease in patient throughput for CR over screen-film; a 30% increase in patient t
hroughput for DR over screen-film, which is statistically significant; and a 16% i
ncrease in patient throughput for DR over CR, which is not statistically significan
t. Measured time to image availability for interpretation is much faster for both C
R and DR versus screen-film, with the mean minutes to image availability calcula
ted as 29.2 ± 14.3 min for screen-film, 6.7 ± 1.5 min for CR, and 5.7 ± 2.5 mi
n for DR. This represents an improved time to image availability of 77% for CR

over screen-film, 80% for DR over screen-film, and 15% for DR over CR. Thes
e results are statistically significant (P <.0001) for both CR over screen-film and
DR over screen-film but not statistically significant for DR over CR. A comparis
on of the digital technology costs illustrates that the high cost of DR may not be
justifiable unless a facility has a steady high patient volume to run the device at
or near 100% productivity. Both CR and DR can improve workflow and product
ivity over analog screen-film in a PACS for delivery of projection radiography se
rvices in an outpatient environment. Cost justification for DR over CR appears to
be tied predominantly to high patient volume and continuous rather than sporadic
use patterns.
<Analysis of the literature>
Performance Aspect
This study is to objectively assess and compare computed radiography (CR), digital radiography (DR) and
screen-film in terms of workflow, productivity, speed of service, and potential cost justification for performing
upright chest examinations on outpatients. This conducted objective timing studies to evaluate patient
throughput and time to image availability across different imaging modalities. Patient throughput was measured
by calculating the time a patient spent in the chest procedure room, from entry to exit. Productivity was assessed

chest exams per period. The measurements were taken during typical workdays and weeks to account for both
consistent and sporadic activity periods. To compare the means between modalities and test for significance,
statistical analysis using unpaired t-tests was performed.
Patient throughput was 30% higher for DR than CR and 12% higher for CR than screen-film. Time to image
availability for interpretation was 77% shorter for CR than screen-film and 80% shorter for DR than screen-
film.
