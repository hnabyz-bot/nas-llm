Introduction

General

This clinical evaluation plan is documented according to the Part A of Annex XIV of Regulation (EU) 2017/745. The clinical evaluation shall be thorough and objective, and take into account both favourable and unfavourable data. Its depth and extent shall be proportionate and appropriate to the nature, classification, intended purpose and risks of the device in question, as well as to the manufacturer’s claims in respect of the device.

Related references

Regulation (EU) 2017/745 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 5 April 2017

Demonstration of equivalence

A clinical evaluation may be based on clinical data relating to a device for which equivalence to the device in question can be demonstrated. The following technical, biological and clinical characteristics shall be taken into consideration for the demonstration of equivalence:

— Technical: the device is of similar design; is used under similar conditions of use; has similar specifications and properties including physicochemical properties such as intensity of energy, tensile strength, viscosity, surface characteristics, wavelength and software algorithms; uses similar deployment methods, where relevant; has similar principles of operation and critical performance requirements;

— Biological: the device uses the same materials or substances in contact with the same human tissues or body fluids for a similar kind and duration of contact and similar release characteristics of substances, including degradation products and leachables;

— Clinical: the device is used for the same clinical condition or purpose, including similar severity and stage of disease, at the same site in the body, in a similar population, including as regards age, anatomy and physiology; has the same kind of user; has similar relevant critical performance in view of the expected clinical effect for a specific intended purpose.

The characteristics listed above shall be similar to the extent that there would be no clinically significant difference in the safety and clinical performance of the device. Considerations of equivalence shall be based on proper scientific justification. It shall be clearly demonstrated that manufacturers have sufficient levels of access to the data relating to devices with which they are claiming equivalence in order to justify their claims of equivalence.

Documentation of clinical evaluation

The results of the clinical evaluation and the clinical evidence on which it is based shall be documented in a clinical evaluation report which shall support the assessment of the conformity of the device.

The clinical evidence together with non-clinical data generated from non-clinical testing methods and other relevant documentation shall allow the manufacturer to demonstrate conformity with the general safety and performance requirements and shall be part of the technical documentation for the device in question.

Both favourable and unfavourable data considered in the clinical evaluation shall be included in the technical documentation.

State of the art and alternative available treatment options

X-ray radiography is the most common medical imaging procedure. In the last two decades, digital radiography has emerged as a viable technology for acquiring x-ray images. Digital radiography offers a number of new capabilities such as post-processing, electronic archival, concurrent access to the images, and improved image distribution. The first of such technologies to become commercially stable was commuted Radiography (CR). More recently, advances in thin-film transistor technology have led to the development of solid-state flat-panel digital radiographic systems. At comparable resolution, these devices offer detective quantum efficiencies (DQEs) Higher than CR and analog scree-film systems.

Currently, there are two general types of flat-panel imaging detectors, direct and indirect, named for the mechanism of x-ray detection they utilize. The indirect flat-panel detectors, which are more common, use a layer of phosphor material that converts x-rays to visible photons. There are also two general types of indirect digital radiographic systems based on the phosphor screens that they employ. In one type, a structured phosphor screen is used as the scintillator, commonly in the form of crystalline CsI Grown in perpen-dicular structures. In the other type, the phosphor screen is turbid in nature with phosphor particles suspended in a layer of binding material. These kinds of indirect detectors often use rare-earth phosphor screens of the type used in screen-film radiography (e.g., Gd2O2S). In either type of detector, the visible photons are subsequently captured by a layer of photodiode pixel elements, where the energy of the light photons is converted to electronic charge. The charges are stored in the capacitors associated with individual pixels. After exposure, the stored charges are read electronically line by line via the panel’s gate and amplifier lines, and the image data are sent to a computer for immediate processing, display, storage, and/or transmission.

The introduction of flat-panel detectors in 2000 led to the development of fully digitalized x-ray systems. Due to their better dose quantum efficiency (DQE), dose reduction could be achieved as compared with conventional film–screen combinations. Further advantages were the instant availability of images and the avoidance of transport of the cassettes to the reading station. Today, digital imaging technology is realized in the majority of radiological departments, creating a filmless workflow. Technical advances and price reductions of hardware and storage media are the reasons why digital imaging now does not suffer from any disadvantages compared with conventional film-based imaging.

These proposed devices are the detectors with indirect energy conversion.

Benchmark device

State of the art

The state-of-the-art in detector for X-ray radiography is the Direct Conversion Flat Panel Detector. The direct conversion flat panel detector is technology is fabricated by depositing a layer of X-ray-sensitized materials onto pixelated TFT arrays capable of directly converting X-ray photons into electrical charges that allow being transferred to thin-film transistors. The most commonly used photoconductor material is amorphous selenium (α-Se) fabricated by evaporation at high temperatures. Upon X-ray irradiation, the α-Se photoconductor can absorb the X-ray energy and convert it into charge carriers which are proportional to the incident X-ray photons. The hole-electron pairs generated in the photoconductor travel along the field lines parallelly with limited lateral diffusion because of the electric field applied in the α-Se. Holes can be collected by the positive bias electrode, whereas electrons can be collected by collection electrodes. The charges are stored on the storage capacitor and then are subsequently read out by thin-film transistors. Each pixel is effectively separated by the field-shaping in the α-Se layer, contributing to a high-quality X-ray image.

Alternative treatments

Our device is an Indirect Conversion Flat Panel Detector for Digital Radiography and there are alternative treatments like Screen-film(X-ray film) for Analog Radiography(Conventional Radiography) and Imaging Plate for Computed Radiography.

The conventional X-ray film consists of a flexible polyester base and a layer of photosensitive emulsion containing silver halide microcrystals. When exposed to X-rays or visible light, these crystals can capture energy and move electrons from the valence band to the conduction band. These electrons become trapped in areas with crystal structure distortion and attract positively charged silver ions, forming metallic silver atoms and creating a latent image. During development, the metallic silver content determines the image's darkness or optical density. The final image is revealed through a photochemical processing process.

Imaging Plate (IP) is a digital replacement for conventional X-ray film. It contains a layer of photostimulable phosphor (PSP) and is used in a cassette similar to traditional X-ray cassettes. X-ray radiation is absorbed by the phosphor layer and stored as energy in metastable regions called F-centers. This stored energy forms a latent image. To reveal the image, the IP is scanned by a laser beam in a reader. The laser scans the IP, and the emitted light, generated during photostimulation, is collected by an optical fiber. This data is processed to create a diagnostic image. The scanning process involves moving the IP in one direction while the laser beam moves perpendicular to it, covering the entire plate surface.

Methods to be used for examination of qualitative and quantitative aspects of clinical safety

For examination of qualitative and quantitative aspects of clinical safety, we search literatures and articles for past studies of equivalence products and the general states of the art, and select more 10 literatures. The source of data will be from scientific literature databases (such as PubMed). The selected literature includes studies comparing the dose and acquisition time between digital radiography, the state of the art using a digital flat panel X-ray detector, and and alternative treatment options, screen film radiography and computed radiography.

High-quality clinical studies should provide information on used method(s), used product(s), number of patients exposed to the device, clinical outcome, all the results the clinical study or investigation planned to investigate, undesirable side-effects that have been observed, confidence intervals/calculation of statistical significance, if there are intent-to-treat and per protocol populations, definitions and results for the population.

The data search key words and limits for the start and end dates of each search will be set. Clinical studies are evaluated through scoring, and the evaluation criteria follow the IMDRF MDCE WG (PD1)/N55 (formerly GHTF/SG5/N1R8:2007). The detailed literature search protocols and appraisal criteria for suitability and data contribution are documented in the A-TCF-5-120 Literature Study.

Another the source of data will be from internet searches for recall data (such as FDA Recall, MFDS Product recall and Suspension of Sales, and MHRA Alerts and recalls) and non-published data (such as the label and instruction for use of the equivalent or benchmark devices).

Also, we additionally use clinical-related information from PMS to re-confirm whether the A1717MCW/A1417MCW/F1417MCW has clinical benefits and residual risks. According to the PMS plan (A-PMS-5-001 Post-market Surveillance Plan), we will collect and utilize available information such as incident documentation, feedback and customer complaints, compile list of trend depending on collected data, scientific literature, and recall data. Please refer to A-PSUR-5-001 Periodic Safety Update Report. The PMCF will be conducted as a part of the PMS activities. According to the PMCF plan (A-PMCFP-5-001 PMCF plan), customer survey data and clinical evaluation data will be collected, analyzed and reported every two years.

After all these activities, the determination of residual risks and side-effects will be stated in A-TCF-5-104 Risk Management Report. Regardless of determined level of frequency and severity of a residual risk and side effect, if such residual risk and side effect cannot be completely eliminated, such risk and side effect shall be determined as residual risk and side effect. All observed adverse events shall be documented, and the course of event should include whether medical intervention (any form of treatment) was necessary to relieve the side-effect, time taken to resolve the side-effect, and whether the event relieved without medical intervention (any form of treatment).

Clinical benefit

The clinical benefits of the Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector include:

Reduction in patient dose in comparison with the film screen system

The device provides higher image quality at the same dose than screen film radiography (SFR), allowing a physician or radiologist to make a more accurate diagnosis. This can help them formulate an accurate treatment plan and provide appropriate care to the patient. It can also lower the dose to the patient.

1. Reduction in patient dose in comparison with the film screen system: screen film radiography(SFR)보다 동일한 선량에서 더 높은 이미지 품질을 제공하여 의사나 방사선과학자가 더 정확한 진단을 내릴 수 있습니다. 이는 환자에게 정확한 치료 계획을 수립하고 적절한 치료를 제공하는데 도움이 될 수 있습니다. 또한 환자의 피폭 선량을 낮출 수 있습니다.

Fast image acquisition time

Digital radiography (DR) using the Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector allows for filmless imaging (no developing process), which means that images can be acquired in real time. Therefore, providing physicians and radiologists with shorter image acquisition times, enabling faster diagnosis and faster treatment planning for patients.

2. Fast image acquisition time: Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector를 이용한 digital radiography(DR)은 필름 없이 이미지를 촬영하도록 해주므로(인화 과정 없음) 실시간으로 이미지를 획득할 수 있습니다. 따라서 의사나 방사선과학자에게 단축된 이미지 획득 시간을 제공함으로써 더 빠른 진단이 가능하고, 환자에게 빠른 치료 계획을 수립할 수 있습니다.

위에서 기술한 첫 번째 임상 이득의 관련 clinical outcome parameter는 detective quantum efficiency (DQE), modulation transfer function (MTF) and linearity입니다. 두 번째 임상 이득의 관련 recovery time, image confirmation time입니다.
