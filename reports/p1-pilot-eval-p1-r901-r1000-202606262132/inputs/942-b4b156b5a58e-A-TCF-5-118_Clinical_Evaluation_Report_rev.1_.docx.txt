Summary

The primary purpose of this report is to evaluate conformity assessment with requirement on safety and performance and acceptable benefit-risk ratio (Regulation (EU) 2017/745 GSPR 1), and acceptability of undesirable side-effects (Regulation (EU) 2017/745 GSPR 8).

This report is made in accordance with the MEDDEV 2.7/1 and Regulation (EU) 2017/745, Annex XIV.

Requirement on safety and acceptable benefit-risk ratio (Regulation (EU) 2017/745 GSPR 1)

We evaluated the safety feature of the device through the device risk management documentation and necessary evaluation from a clinical perspective. The risks identified in the risk management documentation and literature studies have been adequately addressed and all the hazards and other clinically relevant information have been identified appropriately.

The safety characteristics and intended purpose of the device are described in the IFU (Instruction for Use).

We prepared clinical data and the information materials which have full consistency between current knowledge/the state of the art and the risk management documentation for the device.

We researched and summarized the total experience with the device and PMCF (Post Market Clinical Follow-up) of the equivalent device in the market. Nature, extent/severity, probability/frequency, duration of benefits to the patients and of undesirable side-effects and other risks have been considered.

Requirement on performance (Regulation (EU) 2017/745 GSPR 1)

Clinical performance including its claims about clinical properties and safety of the device was evaluated through achievement of device’s intended performance during normal conditions of use and sufficient clinical evidence.

Requirement on acceptability of undesirable side-effects (Regulation (EU) 2017/745 GSPR 8)

Evaluation on the acceptability of the side-effects of a device was considered based on the collected clinical data about the nature, severity and frequency of potential undesirable side-effects. Undesirable side-effects were considered, and we concluded that they are acceptable through the evaluation of collected clinical data and references including properties of benchmark devices that are currently available to the patients in the market, and objective performance criteria from applicable standards and guidance documents.

Warnings

Environment of Use and Storage

Follow the specified process of operation instruction written in this manual for the safety of the users and patients.

Connection

Do not connect the detector with anything other than specified. Otherwise, it may result in fire or electric shock.

To avoid the risk of electric shock, this detector must only be connected to supply mains with protective earth.

Avoid installing the system in such way that it is difficult for the operator to disconnect the appliance plug.

Do not touch Signal Input Part/Signal Output Part and the patient simultaneously. There is a risk of electric shock from leakage current.

Additional equipment connected to medical electrical equipment must comply with the respective IEC or ISO standards. Furthermore, all configurations shall comply with the requirements for medical electrical systems. Anybody connecting additional equipment to medical electrical equipment configures a medical system and is therefore responsible that the system complies with the requirements for medical electrical systems. Attention is drawn to the fact that local laws take priority over the above-mentioned requirements.

Equipment connected to the detector and in the patient, environment must be powered from a medically-isolated power source or must be a medically-isolated device. Equipment powered from a non-isolated source can result in chassis leakage currents exceeding safe levels. Chassis leakage current created by an accessory or device connected to a non-isolated outlet may add to the chassis leakage current of the detector.

The x-ray systems and console software used with the product must be CE certified.

Handling

Always be sure to keep checking the condition of the system and the patient to ensure they are normal during the use of the detector. If any problem is found, take appropriate measures, such as stopping the operation of the detector, as required.

Never disassemble or modify the detector as it may result in fire or electric shock.

Also, since the detector incorporates parts that may cause electric shocks and other hazardous parts, touching them may cause death or serious injury.

Do not hit or drop the detector. The detector may be damaged if it receives a strong jolt, which may result in fire or electric shock if the detector is used without being repaired.

When Problem Occurs

Should any of the following occur, immediately remove the battery from the detector, unplug the power supply cord from the AC outlet, and contact H&abyz Customer Service team or authorized distributor.

When there is smoke, odd smell or abnormal sound.

When liquid has been spilled into the detector or a metal object has entered through an opening.

When the detector has been dropped and it is damaged.

If there are no side effects but other problems occur, please contact H&abyz customer service center.

Maintenance and Inspection

For safety reasons, be sure to turn off the power of the detector when the following inspections are going to be performed. Otherwise, it may result in electric shock.

Make sure to turn off the detector, remove the battery, and unplug the power cable from the AC outlet before cleaning.

Do not use any type of solvent, such as benzene. Otherwise, fire or electric shock may result.

Precautions for use

Preparation

Be sure to connect the cables to the proper connectors. Otherwise, the detector may malfunction or may be damaged.

The battery should be equipped at a specified location. Otherwise, the detector may malfunction or be damaged.

Handling

Handle the detector carefully, as it may become damaged if it is hit, dropped, or receives a strong jolt.

Be sure to use the detector on a flat place so it will not bend. Otherwise, the detector may be damaged.

Be sure to check the detector daily and confirm that it works properly. Sudden heating of the room in cold areas will cause condensation to form on the detector. In this case, wait until condensation disappears before performing exposure. If the detector is used with condensation formed on it, problems may occur in the quality of the detector. When an air-conditioner is going to be used, be sure to raise/lower the temperature gradually so that a difference in temperature in the room and in the detector does not occur, to prevent forming of condensation. Follow the recommended proper room temp.

Do not use the detector near devices generating a strong magnetic field. Doing so may produce image noise or artifacts.

Keep the connectors free from being in contact with the patient.

Connectors are intended to be connected to an external device and must follow IEC standards.

Do not apply excessive weight to the detector. Otherwise, the detector may be damaged.

Use a Weight Distribution Cap when weight of a patient is concentrated on one particular point.

General description of the medical device

The A1717MCW/A1417MCW/F1417MCW are wired or wireless digital flat panel detectors that have been designed for faster, more streamlined approach to digital radiography systems. The detector consists of TFT and Scintillator (CsI) and an electronic device that converts data read through it into digital signals. The detectors support an auto exposure detection technology that allows the detectors to be used without generator integration.

The flat panel sensors of the A1717MCW/A1417MCW/F1417MCW are fabricated using thin film technology based on amorphous silicon technology. Electronically, the sensors are much like conventional photodiode arrays. Each pixel in the array consists of a light‐sensing photodiode and a switching Thin Film Transistor (TFT) in the same electronic circuit. The amorphous silicon photodiode is sensitive to visual light, and its response curve has almost the same performance as human vision. The sensitivity of amorphous silicon photodiodes peaks in green wavelengths, well matched to scintillators such as CsI. It also has excellent linearity according to the exposure dose.

Principles of operation

The X-ray output from the X-ray generator passes through the object to be photographed and is then converted into a digital signal through the detector to be output to the image signal. X-rays weaken in dense tissue such as bones, and soft tissue uses the principle of easy passage to adjust the brightness of the image to express the signal.

The indirect type detector consists of a Scintillator for converting X-rays into visible light and a TFT panel for converting them into data. A TFT array consists of covered with a thin layer of silicon that is amorphous or disordered, and silicon is engraved with millions of transistors arranged in higher order like a grid of graph paper. Each of these thin-film transistors (TFTs) is attached to a light-absorbing photodiode that makes up individual pixels, and visible light entering the photodiode is converted by intensity into a digital signal and output to the screen in a readable digital image.

Clinical background, current knowledge, state of the art

 Description of historical context and developments

X-ray radiography is the most common medical imaging procedure. In the last two decades, digital radiography has emerged as a viable technology for acquiring x-ray images. Digital radiography offers a number of new capabilities such as post-processing, electronic archival, concurrent access to the images, and improved image distribution. The first of such technologies to become commercially stable was commuted Radiography (CR). More recently, advances in thin-film transistor technology have led to the development of solid-state flat-panel digital radiographic systems. At comparable resolution, these devices offer detective quantum efficiencies (DQEs) Higher than CR and analog scree-film systems.

Currently, there are two general types of flat-panel imaging detectors, direct and indirect, named for the mechanism of x-ray detection they utilize. The indirect flat-panel detectors, which are more common, use a layer of phosphor material that converts x-rays to visible photons. There are also two general types of indirect digital radiographic systems based on the phosphor screens that they employ. In one type, a structured phosphor screen is used as the scintillator, commonly in the form of crystalline CsI Grown in perpen-dicular structures. In the other type, the phosphor screen is turbid in nature with phosphor particles suspended in a layer of binding material. These kinds of indirect detectors often use rare-earth phosphor screens of the type used in screen-film radiography (e.g., Gd2O2S). In either type of detector, the visible photons are subsequently captured by a layer of photodiode pixel elements, where the energy of the light photons is converted to electronic charge. The charges are stored in the capacitors associated with individual pixels. After exposure, the stored charges are read electronically line by line via the panel’s gate and amplifier lines, and the image data are sent to a computer for immediate processing, display, storage, and/or transmission.

The introduction of flat-panel detectors in 2000 led to the development of fully digitalized x-ray systems. Due to their better dose quantum efficiency (DQE), dose reduction could be achieved as compared with conventional film–screen combinations. Further advantages were the instant availability of images and the avoidance of transport of the cassettes to the reading station. Today, digital imaging technology is realized in the majority of radiological departments, creating a filmless workflow. Technical advances and price reductions of hardware and storage media are the reasons why digital imaging now does not suffer from any disadvantages compared with conventional film-based imaging.
