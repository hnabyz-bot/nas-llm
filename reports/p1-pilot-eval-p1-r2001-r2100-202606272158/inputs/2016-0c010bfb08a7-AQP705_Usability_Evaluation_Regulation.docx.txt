Purpose

The purpose of this regulation is to be applied in conjunction with the company's design and development activities. Through usability engineering, it aims to develop user-centered medical devices, reduce use errors and associated risks, and enhance the safety and effectiveness of medical devices.

Applied Area

This regulation applies to the overall activities related to the design and development of products manufactured by the company, including planning, inputs and outputs, review, verification, validation, drawing control, changes, and transfer, as well as the basic requirements of the company’s Quality Management System

Work procedures

Preparing the User Specifications

The development team prepares a user specification that includes the following.

1) Intended medical indication (purpose of use)

2) Intended patient population (medical device users)

3) Type of body part or tissue intended to be applied or interacted with

(site of action)

4) Intended user profile (age group, language spoken, occupation, relevant

medical conditions, etc.)

5) Intended use environment (environment of the location where the

medical device will be used, such as temperature, humidity, and ambient

sound)

6) Principle of operation

The user specifications developed by the development team are reviewed by the Quality Manager and approved by the CEO. Approved user specifications are stored in the usability engineering file.

Identifying safety-related user interface features and potential usage errors

The development team shall identify user interface features that may be safety-relevant as part of the risk analysis performed in accordance with ISO 14971:2019, 5.3.

The safety-related user interface characteristics and potential usage errors can be identified by answering the questions presented in ISO 24971:2020 Annex A, which should include at least the questions presented in A 2.1, 2.9, 2.12, 2.28, 2.29, 2.31-2.37.

The development team should identify potential user interface-related errors based on the identified user interface characteristics and user specifications. This identification can be accomplished by conducting a task analysis.

The results of the identification of characteristics related to safety and potential use errors are reviewed by the Quality Manager, approved by the CEO, and stored in the usability engineering file.

Identify known or foreseeable hazards and hazardous situations

The development team must identify known or foreseeable hazards and hazardous situations that could affect patients, users, or others associated with the use of the medical device.

This identification should be performed as part of the risk analysis (ISO 14971:2019, 5.4) during medical device risk management.

The development team should consider the following while identifying risk factors and risk situations.

User specifications including user profiles (see Section 5.1)

Information on known hazards and risk situations in existing user interfaces for similar types of medical devices, if available

Identified usage errors (see Section 5.2)

The results of this identification of risk factors and risk situations are stored in the usability engineering file.

Identify and describe use scenarios related to risk factors

The development team shall identify and describe reasonably foreseeable hazard-related usage scenarios related to the identified hazards and hazard situations.

The description of each identified risk factor-related usage scenario should include all operations as well as the severity of the associated risk.

Selecting Usage Scenarios Related to Risk Factors for Overall Evaluation

The development team should select factor-relevant usage scenarios to include in the overall evaluation.

Usage scenarios related to hazard factors can be selected based on the severity or risk of hazards that may arise from usage errors (see ISO 14971:2019, 5.5)

A summary of the selection technique, the rationale for its use, and the results of its application are stored in the usability engineering file.

Establishing a User Interface Specification

The development team writes a user interface specification document designing to eliminate or reduce the risk of errors in use for the scenarios selected in Section 5.5.

The development team should establish and maintain a user interface specification document, taking into account the following.

User specifications (see Section 5.1)

Known or foreseeable use errors related to the medical device (see Section 5.3)

Usage scenarios related to risk factors (see Section 5.4)

The user interface specification must include.

1) User requirements

2) Technical requirements related to the user interface (display color, text

size, etc.)

3) Requirement of supporting documentation

4) Requirement of separate training

This activity is related to risk control during risk management, and should appropriately control risks related to usage errors by using one or more of the following items listed in order of priority.

Design changes to medical devices

Establishment of protective measures (supplementary devices) in the medical device itself or in the manufacturing process

Providing information on safety

User interface specifications are stored in the usability engineering file. User interface specifications can be integrated into other specifications.

Developing a User Interface Evaluation Plan

General

The development team must establish and maintain a user interface evaluation plan for the user interface.

The user interface evaluation plan should.

Document the purpose and identify planned formative and summative evaluation methods

When applying a usability test as an evaluation method

Document the user population to be included in the test. For conformance testing, you can combine multiple user profiles into a user population, such as.

In formative assessment, clinical personnel, who are employees of the manufacturer, are used to represent the nurse user group.

In the summative assessment, the panel of intensive care nurse practitioners is used to represent the population of critical care nursing users.

Document the test environment and other usage conditions based on the usage specifications

Specific usage conditions that may affect the user's ability to perform tasks.

Conditions of use may include location-related conditions such as lighting, noise, and activity levels.

Conditions of use may include personnel-related conditions such as wearing personal protective equipment (e.g., surgical gloves and safety glasses) and using medical devices.

Conditions of use may include social conditions such as stress levels and team work.

Regulations on whether supporting documents are provided during the examination

Whether medical device-related training is provided prior to testing and the minimum elapsed time between training and the start of testing

There are various methods for evaluating user interfaces and they can be quantitative or qualitative.

User interface evaluation can be performed in several locations, including a laboratory environment, a virtual user environment, or an actual user environment.

The user interface evaluation plan can be integrated into other plans.

The user interface evaluation plan must be stored in the usability engineering file.

Formative Evaluation Plan

Formative evaluation is a stage to evaluate whether there are any unexpected usage errors and risks associated with the user interface of a medical device in the early stages of development that have performed activities in sections 5.1 to 5.6.

The development team must conduct formative evaluations at least 2-3 times to identify shortcomings in the user interface and improve them through the risk control methods in Section 5.6.

The user interface evaluation plan for formative assessment should include the following.

Evaluation method to be used

Parts of the user interface to be evaluated

Timing of performing each user interface evaluation in the usability engineering process

The plan and results of formative evaluation are stored in a usability engineering file.

Summative Evaluation Plan

The comprehensive evaluation is the final step to check whether actual use errors occur according to the ‘use scenarios related to risk factors’ selected in Section 5.5 for medical devices that have been subject to multiple formative evaluations to control risks related to use errors, and to determine whether use-related risks have been appropriately controlled and whether the user interface can be used effectively and safely.

The development team must include the following in the comprehensive evaluation plan.

Basis for producing objective evidence of the evaluation method to be used and the method

The overall evaluation of safety information may require different methods than other parts of the user interface.

Parts of the user interface to be evaluated

Where applicable, criteria for determining whether safety information can be perceived and understood and whether this information supports the proper use of the medical device

The overall evaluation of safety information is typically completed before the overall evaluation of the rest of the user interface begins. This is usually a separate usability test conducted by other users.

Provision of usability and training of attached documents during comprehensive evaluation

Summative assessments can appropriately incorporate training as part of the plan to simulate real-world use. To account for typical learning forgetting, an appropriate waiting period may be necessary between the training and the remainder of the summative assessment.

Usability testing

How the test participant's characteristics represent the intended user profile

Justify how test participants are grouped into individual user groups to determine the number of test participants

Theoretical basis for how to appropriately represent the test environment and conditions of use, and the intended use environment

Definition of correct usage for each risk factor-related usage scenario

How to collect data during usability testing for subsequent analysis of observed usage errors and difficulty of use

Design, implement, and conduct formative evaluation of the user interface

The development team conducts formative evaluations in accordance with the formative evaluation plan in Section 5.7.2, along with the design and implementation of the user interface, in the early stages of medical device development.

The development team shall design and implement the user interface, including any supporting documentation (if required) and training capabilities (if required) specified in the user interface specification.

The development team should achieve this design and implementation by appropriately utilizing usability engineering methods and techniques, including formative evaluation. The results of the formative evaluation should be stored in a usability engineering file.

If a new usage scenario involving a use error, risk, hazard, or risk factor is discovered during this step, the risk for the use error must be controlled and the activities of this regulation must be repeated as appropriate.

If training for a specific medical device is required for the intended users to use the device safely, training capabilities must be designed and implemented for the expected useful life of the medical device by performing one or more of the following.

Provision of materials necessary for training

Ensure availability of materials necessary for training

Regulations to enable training

The organization responsible for training users shall be established to enable training

Conduct a comprehensive usability evaluation of the user interface

When the development team completes the design and implementation of the user interface, it conducts a comprehensive evaluation according to the user interface evaluation plan.

A comprehensive evaluation of each risk factor-related use scenario selected in Section 5.5 is conducted for the final or equivalent user interface to be produced to finally assess whether the risks of use errors identified in the previous steps have been effectively controlled and whether the usability of the medical device has been secured.

For summative evaluations, the user interface may use data obtained from summative evaluations of the same product, together with a technical justification for applying this data.

The development team must analyze the overall evaluation data and identify any usage errors and difficulties encountered.

If a usage error or difficulty in use could lead to a hazardous situation, the root cause of such usage error or difficulty in use must be determined.

The root cause should be determined based on methods including observation of user performance as well as the user's subjective opinion.

If new usage errors, hazards, hazardous situations, or usage scenarios related to hazards are discovered during this data analysis, the activities outlined in this regulation should be repeated as appropriate. Otherwise, it should be determined whether improvements to the safety-related user interface design are necessary and feasible.

If improvements to the user interface design related to safety are necessary and feasible, re-perform the usability engineering process in Section 5.6.

If improvements to the user interface design related to safety are not necessary or feasible, perform the following.

Document the reasons why improvement is not needed or cannot be implemented

Identification of data from the usability engineering process needed to determine the residual risk associated with use

Residual risk assessment (see ISO 14971:2019, 7.3)

The plan and results of the summative evaluation should be stored in the usability engineering file.

If the usability engineering process detailed in this regulation has been followed, the usability of a medical device with respect to safety is considered acceptable unless there is objective evidence to the contrary.

Objective evidence can arise from post-production surveillance.

Evaluation of User Interfaces of Unknown Origin (UOUP)

For user interfaces or user interface parts that do not have adequate development records using the usability engineering process of IEC 62366-1:2015, the process of 5.10.2 applies. However, if the user interface or part thereof is modified, only the unchanged portion of the user interface shall be maintained as a UOUP, and the modified portion of the user interface shall comply with 5.1 through 5.9 of this standard.

Usability Engineering Process for User Interfaces of Unknown Origin

User specifications

Establish a usage specification in accordance with the requirements of Article 5.1 of these regulations.

Review of post-production information

Review available post-production information, including field reports of complaints and incidents or non-incidents regarding UOUP medical devices.

All identified cases of usage errors that could cause hazardous situations, or cases where hazardous factors or hazardous situations suggested by field information could occur due to improper usability, should be stored in the usability engineering file and addressed in 5.10.2 3) ~ 4).

Risk factors and risk situations related to usability

Review the risk analysis of the UOUP medical device and confirm that the risk factors and risk situations related to suitability for use have been identified and documented.

Risk control

Confirm and document that appropriate risk control measures have been implemented for all identified risk factors and risk situations, and that all risk factors have been reduced to an acceptable level based on the risk assessment.

If you decide to change a part of the user interface to reduce the risk to an acceptable level, the changed part is not considered a UOUP and is subject to the requirements of clauses 5.1 to 5.9.

Residual risk assessment

Re-evaluate the overall residual risk based on the new information identified by performing 5.10.2.3) ~ 4) and document the results in the usability engineering file or risk management file.
