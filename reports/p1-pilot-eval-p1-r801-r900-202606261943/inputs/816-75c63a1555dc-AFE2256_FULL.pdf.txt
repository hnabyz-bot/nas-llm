• High Performance:

3 Description
The AFE2256GR device is a 256-channel, analog
front-end (AFE) designed to suit the requirements of
flat-panel detector (FPD)-based digital x-ray systems.
The device includes 256 integrators, a correlated
double sampler (CDS) with dual banking, and 256:4
analog multiplexers.
The device also features four 16-bit successive-
approximation register (SAR) analog-to-digital
converters (ADCs). Serial data from the ADCs are
available in low-voltage differential signaling (LVDS)
format.
The Nap and power-down modes enable substantial
power saving that is especially useful in battery-
powered systems.

7 Detailed Description
7.1 Overview
The AFE2256GR is a 256-channel, charge-to-digital converter. The device is specifically designed to meet
performance specifications of x-ray imaging systems. The AFE2256GR can also be used in multichannel
capacitance measurement applications.
The AFE2256GR supports both electron and hole integration, without any hardware change. The device
operates over a wide range of scan times (from 51.2 μs to 204.8 μs), and has a programmable input charge
range from 0.6 pC to 9.6 pC to optimize the dynamic range of the system.
The device has features specific to x-ray imaging applications (for example, charge injection compensation, pixel
short detection, and ultra-low crosstalk). The device provides test modes such as CSENSOR emulation (emulate
panel capacitance), panel isolation (disconnect inputs from panel), test-patterns, and so forth to reduce system
development time.
The AFE2256GR is available in a COF package that can be directly bonded to the x-ray panel. The COF
connects to a printed circuit board (PCB) using a zero insertion force (ZIF) connector.
TI also offers custom COF package design facility. Contact TI for more details.

7.3 Feature Description
7.3.1 Device Operation
The AFE2256GR performs three main functions in parallel during each scan: charge acquisition, analog-to-digital
conversion, and shifting out digital data. These functions are all controlled by the internal timing generator (TG).
This section describes the details of charge acquisition, analog-to-digital conversion, the data shift out operation,
and the functionality of the timing generator.
The AFE2256GR incorporates a high-speed, phase-locked loop (PLL) that locks to MCLK. For successful locking
of the PLL, MCLK must be in the 10-MHz to 20-MHz range. The PLL takes approximately 20 μs to lock after
applying MCLK; this time must be accounted for in the power-up timing.
If MCLK is stopped, the PLL lock time must be accounted for in the system timing. MCLK is recommended to be
free-running so that the PLL is continuously locked.
The timing generator, analog-to-digital converter (ADC), and low-voltage differential signaling (LVDS) interface all
run on the clocks generated by the PLL.
The scan period (tSCAN) or the line repetition period is set by the MCLK period and the scan time range (STR)
setting that is programmed through the serial peripheral interface (SPI). The selected STR defines the integer
number of MCLK cycles in a tSCAN.
Valid values of STR are 2 and 3, corresponding to the two possible values of MCLK cycles (1024 and 2048),
respectively. Table 1 summarizes the tSCAN values for different STR settings and MCLK frequencies.
For AFE22256GR, STR = 0 and 1 are same as STR = 2, that is 1024 MCLKs. To ensure compatibility with
AFE2256TDU and AFE2256TDR, only STR = 2 will be specified throughout this document.

Each sample-and-hold circuit employs two banks of capacitors, referred to as bank A and bank B. Bank B data
from the previous integration cycle are read (through the on-chip ADC) and bank A samples the data from the
ongoing integration.
Conversely, bank A data are read in the next line period and bank B samples data from the ongoing integration.
This pipelined operation helps optimize frame time for integration and data reads.
Each sample-and-hold circuit has a built-in, low-pass filter (LPF). This filter limits the sampling bandwidth in order
to limit the sampled noise. The device features five user-selectable, LPF filter-bandwidth settings: 210 kHz, 105
kHz, 52 kHz, 26 kHz, and 13 kHz.
The timings of the reset and sampling control signals (IRST, SHR, SHS, LPF1, LPF2, DF_SM[5:0], and TDEF)
are programmed by the user through register settings and are then internally generated by a real-time sequencer
termed the timing generator (TG).

The device starts integration when IRST is low and integrates input leakage current when the external TFT (the

data processing is independent of integrate-up or integrate-down mode.

7.3.1.4 Internal Timing Generator (TG)
The device has an internal timing generator (TG) that creates and synchronizes all the internal clocks and control
signals for multiple successful scans. The TG generates these signals with a period of tSCAN. The A_BZ signal is
generated with a period of 2 × tSCAN.
As shown in Figure 31, the TG uses three external CMOS level signals to operate: SYNC, MCLK, and TP_SEL.
Figure 31. Timing Generator Block Diagram
The MCLK signal is the system master clock. All internal front-end and back-end controls and clocks are derived
from MCLK.
The SYNC signal is the synchronization pulse that indicates the beginning of the scan time. When SYNC is
detected, the TG logic starts counting the MCLK cycles and internally generates all required clocks and control
signals for the integrators, the ADCs, and the data serializer. Ideally, SYNC only must be applied the first time to
synchronize the TG with the external controller. Afterwards, the TG keeps track of the MCLK and internally
generates the programmed waveforms with a period of tSCAN.
Note than when SYNC is applied, the scan is always started with A_BZ high. In other words, the AFE2256GR
deterministically selects the CDS bank for the scan. By using the SPI interface, the user can program the rising
and falling edge for every control signal: IRST, SHR, SHS, LPF1, LPF2, TDEF, and DF_SM[5:0].
Each signal is defined by a 16-bit word in the timing generator register space corresponding to that signal.

7.3.1.4.1 Constraints on TG Values
The rising edge can be programmed to be before or after the falling edge, thus providing greater flexibility in
timing. Use this option for charge injection compensation and gate driver signal generation.
The following constraints are applicable to the otherwise fully flexible timing generator register space. These
restrictions are in place only for an active scan in order to retain the analog performance as per the specification:
• The IRST rising edge must not be programmed as 00h.
• The SHR rising edge must be at least one value greater than the IRST falling edge.
• The SHS rising edge must be at least one value greater than the SHR falling edge.
• The LPF1 falling edge must be at least one value greater than the SHR falling edge.
• The LPF2 falling edge must be at least one value greater than the SHS falling edge.
• The SHS falling edge must not be programmed as either 00h or FFh.

7.3.1.4.2 Timing Profiles
The device allows two different timing profiles (TPα and TPβ) to be programmed into the device, thus allowing the timing profile to be quickly changed
using the TP_SEL and SYNC pins. This scheme avoids any latency that results from reprogramming the timing generator register space for a new profile
using the serial interface. The register content of TPα and TPβ must be programmed before proceeding with a valid scan. MCLK must be running when
the timing profiles are programmed.
Both timing profiles (TPα and TPβ) share the same register addresses; that is, address 40h to 55h. For example, address 40h is common to the IRST
signals for both profiles TPα and TPβ.
Only one of the profiles is active (used for timing generation by the TG machine) at a time. The serial interface write operations on the timing generator
register space always occur on the inactive profile, thus ensuring that the active profile is not disturbed when the inactive profile is modified.
TP_SEL_SYNC (generated by latching TP_SEL with SYNC, see Figure 31) is used to decide which profile is active.
The user can switch between TPα and TPβ by toggling the TP_SEL pin and by providing a SYNC pulse, as shown in Figure 33. The SYNC signal is not
recommended to be applied for successive scans that use the same timing profile. When switching between timing profiles, SYNC must be applied at the
start of a new line of CDS bank A, as shown in Figure 33. Otherwise, the output data (DOUT) bit alignment with respect to DCLK can become corrupted.
If SYNC cannot be applied at the start of a new line (for example, for the first SYNC after power-up), a bit realignment is necessary in the system field-
programmable gate array (FPGA) to correctly realign the bits to correctly form the ADC word from the DOUT serial stream. The bit alignment technique is
described further in the Application and Implementation section.

7.3.2 TFT Charge Injection Compensation and Photodiode Residual Charge Compensation
Turning the thin-film transistor (TFT) of the flat-panel detector on and off injects charge onto the integrator inputs,
thus resulting in decreased dynamic range. The device provides a special feature (enabled by setting register
5Dh) to compensate for this TFT charge during both turn-on and turn-off.
The compensation charge injected by the scheme can be either positive or negative. The compensation scheme
uses the external voltages COMP1 and COMP2, of which one can be grounded.
There are five charge injection capacitors (C[4:0]) that are sized to have a piece-wise approximation of an
exponential charge injection, as given by Equation 1:
C[4] = e × C[3] = e2 × C[2] = e3 × C[1] = e4 × C[0]
where
• e = 2.718 (Napier’s constant) (1)
Each of these capacitors can be fully disconnected if not required by using the static bits DFTV_CAP_SEL_x in
configuration register address 5Dh.
When ENB_DFTV_ALL = 0, all charge injection capacitors are disabled. Set ENB_DFTV_ALL to 1 in order to
use any charge injection capacitor.
The connection between the capacitors C[x] and the COMP1, COMP2 voltage is controlled by the DF_SMx
signals that are generated by the TG. A low value of the DF_SMx signal connects C[x] to COMP1 and a high
value of DF_SMx connects C[x] to COMP2.
Similar to other signals generated by the TG, the rising and falling edges of DF_SMx are determined by the 16-
bit contents of addresses 50h to 54h in the timing generator register space. Furthermore, similar to other control
signals, the register bank contains two separates sets of timing profiles for each DF_SMx signal.
The device can also independently compensate for a stored residual charge on the photodiode by using the
COMP3 and COMP4 external voltages in conjunction with DF_SM5 and capacitor C[5]. Configure the
ENB_DFTV_ALL = 1, DFTV_CAP_SEL_5 = 1, and DF_SM5 timings to use this feature.
The DF_SM5 signal is controlled by the TG similar to DF_SM[4:0]. A low value of the DF_SM5 signal connects
C[5] to COMP3 and a high value of DF_SM5 connects C[5] to COMP4. Only COMP3 can be connected to an
external low-noise voltage source; COMP4 can be grounded.

7.3.3 Quick Wakeup

The power-up time is the minimum time required for the device during power-up from nap or power-down, after
which the device starts providing a stable output. After the power-up time, all internal nodes of the device are at
steady-state values.
The EXTC_I pin has a low-pass filter with a very low cutoff frequency. The low cutoff frequency is essential for
optimum noise performance of the device. During power up, EXTC_I takes a large duration of time to reach a
steady-state value and is the dominating factor influencing power-up time.
Quick wakeup mode (set by register 13h, bit 5 = 1) increases the bandwidth of the EXTC_I low-pass filter, thus
reducing the power-up time. Therefore, to avoid negatively affecting noise, reset the quick wakeup bit to 0 before
starting the data capture. Figure 69, Figure 70, and Figure 71 detail usage of this bit.

7.3.4 Pixel Short Detection
Shorted pixels in the panel overload adjacent channels, thus creating T-shaped artifacts in the image. The device
includes a pixel short-detection feature that is helpful in suppressing the T-defect displayed in the image. The
pixel short-detection mechanism detects a pixel overload condition and immediately resets the integrator. This
feature avoids driving the integrator into deep saturation, which can affect adjacent pixels. Note that because the
integrator is in a reset state, the current drawn by a shorted pixel can be provided without changing the data line
voltage. This feature can be enabled by configuring the ENB_TDEF bit (register bit 5Dh, bit 10) = 1 and selecting
the appropriate timings in register 4Ah; see Figure 1 for the TDEF signal timings.
7.3.5 Csensor Emulation
This feature is provided to emulate the data line capacitance (Csensor) to test the AFE2256GR noise performance.
At the input of every channel, a capacitor with a value of 20 pF, 30 pF, or 50 pF per channel can be added by
configuring the ADD_CAP setting (register 5Dh, bits 12-11), as shown in Figure 35. Using this option, channel
noise can be measured without connecting the devices to a panel. Use Csensor to evaluate and debug system
noise performance. This option is usually used in conjunction with the isolate panel feature (see the Isolate Panel
section) to avoid external noise pickup during noise measurement.
7.3.6 Isolate Panel

7.4 Device Functional Modes
7.4.1 Charge Integration Modes
The AFE2256GR supports electron or hole integration. The charge integration modes are completely register-
programmable, and no hardware changes are required. In electron integration mode, the positive charge drawn
from the channel input increases the integrator output voltage. Therefore, this mode is referred to as integrate-up
mode. Conversely, in hole integration mode, the positive charge flows into the input pin of AFE2256GR, reducing
the integrator output. Therefore, this mode is referred to as integrate-down mode. However, in each mode, the
output code always increases proportionally to the magnitude of the incoming charge. The default register
settings for each mode are different, and must be configured accordingly; see the Register Initialization section
for more details.

7.4.3 Power-Down (PD) Modes
When the device is not performing an active scan, the device can be kept in a power-down state in order to
reduce overall energy consumption of the system. The AFE2256GR offers two power-down modes: nap and
power-down (PD).
The main difference between nap and PD modes is in terms of power consumption and power-up time. See the
Electrical Characteristics table for power consumption and power-up times in these modes.
All register contents are preserved in these modes. Therefore, register reconfiguration is not required at power-
up.
The LVDS interface is powered down during these modes. Thus, if the system uses a sync/deskew pattern-
based bit-alignment technique, then bit realignment is required.
Entry and exit from nap and power-down modes is discussed in the Initialization Setup section.
7.5 Programming
The device provides many registers to allow the user to optimize performance in a use case. Many parameters
are also trimmed at the factory.
7.5.1 Register Initialization
During power-up, the internal registers must be initialized to the default values after the supplies are settled. The
device-specified performance can only be achieved after successful initialization. For example, after power-up
and before the device default values are loaded, the power consumption can be higher than the specified value
as a result of the unknown internal register settings.
Register initialization must be done as follows:
1. Set the RESET bit (register 00h, bit 0) high. Setting this bit initializes the internal registers to the default
value (0h) and then self-clears the RESET bit to low in approximately 200 ns.
2. Optionally, quick wakeup mode (register 13h, bits 15-0 = 0020h) can be set to reduce power-up time. In
order to obtain specified noise performance, disable this mode before initiating data capture if quick wakeup
mode is used (that is, set register 13h, bits 15-0 = 0h).
3. Set the TRIM_LOAD bit (register 30h, bit 1) = 1. Wait for a minimum of 100 μs then reset the TRIM_LOAD
bit (register 30h, bit 1) = 0, which loads the factory-set trim values. This step is essential to get specified
device performance.
4. Program the default register values as explained in the Default Register Settings section.
NOTE
1. If a quick reset of the register space is desired during device operation, setting the RESET bit
high is sufficient.
2. The TRIM_LOAD bit must only be cycled if the AVDD1, AVDD2 power supplies are removed
after the last TRIM_LOAD cycle.
3. A RESET or TRIM_LOAD cycle is not required if only the power modes (nap, power down) are
exercised.
4. For the register initialization to take effect, the AVDD1, AVDD2 supplies must be applied and
stable.
5. All register settings are cleared on reset. Thus, the register configuration described in the
Default Register Settings section is required after each reset.
The register initialization affects the register space, serial interface, and timing generator engines and configures
the device circuits in the default states. When the internal registers change values, the data propagating through
the pipe are affected. At the same time, some glitches can appear on the output as a result of transitioning
register values (for instance, if any of the output-controlling modes change, glitches can occur).

7.5.2.1 Serial Register Readout
The device includes an option to read the contents of the internal registers on the SDOUT pin. Use this option as
a diagnostic check to verify the serial interface communication between the external controller and the ADC. The
data read operation is shown in Figure 37.
By default, the SDOUT pin is tri-stated after power-up and device reset. When the readout mode is enabled
using the REG_READ bit, SDOUT serially outputs the contents of the selected register.
Steps for register readout are as follows:
1. Set the REG_READ bit (register 00h, bit 1) = 1 to enable serial readout mode. This setting disables any
further writes to the internal registers, except for register 00h. Note that the REG_READ bit is also located in
register 00h. Only the contents of register 00h cannot be read in register readout mode.
2. Initiate a serial interface cycle specifying the address of the register (A[7:0]) whose content must be read.
Only the SPI command address field is considered and data are ignored. Data can be set to 0.
3. The device serially outputs the contents (D[15:0]) of the selected register on the SDOUT pin, MSB first.

4. Data are shifted out on the SCLK falling edge. The external controller can latch the contents at the SCLK
rising edge.
5. To exit serial readout mode, reset register 00h, bit 1 = 0 to enable writes to all device registers.

7.5.2.1.1 Timing Profile Register Readout
Register readout operation always reads the register values of the inactive timing profile. Hence, the inactive
timing profile register readout is same as any other register readout. The active timing profile registers cannot be
read directly. Register readout operation does not disturb an ongoing scan.
The steps to read active timing profile registers are:
1. Change the active timing profile to inactive. That is, toggle TP_SEL state and apply SYNC signal. For
example, if TPβ (TP_SEL = 1) is the active timing profile, then change the active timing profile to TPα (make
TP_SEL = 0 and apply SYNC).
2. Set REG_READ (register 00h, bit 1) = 1 to enable register readout. Even though the device was in register
readout (REG_READ = 1) before the timing profile change, program REG_READ bit to 1 again for correct
readout operation.
3. Read the timing profile registers by following the usual register readout steps.
4. To exit register readout, reset REG_READ (register 00h, bit 1) = 0.
5. The active timing profile is restored after timing profile register readout. Using the example in Step 1, TPβ
(TP_SEL = 1) is restored as the active timing profile.

8.1 Application Information
8.1.1 Initialization Setup
8.1.1.1 Recommended Device Start-Up Sequence
The sequence for device start-up is:
1. Apply the power supplies: the AFE2256GR uses two power supplies (AVDD1 and AVDD2). AVDD1 is
recommended to be applied before AVDD2.
2. Apply MCLK and wait for the PLL lock (20 μs).
3. Register initialization is as explained in the Register Initialization section and discusses clearing the register
space, loading the factory trim values, and the default register settings.
4. Program the registers (such as device settings and TG profiles).
5. Apply the TP_SEL, SYNC, and bit alignment (optional).
6. Start integration and readout.
See Figure 69 for the recommended power-up sequence.

8.1.2.1 Bit Alignment
Bit alignment can be done by using either of the two following methods:
• Using FCLK or
• Using the sync/deskew pattern.
8.1.2.1.1 Using FCLK
FCLK sends a fixed bit stream, FFF000h. If FCLK is deserialized identical to DATA deserialization, then the
parallel data has the same bit shift on the DATA and FCLK lines, assuming that delays are matched on the
FCLK and DATA paths.
Figure 74 shows an example of bit alignment using FCLK. After deserialization FCLK has a pattern of 7FF800h,
indicating that a left-shift by 1 is required. Assuming no skew exists between FCLK and DATA, a 1-bit left-shift on
the parallel DOUT gives the correct bit alignment.
(1) The example shown is for CONFIG_MODE = 1.
Figure 74. Bit Alignment Using FCLK
As a result of trace routing length mismatch and FPGA buffer delay mismatch, there can be a skew between
FCLK and DATA at the deserializer. This skew can be corrected during system calibration by adding an
additional bit shift in the bit alignment block.

8.1.2.1.2 Using the Sync/Deskew Pattern
Figure 75 shows a block diagram of the sync/deskew pattern based on the bit-alignment technique. The device is
configured to send a sync/deskew test pattern. After enabling the test pattern, the bit-alignment logic in the FPGA
can be enabled by asserting Align_Enable = 1. The bit-alignment block compares parallel data with the 24-bit
pattern FFF000h to determine the bit shift required for the correct bit alignment. After the bit alignment, the
Align_Enable signal is deasserted to 0. The bit-alignment logic now holds the calculated bit-shift value. The
device can be configured to send the normal channel data by disabling the test pattern. The bit-alignment logic
applies the same bit shift to obtain the correct parallel data. Note that MCLK must not be disturbed between the
alignment and data capture, otherwise the bit-alignment value can change.

8.1.2.2 Word Alignment
Word alignment is essential to identify the start of a new row in the parallel data stream. Consider the forward
scan mode where channel data are in the following order: 0, 64, 128, 192, 1, 65, 129, 193, 2, 66, 130…63, 127,
191, 255; 0, 64, and so forth.
The AFE2256GR sends an alignment vector, as described in the Alignment Vector section. Thus, in forward scan
mode, the FPGA receives four successive samples with Y = 1 corresponding to the data of channels 0, 64, 128,
and 192. Using this information, the start of a row can be detected.
The gate driver start signal can be used to synchronize the gate driver signals and data capture. In such a case,
the word alignment block gets the gate driver start signal as an input. Incoming data from the bit alignment block
are ignored until the gate driver start is asserted. After asserting gate-start, the word-alignment block starts
sending data to the image generation logic on the subsequent first channel.
Figure 76 shows the location of the first row with respect to the gate driver start signal. Data capture starts at
detection of four successive samples with Y = 1.

8.1.3 Generating Gate Driver Signals from the AFE2256GR
The AFE2256GR provides a feature where gate driver control signals can be generated from the device. This
feature is possible because the device provides one fully functional additional TG register for this purpose and
the associated TG signal can be brought out on the SDOUT pin. This additional TG register is available in both
timing profiles (TPα and TPβ), just like the other TG signals.
In a typical application scenario, the SDOUT pin of the device is routed to the FPGA or gate driver logic. The
FPGA can directly send a signal on SDOUT to the gate driver or can process the signals further (such as
counting the number of lines).
Note that each device can produce only one gate driver signal. which is not a limitation because a typical system
has multiple AFE2256GR devices and each device can provide a different signal on SDOUT. These signals are
synchronized by the common SYNC signal. For example, one device can produce the gate output enable signal
and other device can be configured to generate the gate driver clock. To use this scheme, the SDOUT signal
from multiple devices must be independently routed to the FPGA.
If the SDOUT from a single device is connected to the FPGA, then the gate driver output enable signal can be
generated from the device. Using this signal, other signals can be derived from inside the FPGA.
To generate gate driver signals from the AFE2256GR:
1. Configure the rising and falling edges of the gate driver signal with register 4Bh to select the timing profile.
This configuration process is similar to programming any other timing signal (IRST, SHR, and so forth).
Ensure that the correct timing profile is programmed and selected. The active-low gate driver signal can be
generated by programming the rise edge to be greater than the falling edge.
2. The gate driver signal can be brought out on the SDOUT pin by configuring the PROBE_SIGNAL bits
(register 5Ah, bits 7-0) = 40h.
8.1.3.1 Generating Gate Driver Signals in Power-Down, Nap Mode
The timing generator (TG) generates the internal timing and gate driver signals using the clock from the PLL.
Thus, if the AFE2256GR is required to generate the gate driver signals in power-down or nap mode, the PLL
must remain active during the power-down or nap mode. Note that MCLK must be running for the gate driver
signal generation.
To enter nap mode with continuous gate driver signal generation, follow the same sequence as described in the
Recommended Nap and Power-Down (PD) Entry, Exit Sequence section. Instead of configuring register 13h with
F8A7h, program register 13h = F827h.
To enter power-down mode with continuous gate driver signal generation, follow the same sequence as
described in the Recommended Nap and Power-Down (PD) Entry, Exit Sequence section. Instead of configuring
register 13h with FFBFh, program register 13h = FD3Fh.

8.2.2.5 Timing Profile Selection
The AFE2256GR provides a dual timing profile structure. Use this feature when an application must change
timings during image capture. For more details, see the Timing Profiles section.
For the dual timing profile, the programming TG operation (see the Programming the TG section) must be
repeated for each timing profile.
Consider the following precautions when using the dual timing profile capture:
• The TP_SEL signal can change states at any time during the capture. Applying SYNC dictates the instance
when the timing profile changes. Thus, timing requirements for the TP_SEL signal are relaxed.
• SYNC can be a periodic signal or a one-shot pulse. If SYNC is periodic, then the duration must be an integral
multiple of 2 × tSCAN (that is, the SYNC pulse can only be applied on an even-line, bank A). The same

restriction applies for a one-shot SYNC pulse as well. If SYNC is applied at any other point, glitches can
result in the PLL-generated clock. These glitches can lead to a bit alignment change.
For a single timing profile, any one timing profile can be programmed.
As discussed in the Timing Profiles section, the device permits programming of the timing profile that is not
being used for data capture. For example, if TPα is used for TG (TP_SEL = 0), then TPβ can be programmed.
Thus, change the TP_SEL state and apply SYNC to activate a programmed timing profile.

9 Power Supply Recommendations
The power-supply design can affect noise and crosstalk performance of the system. Consider the following
recommendations when designing the power supply for an AFE2256GR-based system.
• Parameters such as thermal noise, low-frequency noise, PSRR, and load regulation must be considered
when selecting the low-dropout regulator (LDO). Using a low-noise LDO (such as the TPS7A8300) is
recommended for AVDD1 and AVDD2.
• In order to improve the system power efficiency, a dc-dc converter can be used to drive the LDO. In this case,
the dc-dc converter clock frequency must be an integral multiple of the line rate, otherwise increased noise
can result in the image. When selecting the LDO, consider the LDO PSRR at the dc-dc converter switching
frequency.
• Using LDOs with an output enable and power-good feature is preferable. Use these features for power-supply
sequencing.
10 Layout
10.1 Layout Guidelines
This section discusses the general guidelines when designing the system board.
• The AFE2256GR has two EXTC_I pins. These pins are shorted inside the device and, therefore, board layout
can be simplified by connecting only the one EXTC_I (nearer to EXTC_O) to the EXTC_O pin. EXTC_I must
be directly connected to the EXTC_O pin, which is the output of the internal low-noise buffer.
• Each EXTC_I pin must be bypassed to ground by low-ESR ceramic capacitors. Parallel combinations of
22-μF, 1-μF, and 0.1-μF capacitors near each EXTC_I pin is recommended. Use X5 or X7 capacitors.
• Switching signal traces or vias must not be present near the reference routing. Even COMP signals must be
avoided near VREF because COMP signals contain switching currents.
• Voltage references and digital-to-analog converters (DACs) must be placed away from digital circuits. Placing
these references and DACs in the region of the board with no switching activity is recommended.
• Provide good ground connectivity by using ground planes on multiple layers. Connect all ground planes using
multiple stitching vias; especially where vias on signal traces are placed.
• Each ground pin must directly connect to the AGND (analog ground) plane. Do not share ground vias of
different ground pins.
• Provide a 1-μF bypass capacitor to ground for each supply pin.
• LVDS signals must be routed differentially with a differential impedance of 100 Ω.
• Wherever digital signal traces change the plane, provide a ground via near the signal via to provide the
shortest path for return currents.
• Connecting VTESTC to a test point is recommended and can be used during debug and for future purposes.
