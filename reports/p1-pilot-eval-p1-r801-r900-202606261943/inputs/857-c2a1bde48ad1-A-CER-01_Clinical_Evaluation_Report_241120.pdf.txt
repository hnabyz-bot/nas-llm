1.0 Summary
1.1 Introduction
This clinical evaluation report is executed to show the valid clinical proofs of safety and
performance based medical device. Clinical evaluation report is made in written report form
relevant to safety, performance and using purpose of the product after specifying the product
information, applicable scope of clinical evaluation, sources of clinical evaluation data, evaluating
the clinical data and analyzing the clinical data to get the product information for clinical evaluation.
This report is to evaluate the safety and performance of foreign literature clinical research paper
data then reflected into post production management reports and risk management instructions for
users. The selected main methods and procedures are focused on MEDDEV.2.7.1 Rev.4 (June.
2016) Clinical Evaluation and Council Directive 93/42EEC as amended by Directive 2007/47/EC
concerning medical device Annex X – Clinical evaluation and EN ISO 14971 and other harmonized
standards.
1.2 Device Description
1.2.1 Product name
: Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector
1.2.2 Model name
: HAD1717MG, HAD1717MC
1.2.3 Classification & GMDN code
: Class Ⅱa and 61108 Indirect flat panel x-ray detector
1.2.4 Instruction
This is a wired digital flat panel detector, a faster and more efficient digital radiography system.
The detector is used in combination with a TFT glass and scintillator (CSI, GDX) and supports
automatic trigger signal detection technology that can be used without generator integration.
1.2.5 Intended use
HAD1717MC and HAD1717MG are indicated for digital imaging solution designed for general
radiographic system for human anatomy. It is intended to replace film or screen based radiographic
systems in all general purpose diagnostic procedures. Not to be used for mammography.

3.0 Clinical background, current knowledge, state of the art
Radiography is popular method for presenting the body structure for diagnosis in these days.
Flat panel x-ray detectors are a class of solid-state x-ray digital radiography devices similar in
principle to the image sensors used in digital photography. Flat-panel detectors are more sensitive
and faster than film. Their sensitivity allows a lower dose of radiation for a given picture quality than
film. This gives advantages of immediate image preview and availability.
Indirect detectors use a phosphor (scintillator) material that absorbs X-rays and produces a
proportionate number of light photons that subsequently interact with a photodiode electrode on
the TFT array, to produce the corresponding charge in the detector element capacitor. Phosphors
are either unstructured, such as gadolinium oxysulfide (Gd2O2S), or structured, such as cesium
iodide (CsI). Benefits of the unstructured phosphors are low cost and inert physical characteristics;
however, the classic tradeoff of spatial resolution versus absorption efficiency is a distinct
disadvantage. To achieve good absorption efficiency typically requires a thick phosphor, but to
achieve good spatial resolution requires a thin phosphor, and thus a compromise must be struck
between these two opposite factors. Benefits of the structured phosphor are the ability to confine
the light photons in the needle structure, thus limiting lateral light spread and providing high
resolution, while at the same time being able to deposit a thick phosphor layer, thus achieving good
absorption efficiency. Fragility of the CsI phosphor and slight hygroscopic (water absorbing)
characteristics are disadvantages. Because of good absorption and good resolution, the structured
phosphor is widely employed.
The first commercial solid-state detector-based digital fluoroscopy products became available in
2001. X-ray detector technology has been applied at clinical application for about 20years or more
across the world since it had been developed. So it has a long standing technology with general
performance and safety for medical application. There is no new clinical application of existing
technology or the result of incremental change of an existing technology in this equipment. This
equipment is not a completely new technology. It’s main features and option items are general
function of most x-ray detector. This equipment’s intended use and purpose is same with the other
x-ray detector.
This document is identified the medical fields concerned/relevant medical condition.

Purpose
It is important to optimize digital radiographic technique settings for small animal imaging in
order to maximize image quality while minimizing radiation exposure to personnel. The
purpose of this study was to evaluate two objective methods for determining optimal kVp
values for an indirect flat panel digital detector.
Method
One method considered both image quality and personnel exposure as endpoints and one
considered only image quality. Phantoms simulated veterinary patients of varying thicknesses
with lesions of varying sizes. Phantoms were exposed to a range of kVp values (60, 81, 100,
and 121), using different mAs settings for each phantom. Additionally, all phantoms were
exposed to a standard test exposure of 100 kVp/2.5 mAs. Scattered radiation was recorded
and used as a measure of personnel exposure. When personnel exposure was considered, a
figure of merit was calculated as an endpoint of optimization. The optimal kVp value for each
phantom was determined based on the highest signal difference-to-noise ratio with or without
inclusion of the figure of merit.
Results
When personnel exposure was not considered, increasing kVp resulted in higher signal
difference-to-noise ratios and personnel exposure increased when both patient thickness and
kVp increased.

Findings indicated that a single standard technique of 100 kVp/2.5 mAs was only optimal for
most medium-sized patients. Images of thinner patients should be made with a lower kVp.
Very large patients require a higher kVp than 100 regardless of the optimization method used.
