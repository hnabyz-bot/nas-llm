Amorphous silicon (a-Si:H) flat-panel ~FP! imaging systems have recently become commercially
available for both chest and mammographic imaging applications. It has been shown that this new
detector technology offers better image quality and various operational advantages over the com-
puted radiography ~CR! which to date has been the most widely implemented and used digital
radiography technique. However, most image quality measurements reported on flat-panel systems
have been performed on prototype systems in laboratories while those for CR systems were typi-
cally independently performed and reported on in separate studies. To directly compare the two
technologies, we have measured the image properties for a commercial amorphous silicon/cesium
iodide @a-Si:H/CsI(Tl)# flat-panel based digital chest system and a commercial CR system under
clinical imaging conditions. In this paper, measurements of image quality metrics, including the
modulation transfer functions ~MTFs!, noise power spectra ~NPSs!, and detective quantum efficien-
cies ~DQEs!, for the FP and CR systems are presented and compared. Methods and issues related to
these measurements are discussed. The results show that the flat-panel system has slightly lower
MTF but significantly higher DQEs than the CR system. The DQEs of the flat-panel system were
found to increase with the exposure while those of the CR system decrease slightly with the
exposure. © 2004 American Association of Physicists in Medicine. @DOI: 10.1118/1.1625102#

I. INTRODUCTION
Despite advances in new modalities, projection x-ray imag-
ing remains to be the primary tool for initial diagnosis of
chest diseases. Conventional projection x-ray imaging has
relied on the use of screen/film ~S/F! combinations for x-ray
detection and image recording. Although the quality of S/F
combinations has been improved and optimized over de-
cades, there are several fundamental drawbacks. Intrinsic to
the use of film as the image recording medium is a non-linear
relationship between the film density and base 10 logarithm
of the detector exposure, known as the H and D curve. Be-
cause the curve generally becomes flatter towards the low
and high exposure ends, good image contrast can only be
obtained for a narrow range of exposures. This leads to a
limited useful exposure range and results in a significant rate
of retakes from over- or under-exposures. Another drawback
in quality aspect is the poor compromise between the spatial
resolution and x-ray absorption ability of the S/F combina-
tions. To maintain reasonable spatial resolution, the phosphor
thickness must be kept reasonably thin to contain lateral scat-
tering and spreading of fluorescent light. However, this
would lead to compromised x-ray absorption ability, in-
creased noise fluctuations ~quantum mottles! and thus limited
ability to detect low contrast objects in the S/F images. Due
to the analog nature of S/F imaging, there are also opera-
tional disadvantages in image storage, retrieval, distribution,
and display. Films accumulated over years of operation can
become increasingly difficult and expensive to store, orga-
nize and retrieve. Retrieval and distribution of films are labor
intensive and often lead to loss or misplacement. Although
trans-illuminated film may still be the best image display
technology available ~in terms of spatial resolution and
brightness range!, it is inflexible and cannot adapt to sub-
optimal viewing conditions, objects having too little, too
much contrast, residing in too dark or too bright regions or
are too small in size. The analog form of film image storage
does not have the provision for post-acquisition processing
for image enhancement or analysis.
Various digital radiography techniques have been devel-
oped and investigated over the last two decades in the hope
of improving both the quality and utilization of x-ray image
data.1–22 Due to the rapid advances of digital and computer
technology, the concept of totally digital and filmless radiol-
ogy operation has also become feasible. Thus, the incentives
of finding a high quality yet economic digital image acqui-
sition technique for large-scale implementation of digital ra-
diography have grown high.
The CR technology was first introduced in early 1980s
and has been widely accepted as a viable candidate for large-
scale implementation of digital radiography over past
decades.16 –25 Due to its compatibility with existing x-ray
equipment, the implementation of CR can be economic and
less demanding in terms of initial cost and personnel train-
ing. Its operational advantages have been well recognized

and utilized in areas that can benefit most from more flex-
ibility and convenience in operation but are less demanding
in image quality, e.g., bedside radiography, imaging in inten-
sive care units or emergency rooms.26 –30 Furthermore, the
CR technology has been increasingly integrated with the Pic-
ture Archiving and Communication Systems ~PACS! for
filmless radiology, further increasing its installation base.
However, despite the use of digital image processing and
enhancement to improve the presentation of CR images, the
fundamental quality of CR images has been measured and
perceived to be lower than that of S/F images. Thus, there
has been a more reserved acceptance in applications that de-
mand high image quality, including notably primary chest
imaging and mammographic imaging. This lower image
quality has been attributed to the poorer x-ray absorption
ability and several other characteristics of the storage
phosphors.17,18,31
In the past few years, amorphous silicon flat-panel detec-
tors have become commercially available for primary chest
and mammographic imaging. These detectors either use an
x-ray scintillator plate coupled to an array of photodiodes on
the a-Si plate or employ a selenium plate to directly convert
x-rays into charges. The imaging properties of these two dif-
ferent types of systems have been measured and reported on
by various groups.1,4,6,7,9,32,33 However, most of these mea-
surements have been performed on prototype systems in
laboratory settings rather than commercial systems installed
in clinical environments. Comparison to the widely used CR
systems has been difficult because most reports have focused
on measurements for a specific type of technologies. Since
these measurements were often conducted under different
conditions or using different methodologies, it is generally
difficult to obtain a fair comparison of the FP and CR sys-
tems based on separate studies or reports.
In order to obtain a realistic evaluation and a fair com-
parison of the FP and CR techniques for chest imaging, we
have measured and compared the image properties of a com-
mercial flat-panel system and a commercial CR system using
the same methodology and under nearly identical exposure
conditions. In this paper, the results from these measure-
ments are presented and used to compare the FP and CR
techniques. The methodology and relevant issues for the
measurements are also described and discussed.

~DQE!. The raw image data from the FP system are ampli-

sure at the detector input. To check the linear response of the
FP system, uniform exposure images were acquired at vari-
ous mAs settings while keeping the kVp fixed at 70 or 120.
The mean image signal over a 1003100 region-of-interest
~ROI! at the center of the image was then measured as a
function of the exposure. The mean signals over the ROI
were computed and averaged over four independent mea-

the measured exposure.

35050A, Keithly Instruments, Cleveland, OH!. The measure-

D. Modulation transfer function
The modulation transfer function ~MTF! is often mea-
sured to characterize and quantify the spatial resolution prop-
erties of an image detector system. It is also required for
computing the DQE of an imaging system. The MTFs were
measured by using the tilted slit method. This method has
been widely used to measure the presampling MTFs of digi-
tal imaging systems. The measurement technique and proce-
dure have been described in details by Fujita et al.36 With
this method, an x-ray slit was tilted slightly from the vertical
or horizontal directions. Signal profiles across the slit are
used to estimate the line spread functions ~LSFs! which are
then Fourier transformed to compute the MTFs. However,
LSFs measured along successive lines are sampled with
slightly shifted phases which reflect the relative position of
the slit center with respect to the sampling points. A series of
successive lines can be identified for the slit center to shift
from one sampling point to the next, covering one complete
cycle of sampling phases. Signal profiles along these lines
can then be combined together into a single profile which
covers one cycle of sampling phases and provides a finely
sampled LSF. This LSF can then be used to compute the
so-called presampling MTF as the effective sampling dis-
tance is substantially reduced. Alternatively, the profiles
along these lines can be used to compute MTFs correspond-
ing to different sampling phases. The results can be averaged
to obtain the phase-averaged or expectation MTF ~EMTF!
which has been suggested for use in computation of the
DQEs.37
An x-ray slit camera ~model 07-624, Nuclear Associates,
Carle Place, NY! was employed to measure the LSFs. The
camera has a 10
mm wide, 8 mm long slit ~with 4° relief
angles on each jaw! in 1.5 mm thick tantalum. The slit cam-
era was attached to the input of the CR cassette, or the FP
detector assembly to minimize the image parallax and focal
spot blurring. The slit was placed at the center of the detector
and tilted at a slight angle (;2°) to the vertical or horizontal
directions for measurement of the horizontal or vertical LSFs
and MTFs. X-rays were generated with a technique of 200
and 50 mAs at 70 and 120 kVP , respectively. The small focal
spot ~0.6 –mm! mode was used to reduce focal spot blurring
during image acquisition. To reduce x-ray and optical scatter,
image areas outside the slit were shielded by closing down
the collimator as much as possible and adding additional
attenuators ~lead plates! around the slit. This procedure also
helped prevent the flat-panel detector from being overex-
posed and avoid the unwanted ghost signals which may per-
sist for a long time and appear in images acquired later.
Image data were corrected for spatial variation along the
slit and across the image lines used. The biases of the signal
profile, which might originate from the dark current noise,
transmitted x-rays, x-ray and optical scatter, were estimated
by averaging the signals far away from the slit and subtracted
from the signal profiles. With the slit tilted by ;2°, a com-
plete cycle of sampling phases were found to correspond to
about 25 successive image lines. Signal profiles for these
lines ~already corrected for biases from dark current noise,
transmitted x rays, x-ray and optical scatter! were combined
into a single finely sampled LSF, which was normalized to
have a peak value of 1. To eliminate effects of scattered
radiation, the signal profile below 1% of the maximum signal
value was cut and replaced with an exponential extrapolation
extended to 0.001% signal level.37 The corrected fine
sampled LSF was then Fourier transformed, and the modulus
of the transform was divided by the sinc function for the slit

Image blurring in CR imaging occurs mainly in the read-
out process. As the laser beam penetrates and stimulates the
trapped electrons in the storage phosphor layer of the ex-
posed CR imaging plate, it spreads laterally and leads to
readout of signals from neighboring pixels. Image blurring in
the CsI~Tl! layer of the FP detector is caused by lateral
spreading of the fluorescent light converted from absorbed x-
rays. This appears to be similar to the blurring process in
conventional screen/film imaging. However, the CsI~Tl!
layer used in a FP detector was fabricated with needle struc-
ture ~as is the case with the one studied in this paper! which
helps channel the fluorescent light to the photodiodes with
reduced lateral spreading.34 Combining this feature with the
intrinsically better absorption properties of CsI~Tl!, a better
compromise between spatial resolution and x-ray absorption
was achieved as compared to that for the CR system. How-
ever, this can be used to improve the image quality in differ-
ent ways by varying the scintillator thickness in designing
the FP detector. Much improved MTFs can be achieved by
selecting a thinner CsI~Tl! layer while maintaining the same
x-ray absorption ability of the CR imaging plate. Alterna-
tively, significantly improved x-ray absorption can be
achieved by choosing a thicker scintillator but maintaining
the same MTF. The FP detector studied in this paper appar-
ently takes advantage of the needle structure of CsI~Tl! by
going for significantly improved x-ray absorption while
maintaining an MTF similar to that of the CR system.
The higher kVP dependence of the MTFs with the CR
system may be attributed to the difference in the way images
are formed with the two detectors. With the CR system, x-ray
information is stored as the density of trapped electrons with
little blurring. During readout, the trapped electrons are ex-
cited resulting emission of photons and some of which es-
cape the phosphor are then collected and converted into elec-
tronic signals by multiple photomultiplier tubes ~PMTs!. Due
to the use of laser scanning readout method, scattering of the
light emitted from stimulated trapped electrons does not
cause blurring. The major factors that contribute to image
blurring are the laser spot size and lateral spread of the laser
beam inside the storage phosphor. The latter results in an
effect highly dependent upon the depth of the trapped elec-
trons stimulated. The deeper they are, the more the laser
beam spread and the greater the blurring effect is. Thus, the
x-ray kVP would have an effect on the overall blurring. At
higher kVP ’s, the distribution of absorbed x-rays tend to be

C. Noise power spectra
Figures 3~a! and 3~b! illustrate the 2D NPSs for the FP
and CR systems measured at 120 kVp with ;1 mR expo-
sure. The plots present different noise spectra patterns for
two detector systems. To study the noise spectra behaviors,
the one-dimensional normalized NPSs of the FP system in
horizontal direction are plotted as a function of the spatial
frequency for 70 kVp and 120 kVp in Figs. 4~a! and 4~b!,
respectively. The normalized NPSs of the CR system in hori-
zontal direction are plotted for 70 kVp and 120 kVp in Figs.
5~a! and 5~b!, respectively. Notice that the normalized NPSs
are NPSs divided by the mean signal squared. Thus the plots
essentially show the noise ratio squared for various spatial
frequencies and exposures for signals detected in a unit area
of 1 mm2 . NPSs for both FP and CR systems decreased with
the exposure. This is expected as the relative quantum noise
would decrease with higher x-ray fluence. NPSs for both FP
and CR systems also decreased with the spatial frequency.
However, those for the FP system decreased with the spatial
frequency at a significantly faster rate. Since the NPSs were
measured for two different sets of exposures at 70 and 120
kVp, it is hard to directly compare the two systems as to how
the NPS varied with the exposure or the kVP in Figs. 4 and 5.
In Fig. 6, the NPSs are plotted as a function of the exposure
for the two different detector systems and two different
kV P’s for ~a! 0.5 cycles/mm, ~b! 1.5 cycles/mm, and ~c! 2.5
cycles/mm in horizontal direction, and ~d! 1.5 cycles/mm in
vertical direction. It becomes apparent with Figs. 6~a!– 6~d!
that the normalized NPSs at 120 kVP are slightly higher than
those at 70 kVP for both FP and CR systems. Furthermore,
the normalized NPSs for the CR system are significantly
higher than those for the FP system. The difference increases
from a factor of 2.88 at 1.3431027 C/kg ~0.052 mR! to a
factor of 7.01 at 1.7631027 C/kg ~6.84 mR! at 1.5
cycles/mm in horizontal direction for 70 kVP and is expected
to be even greater at higher frequencies. The NPSs remain
largely unchanged for FP system in both horizontal and ver-
tical directions. The NPSs in vertical direction are slightly
higher than that in horizontal direction for CR system.
Figure 6 shows that the normalized noise power spectra
(NPS/S 2 ) are approximately inversely proportional to the ex-
posure at the detector input as indicated by the good linear fit
and the slope of the fitted lines for the data points. This is
expected for a quantum limited system as the noise variation
of the signals should be proportional to the signal in quantum
limited imaging situations. It should be noted, however, that
this is also contributed by the use of subtracted image data
for NPS/S2 measurements as the original image may contain
a structure noise component whose variance may increase
with the exposure or signal level squared rather than the
exposure or signal themselves. The effects of the structure
noises were demonstrated by NPS/S2 measurements using
un-subtracted images. The results of such measurements are
shown in Fig. 7. With these measurements, the values of
NPS/S2 tended to decrease with the exposure at a slower rate
and were higher than those obtained with subtracted images.
The differences increased with the exposure level indicating
that the structure noise is limited at higher exposure levels.
Two subtraction methods may be used for NPS/S2 mea-
surements. One is to subtract two identically exposed images
to cancel out effects of structure noises. With this method,
the noise variances in the two images are identical and su-
perimposed in the subtracted images. Thus, the noise vari-
ance computed in the subtracted image must be divided by 2
to obtain the random noise component in the unsubtracted
image. The other method is to average multiple identically
exposed images to smooth out the random noise component
leaving just the structure noise. This average image can then
be subtracted from each single image to cancel out the struc-
ture noise without introducing additional noise.1,32 Thus, the
noise computed in the subtracted image was directly used to
estimate the random noise component in the unsubtracted
image. We have used both methods to measure the NPS/S2
and the results are largely the same within linear response
range of the detector system.

D. Detective quantum efficiency
The DQEs of the FP system in horizontal direction are
plotted as a function of the spatial frequency for various
exposure levels at 70 and 120 kVP in Figs. 8~a! and 8~b!,
respectively. Similarly, the DQEs of the CR system in hori-
zontal direction are plotted for various exposure levels at 70
and 120 kVP in Figs. 9~a! and 9~b!, respectively. The DQEs
of the FP system increased with the exposure level while
those of the CR system had a slight tendency to decrease
with the exposure level. This exposure dependence is also
shown in Fig. 10 where the DQE is plotted as a function of
the exposure for the two different detector systems and two
different kVP ’s for ~a! 0.5 cycles/mm, ~b! 1.5 cycles/mm,
and ~c! 2.5 cycles/mm in horizontal direction, and ~d! 1.5
cycles/mm in vertical direction for comparison. The plots
show that DQEs of the FP system were higher by at least a
factor of 2 than those of the CR system within the clinical
exposure range (,1 mR). The difference was even greater
as the exposure increased beyond this range.
The higher DQEs of the flat-panel system studied are ex-
pected as it is well known that the needle structure CsI scin-
tillator, similar to those used in the image intensifier, is ca-
pable of achieving more efficient x-ray absorption than the
x-ray phosphor screens ~for general radiography! while
maintaining similar spatial resolution. The different patterns
of exposure dependence of the two detectors may be attrib-
uted to their different noise properties. The FP detector is
subject to a fixed noise which originates from the image
readout process. Thus, the higher the exposure level, the less
the image SNR would be degraded by this noise. Despite this
degradation, the flat-panel detector studied still outperformed
the CR detector by a factor of 2 or greater for the exposure
range studied. It has often been suggested that the most flat-
panel detectors to date may have unusable DQEs at substan-
tially lowered exposure levels, e.g., those typical of fluoro-
scopic applications. However, our exposures were not low
enough to confirm these suggestions. The CR readout sys-
tem, on the other hand, has an adjustable gain control
~through varying the photomultiplier tube voltage and the
electron multiplication gain! prior to image signal readout.
Thus, the image SNR would be much less affected by the
exposure level and does not decrease with the exposure level.

F. Comparison with previously reported
measurements
MTFs, NPSs, and DQEs of both the Fuji CR and GE FP
systems have been measured and reported by several other
research groups.1,20–22,32,33,39 However, these measurements,
especially those for the FP systems, have generally shown
some disagreement with each other. These disagreements
may be attributed to the differences in the methodology, ex-
perimental setup and exposure factors used in these measure-
ments, including but not limited to the kVP , filtration, alu-
minum HVL, estimated photon fluence, and methods to
flatten the noise images in measuring the NPS.
Our CR measurements agree well with those by Flynn
et al.21,22 Measurements on the GE XQ/i FP detector have
been previously reported by Granfors, Floyd, and
Samei.1,32,33,39 Measurements by these groups and us were
made for different ranges of exposures. Performed at only
one exposure, 0.321 mR, the DQE values measured by Floyd
were very close to those by Granfors. Measurements by
Granfors and us were both made over a wide range of expo-
sures: 0.058 to 8.30 mR and 0.058 to 6.84 mR, respectively.
Our measured DQE values showed the same trend of in-
creasing with the exposure level as those by Granfors. This is
expected as both measurements employed the subtraction
method, although two different versions. However, our val-
ues were significantly lower for all exposure levels and fre-
quencies, except the one made at highest exposure, 6.84 mR,
at zero frequency. Since our estimated photon fluence was
very close to that by Granfors, this difference in DQE values
may be attributed to the higher normalized NPS values we
measured. At higher frequencies, our measured normalized
NPS values were less different from those by Granfors.
However, our measured MTF values were significantly lower
than those by Granfors, thus still keeping our measured DQE
values significantly lower than those by Granfors.
Measurements by Samei covered a smaller range of expo-
sures: 0.27, 1.19, and 4.9 mR. Their measured DQE values
showed the opposite trend as they decreased, rather than in-
creased as with those by Granfors and us, with the exposure
levels. This may reflect the fact that Samei’s measurements
have employed the nonsubtraction method for removing the
non-noise signal background. Although Floyd has demon-
strated that the subtraction and nonsubtraction methods re-
sulted in similar DQE measurements, the measurements
were performed at a medium exposure level, 0.321 mR, at

IV. CONCLUSIONS
We have measured the image properties of a commercial
flat-panel digital chest radiography system and compared
them with those of a CR system. The results show that the FP
system has slightly lower MTF but significantly higher
DQEs than the CR system. The NPSs of the FP system were
found to be generally lower than those of the CR system and
decrease at a faster rate with the spatial frequency. The DQEs
of the FP system were found to be significantly higher than
those of the CR system. They were found to increase with
the exposure while those of the CR system decrease slightly
with the exposure.
