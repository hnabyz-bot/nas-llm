Optimisation of direct digital chest radiography using Cu filtration

Purpose: To assess the impact of additional Cu filtration with the DRX-1 detector in optimisation of adult
chest radiography.
Methods: A simulated anatomical phantom was imaged at different levels of Cu filtration (0 mm, 0.1 mm,
0.3 mm and 0.5 mm of Cu) at 120 kVp. Image quality was assessed using exposure index, conspicuity
index, signal-to-noise ratio and pixel density. Dose area product (DAP) meter was used to measure dose;
effective dose and risk of exposure induced cancer death (REID) were estimated using the PCXMC
software 2.0.
Results: Exposure index increased with increasing Cu thickness; 0.3 mm Cu filtration increased
conspicuity index of simulated nodules, with a minimal decrease in signal-to-noise ratio, and pro-
nounced decrease in pixel density. Patient doses were reduced by 20.7%, 36.6%, and 39.79% at 0.1 mm,
0.3 mm, and 0.5 mm levels of Cu filtration respectively.
Conclusion: Additional beam filtration using 0.3 mm Cu filter with the DXR-1 detector reduced patient
doses by 37% without decline in image quality.

Introduction
A chest X-ray (CXR) is the most common and cost-effective
radiographic examination,1,2 and the first imaging pathway for
the detection of pulmonary, cardiovascular and chest skeletal ab-
normalities.3,4 In some populations, CXR constitutes one third of all
X-ray referrals, and accounts for 18% of the collective dose.1,5 The
absorbed dose has potential to cause somatic and genetic changes
to biological molecules. 6 Over the past two decades, there has been
a reduction in patient dose for all types of radiographic examina-
tions except plain chest radiography; this in part is due to the
anatomical noise caused by superimposition of thoracic structures,
and their differential attenuation of the X-ray beam which impact
on image quality. 7,8 Another trend is that film-screen radiography is
gradually being replaced by computed radiography and direct
digital radiography.3,9 The advantages of going digital are many and
include more efficient data management from acquisition through
storage to display; the high detective quantum efficiency of digital
detectors, wide dynamic range of the display components, and
post-processing adjustment of image contrast and brightness also
offers increased image quality and diagnostic performance.4,9,10 In
spite of these technological advancements, the radiation dose from
these systems is still relatively high9 ; it therefore becomes neces-
sary to reduce dose in digital systems whilst maintaining image
quality. 9e11
To minimize the detrimental effects of ionizing radiation in
diagnostic digital chest radiography, it is necessary to optimise the
imaging procedure. 6,11 Optimisation involves striking a balance
between radiation dose and image quality,10e12 and this can be
achieved through beam filtration.12e14 Filtration hardens the X-ray
beam and gives it a higher half value layer (HVL) by removing the
lower energy photons out of the spectrum using the k-edge ab-
sorption principles. 9,11,15 X-ray tubes have inherent filtration, which
does not completely attenuate the low energy radiation. To increase
the efficacy of the filtration process, different filter materials or a
combination of materials are often used to provide additional
filtration.14,16
In order to determine the influence of filters on radiation dose
and image quality of chest radiographs, simulation studies with
phantoms are needed. Although phantoms do not account for the
standard variation that occurs between patients and the presence
or absence of disease, they allow for consistency and reproduc-
ibility of assumed organ composition and avoids subjecting
humans to direct radiation risk.17 The style N1 “Lungman”
anthropomorphic chest phantom is optimal for this purpose; its

sought to assess the impact of additional Cu filtration with the DRX-

phantoms.
Methods
The CARESTREAM DXR-1 35  43 cm cassette having a wireless
amorphous silicon caesium iodide (aSi:CsI) detector was used for
image acquisition. The cassette was inserted in a bucky containing a
72 lp/cm 14:1 grid mounted on a wall stand. Exposures were made
with an X-ray tube having a focal spot size of 1.2 mm and a tube
potential of 125 kVp; the field size was kept constant for all ex-
posures. Inherent filtration was equivalent to 2.5 mm of
Aluminium, and automatic exposure control was used to set mAs
values so that the dose to the detector was kept constant for all
exposures. A dose-area product (DAP) meter was attached to the
window of the X-ray tube to measure the dose at the radiation field
and the tissue area irradiated. Cu filters of varying thicknesses
(0.1 mm, 0.3 mm, and 0.5 mm) were used to provide added filtra-
tion; these Cu filters were placed on the tube side of the dose-area
chamber in turn.
The style N1 “Lungman” anthropomorphic anatomical chest
phantom was used for this study (Fig. 1a & b). The phantom
contained simulated pulmonary nodules (PNs) (0.8 mm, 1 mm
and 1.2 mm) in the lungs at different positions: 1.2 mm nodule in
the hilum of the left lung, and 0.8 mm and 1 mm in the lower
and upper right lung lobes respectively. The phantom was posi-
tioned against the erect bucky mounted on a wall stand, and
radiographed multiple times at four different levels of beam
filtration (0 mm, 0.1 mm, 0.3 mm and 0.5 mm of Cu); for each
level of filtration, exposures were made five times. The source-
to-image distance (SID) was 180 cm and kVp was kept constant
at 120 kVp.
To assess image quality of all images at each level of Cu filtration,
two regions of interest (0.8 mm and 1.2 mm PNs) were chosen;
these nodules were chosen because hilar and lower lobe nodules
are most pathognomonic of active pulmonary disease.20 Addi-
tionally, the selected nodules covered the range of nodule sizes
available in the phantom. The conspicuity index of each PN at
different levels of Cu filtration was evaluated by measuring the
change in their conspicuity using Image J software as describe by
Manning et al. 21 ; signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and pixel density (PD)
of the two pulmonary nodules were measured using the CARE-
STREAM PACS software. Exposure index was assessed from the
direct readout from the DXR-1 detector to assess whether the noise
level is acceptable.
Patient dose was assessed from the dose area product (DAP)
measured by the DAP meter attached to the window of the X-ray
tube. Effective dose and risk of exposure induced cancer death
(REID) were calculated using the PCXMC software 2.0. Effective
dose and risk of exposure induced cancer death (REID) reductions
were assessed by comparing the results to the International Com-
mission for Radiological Protection (ICRP) 103 standard in the
PCXMC software 2.0.

measures of dose and mean pixel density at different thicknesses of
Cu filtration. The Bonferroni post-hoc test was used to determine
whether any level of Cu was statistically associated with dose, pixel
density, and exposure index.22 Student's paired t-test was used to

Results
The exposure index increased with increasing Cu thickness:
1395.7, 1405.3, 1512.3, and 1435.7 for 0 mm, 0.1 mm, and 0.3 mm
and 0.5 mm Cu thicknesses respectively (Fig. 2). The conspicuity
index of the 0.8 mm pulmonary nodule was lower than that of the
1.2 mm nodule. Compared to 0.0 mm Cu filtration, the conspicuity
index of the 1.2 nodule increased by about 2.6% at 0.1 mm Cu, and
4.5% at 0.3 mm Cu filtrations; it then decreased by 32% at 0.5 mm Cu
filtration; conspicuity index of the 0.8 mm nodule increased by 2.3%
at 0.1 mm, and 1% at 0.3 mm levels of Cu filtration but decreased by
27.1% at 0.5 mm Cu filtration (Fig. 3).
Signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and pixel density measured at the
region of interest decreased with increasing thickness of Cu filtra-
tion. SNR measured at the region of the 0.1 mm nodule decreased
by 3% and 6% at 0.1 mm Cu and 0.3 mm Cu filtration settings
respectively. Similarly, the SNR measured at the region of the
1.2 mm nodule decreased by 2% and 3% at 0.1 mm Cu and 0.3 mm
Cu filtration settings respectively (Fig. 4). When comparing pixel
density (PD) of the PNs at different levels Cu filtration to 0.0 mm Cu
filtration, the pixel density measured at the region of the 0.8 mm
nodule decreased from 0.524 to 0.032 at 0.1 mm Cu and 0.3 mm Cu
filtration settings respectively; PD measured at the region of the
1.2 mm nodule decreased from 0.265 to 0.258 at corresponding
levels of Cu filtration (Table 1).
There was decreasing radiation dose trend due to added Cu
filtration: 0.1 mm of Cu decreased dose by 20.7%; 0.3 mm of Cu
decreased dose by 36.6%; 0.5 mm of Cu decreased by 39.79% (Fig. 5).
In comparison to the ICRP standard, a 5.8% decrease in effective
dose (E) was observed with 0.3 mm Cu filtration, however there
was a steady dose increase at 0.5 mm (Fig. 6). The risk of radiation
exposure induced cancer death (REID) per 10,000,000 people
decreased from 5.02 to 4.9 for males and 10.02 to 9.02 for females at
0.3 mm Cu filtration (Fig. 7).

best for adult chest radiography. In addition, the amount of dose
reduction and image quality possible is influenced by the detective
quantum efficiency (DQE) of the image receptor. The wireless
CARESTREAM DXR-1 system was recently introduced into medical
practice; it is necessary to assess the impact of added filtration with
this system in optimisation.
The results show that exposure index (EI) increased with addi-
tional Cu filtration (Fig. 2). Exposure index gives an indication of the
exposure received by the image receptor, and is influenced by many
factors including thickness of the part traversed by the beam,
exposure parameters and the detective quantum efficiency (QDE)
of the detector. EI is used to assess whether the noise level is
acceptable. 23 For film-screen radiography, the noise level can be
assessed from the density on the radiograph. Since digital systems
have wide dynamic range and allow post-processing image ad-
justments, there is a tendency to increase exposure which increases
patient dose without the noise levels being exceeded.1,23 Therefore,
to assess the impact of added filtration on image quality of digital
systems, it is better to use conspicuity index, signal-to-noise ratio,
and pixel density instead of EI.
Additional beam filtration with Cu influenced lesion conspi-
cuity (Fig. 3). Two pulmonary nodules were chosen for this study:
1.2 mm and 0.8 mm nodules in the hilum of the left lung and
lower lobe of the right lung field respectively. The 1.2 mm nodule
had a higher conspicuity index than the 0.8 mm nodule. In com-
parison to absence of Cu filtration, the conspicuity index of the 1.2
nodule increased by about 4.5% at 0.3 mm thickness of Cu filtra-
tion but decreased by 32% at 0.5 mm filtration; that of the 0.8 mm
nodule increased by 1% at 0.3 mm Cu but decreased by 27% at
0.5 mm Cu filtration (Fig. 3). The conspicuity of a lesion is central
to correct radiodiagnosis; it refers to the prominent appearance of
a lesion in its surrounding, and is influenced by a number of fac-
tors including image quality. Simulation of pulmonary nodules
(PNs) is a good way of evaluating the impact of filtration on image
quality since they are very difficult to detect on CXR,4,24 therefore
a 4.5% increase in conspicuity index of these nodules with addi-
tional 0.3 mm Cu filtration suggests an improvement in image
contrast.
There was a slight decrease in signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) at the
region of interest with increasing filtration (increased Cu thick-
ness). SNR is the ratio of the signal intensity at the region of interest
(ROI) and the standard deviation of the signal intensity in a region
outside the lesion being imaged. SNR integrates contrast, spatial
resolution and noise, which are the components of image quality;
the higher the SNR, better the image quality.1 The difference in
mean SNR (0.03) for all nodules between 0 mm and 3 mm Cu
filtration was not pronounced (Fig. 4), signifying almost no decline
in image quality with 0.3 mm additional filtration. Similarly, pair-
wise comparison analysis of pixel density of the PNs at 0.0 mm Cu
versus higher levels of Cu filtration demonstrated that the mean
pixel density of the 0.8 mm nodule decreased from 0.524 at 0.1 mm
Cu to 0.32 at 0.3 mm Cu filtration; pixel density of the 1.2 mm
nodule decreased from 0.265 to 0.258 at 0.1 mm and 0.3 mm Cu
filtrations respectively (Table 1). Reduced pixel density implies
larger pixel sizes which are more responsive to radiation. Therefore,
the DXR-1 detector used is more responsive to X-ray photons and
holds more information from the transmitted X-ray beam at higher
levels of Cu filtration.
A substantial reduction in radiation dose with increasing levels
of Cu filtration was observed; 0.1 mm Cu reduced dose by 20.7% and
0.3 mm and 0.5 mm Cu reduced dose by 36.6% and 39.8% respec-
tively (Fig. 5). The difference in dose reduction for 0.5 mm Cu
compared the 0.3 mm filter was very minimal (36.6% vs. 39.8%). The
37% reduction in radiation dose and non-decline in image quality
with additional 0.3 mm of Cu suggest that this level of Cu filtration
is the optimum filter thickness feasible to balance radiation dose
and image quality for chest radiography using the DXR-1 system. To
evaluate the impact of Cu filtration on dose reduction with this
system in clinical practice, the results of this study were compared
to the ICRP 103 standard in the PCXMC 2.0 software and a 5.8%
decrease in effective dose (E) with additional 0.3 mm Cu filtration
was observed (Fig. 6); similarly, the risk of exposure induced cancer
death per 10,000,000 people decreased from 5.02 to 4.9 for males,
and 10.02 to 9.02 for females at the same level of Cu filtration
(Fig. 7).
Other studies have demonstrated comparable dose reductions
with and without decline in image quality at varying thicknesses of
Cu filtration.16,19,25,26 Smans et al.19 reported optimum balance
between image quality and radiation dose (25% reduction in pae-
diatrics lung doses) for paediatric chest radiography at 60 kVp with
additional Cu filtration. Hammer et al. 26 reported a 30% dose
reduction at 0.3 mm Cu filtration with caesium iodide-amorphous
silicon detector without decline in image quality. Cu filters of
0.1 mm, and 0.3 mm have also been reported to reduce adult
antero-posterior chest X-ray doses by 25e44% at varying kVp

values but with significant decline in image quality.16
The CARESTREAM DXR-1 system offers an easy conversion from
conventional to digital radiography. With the wide dynamic range
and image post-processing improvement in image quality of digital
systems, the major concern in optimising the imaging procedure
with the DXR-1 system is the radiation dose to the patient. Added
beam filtration with 0.3 mm thickness of Cu on DXR-1 system has
shown a 37% reduction in dose and 5.8% lower effective dose
compared to the ICRP 103 standard without decline in image
quality.

Conclusion
A 37% reduction in patient doses is feasible with additional
0.3 mm Cu beam filtration on the DXR-1 cassette; this provides an
optimum balance between image quality and radiation dose for
plain chest radiography at 120 kVp.
