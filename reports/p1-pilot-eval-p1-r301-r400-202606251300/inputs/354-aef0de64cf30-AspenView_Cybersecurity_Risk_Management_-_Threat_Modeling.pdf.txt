2. Comprehensive Threat Modeling
2.1 Introduction
• AspenView is a medical software application that interfaces with an X-ray detector
through on-board Wi-Fi to display images for diagnostic purposes.
• Purpose of the Document: This document aims to identify and analyze potential
cybersecurity risks associated with the AspenView system throughout its lifecycle,
providing pre- and post-mitigation risk assessments.
2.2 System Description
2.2.1 Components
o AspenView Software: Application used by healthcare providers.
o X-ray Detector: Captures images and communicates with AspenView.
o Wi-Fi Module: Facilitates wireless data transfer between the detector and
software.

2.3 Assumptions
• Environmental Assumptions: Assume that hospital networks are hostile environments
where adversaries may control network traffic, enabling them to alter, drop, and replay
packets.
• System Assumptions: AspenView and its components are assumed to be targeted by
threats due to the sensitivity of the data and the critical nature of medical diagnostics.
2.4 Threat Identification and Methodology
• Threat Methodology: The STRIDE methodology is selected for its comprehensiveness
in identifying Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of
Service, and Elevation of Privilege, which are relevant to the AspenView system's
security needs.

2.5 Identification of Cybersecurity Risks
• Supply Chain Risks
o Counterfeit Components: The use of counterfeit hardware components in the X-
ray detector could lead to vulnerabilities that are hard to detect and mitigate.
o Compromised Software Supply: Malicious or buggy code could be introduced
into the software through third-party libraries or dependencies used within
AspenView.
o Mitigations:
▪ Conduct audits and regular security assessments of all suppliers.
▪ Implement a secure software development lifecycle (SDLC) that
includes vetting of third-party components.
▪ Use cryptographic signatures to verify software integrity at installation.
• Manufacturing and Deployment Risks
o Insecure Default Configurations: Devices shipped with insecure defaults (e.g.,
default passwords, open ports) can be easily exploited.
o Physical Tampering: Devices could be physically tampered with during
assembly or shipping, including the insertion of malicious components or
modification of firmware.
o Mitigations:
▪ Ensure secure configuration guides are followed during installation.
▪ Perform security checks and audits at various stages of the
manufacturing and deployment process.
• Maintenance and Update Risks
o Update Integrity: Updates might be intercepted or replaced with malicious
versions if not properly secured.
o Unauthorized Access: During maintenance, systems might be temporarily less
secure and more susceptible to unauthorized access.
o Mitigations:
▪ Use secure, encrypted channels for transmitting updates.
