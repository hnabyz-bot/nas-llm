EVALUATION OF TWO OBJECTIVE METHODS TO OPTIMIZE KVP AND
PERSONNEL EXPOSURE USING A DIGITAL INDIRECT FLAT PANEL
DETECTOR AND SIMULATED VETERINARY PATIENTS
C HRISTINA C OPPLE, IAN D. ROBERTSON, D ONALD E. THRALL, EHSAN S AMEI
It is important to optimize digital radiographic technique settings for small animal imaging in order to maximize
image quality while minimizing radiation exposure to personnel. The purpose of this study was to evaluate two
objective methods for determining optimal kVp values for an indirect flat panel digital detector. One method
considered both image quality and personnel exposure as endpoints and one considered only image quality.
Phantoms simulated veterinary patients of varying thicknesses with lesions of varying sizes. Phantoms were
exposed to a range of kVp values (60, 81, 100, and 121), using different mAs settings for each phantom.
Additionally, all phantoms were exposed to a standard test exposure of 100 kVp/2.5 mAs. Scattered radiation
was recorded and used as a measure of personnel exposure. When personnel exposure was considered, a figure of
merit was calculated as an endpoint of optimization. The optimal kVp value for each phantom was determined
based on the highest signal difference-to-noise ratio with or without inclusion of the figure of merit. When
personnel exposure was not considered, increasing kVp resulted in higher signal difference-to-noise ratios and
personnel exposure increased when both patient thickness and kVp increased. Findings indicated that a single
standard technique of 100 kVp/2.5 mAs was only optimal for most medium-sized patients. Images of thinner
patients should be made with a lower kVp. Very large patients require a higher kVp than 100 regardless of the
optimization method used. Personnel exposure from optimized techniques was low and not expected to exceed
annual occupational dose limits. C© 2012 Veterinary Radiology & Ultrasound.

animal digital radiography, personnel exposure.
Introduction

common in veterinary practice and there is a grow-
ing need for more objective methods to optimize digital
radiographic exposure factors. The objective endpoints for
optimization should ideally incorporate maximization of
image quality as well as minimization of personnel expo-
sure. The spectral sensitivity 1 and dynamic range 2–4 of dig-
ital radiographic systems allow a wide range of kVp and
mAs values to produce a diagnostic quality image. A unique
characteristic of digital imaging is that, unlike film-screen
radiography, the elements of image capture and display are
separate entities. This feature of digital radiography results

For digital images, contrast is no longer the primary con-
tributor to image quality as postprocessing techniques can
be used to modify the image, allowing radiologists to view
the image at any level of preferred contrast. Instead of con-
trast, the signal difference-to-noise ratio is accepted as a
more relevant, objective, measure of digital image quality.
A digital radiographic image is therefore optimized when
the signal difference-to-noise ratio is highest. In human
digital imaging, a figure of merit has been used as an objec-
tive endpoint for radiographic technique optimization and
quantified as the signal difference-to-noise ratio squared
per unit exposure.1 A recent review of unique characteris-
tics of digital imaging systems highlighted the importance
of applying objective measures for optimizing techniques in
pediatric and adult radiography.5 After evaluating exposure
indices for computed radiographic imaging of the human
thorax and lumbar spine, authors concluded that radio-
graphic technique settings could be decreased while main-
taining diagnostic quality and that these findings could be
used to improve ALARA compliance.8
An optimal exposure technique is one that produces a
high quality image while at the same time reducing possible
personnel exposure as much as possible. We hypothesized
that a single kVp/mAs combination, termed the standard
technique, would not be optimal for all veterinary patients,
especially when personnel exposure was taken into consid-
eration. Objectives of this study were to (1) calculate signal
difference-to-noise ratio (Sd NR) and figure of merit values
for varying exposure techniques and varying patient thick-
nesses using an indirect flat panel detector radiographic
system; (2) apply the signal difference-to-noise ratio and
figure of merit values as objective methods for optimizing
kVp settings; and (3) compare personnel exposure resulting
from optimized techniques to personnel exposure resulting
from the standard technique for each patient thickness.
Materials and Methods
All images were acquired using a calibrated x-ray system.
Prior to data collection, a radiation safety officer inspected
the system to ensure that the output of the x-ray tube was
linear with acceptable variability at all test kVp values. The
detector used in this study was a solid-state, amorphous
silicon, indirect, flat panel detector∗. Specifications for the
∗CXDI-50G, Canon, USA, Inc., Lake Success, NY.
detector are: sensor, LANMIT 4; scintillator, Gadolinium
Oxysulfide (Gd2 O2 S: Tb); image size, 35.56 × 43.18 cm
(14 × 17 inches); A/D, 14-bit; pixel pitch, 160 × 160 mi-
crons; pixels, 2208 × 2688; grayscale, 4096 (12-bit).9 The
flat panel detector was calibrated, without a grid, accord-
ing to manufacturer guidelines, within 24 h of exposures.
This ensured that proper gain and bad pixel corrections
had been applied, thereby confirming minimal noise con-
tribution from the detector.
All image analyses were performed by the first author,
using raw image data and public-domain software†. Nu-
merical data resulting from image analyses were imported
into a commercial software program‡ for calculations and
creation of graphs and tables.
The kinetics of exposure response for the detector was
measured by exposing the detector to incrementally in-
creased mAs values, at a fixed kVp, without a grid and
without an object on the surface of the detector. The mAs
values ranged from 0.5 to 12.5. A kVp of 81 was chosen
because it was the most common setting the detector plate
was exposed to on a regular basis at our institution. Expo-
sure was measured with a dosimeter (Triad TnT Dosimeter,
Model 35050AT), using a 15cc ion chamber§. All exposure
readings were in milliroentgen (mR) and then converted to
coulombs/kilogram (C/kg); all calculations of this experi-
ment incorporate the measured exposure in milliroentgen.
The source-to-detector distance was 158.1 cm for all expo-
sures. To ensure minimal contribution from backscattered
radiation, the distance from the dosimeter to the detector
was 25.4 cm, with the ion chamber placed in a plane paral-
lel to the direction of the heel effect. Incident exposure on
the detector was calculated for each mAs value at 81 kVp,
using the inverse square law and the following equation:
E i = E m × (SMD2/SID2 ) (1)
where Ei represents the incident exposure on the detector,
E m represents the measured exposure to the detector by
the dosimeter, SMD equals the distance from the dosime-
ter to the detector, and SID equals the source-to-detector
distance.
Following calculation of incident exposure, each detector
image for each mAs setting was analyzed to determine the
mean pixel intensity of a background region of interest.
The background region of interest was a circle, equivalent
to the shape of the lesions used later on in the study. This
circular region of interest was placed in the center of the
image. The size and location of the region of interest was
identical for every detector image. Log-mean pixel intensity
was plotted as a function of incident exposure. The slope

Lesion phantom thicknesses were selected for each back-

approximately 10%. This targeted contrast value was cho-
sen in order to achieve a perception level, based on the
Rose Model, which would be 2–3 times beyond the con-
trast threshold of the human visual system. This level of
contrast would also be expected to increase the certainty
of seeing a low contrast object, such as a soft tissue lesion,
despite the presence of noise.10
A series of pilot test images were first acquired with 1-mm
thick lesions across all phantom thicknesses and kVps. The
lesion contrast was calculated using the following formula:
C E = (PB − P L )/m (2)
where CE equals the contrast value, P B equals the mean
background phantom pixel intensity, P L represents the
combined mean background plus mean lesion pixel inten-
sity, and m is the slope of the detector’s response to expo-
sure. (Equation 2 is a direct derivation from the logarithmic
response of the detector, based on the Taylor Expansion
method.) From these measurements, the lesion thickness to
achieve the nominal target contrast of 10% was determined
by linear extrapolation.
Only soft tissue lesion equivalent phantoms were used
in this study because soft tissue lesions have inherently low
subject contrast, making them more difficult to see. Table 2
lists the background phantoms and corresponding soft tis-

To optimize exposure factors for patients, patient
phantoms were exposed to the test standard technique of
100 kVp and 2.5 mAs 6 as well as a test range of kVp values
with clinically relevant corresponding mAs values unique
to a specific patient thickness (Table 2). The test kVp
values were 60, 81, 100, and 121. Based on previous work,
a single exposure method was used to calculate signal
difference.1 Each patient phantom was exposed to the
test standard technique and the test variable kVp values
a total of 10 times. This repetition was done to minimize
effects of x-ray tube variance in final calculations and to

allow quantification of interexposure repeatability. Signal
difference-to-noise ratio was calculated using the following
formula 1 :
S d NR = (PB − P L )/σB (3)
where PB and P L are the pixel intensities of a region of
interest, or signal, in the background phantom that did
not (P B ) and did (PL ) include the lesion phantom. σ B
was the standard deviation of the background, or noise.
The calculated signal difference-to-noise ratio was used
as an objective measure of image quality. The kVp that
produced the highest signal difference-to-noise ratio was
defined as optimal for image quality, without consideration
of personnel exposure or exposure to the subject. The
hypothesis was accepted regarding optimization based on
image quality if techniques other than the standard were
found to be optimal for different patient thicknesses.
Simulated personnel exposure was measured in mil-
liroentgen using the same dosimeter and ion chamber that
was used for the plate exposure response kinetics study de-
scribed above. The ion chamber was placed 5.1–43.2 cm
away from the edge of the patient phantom, directly on the
x-ray table. The variability in these distances was intended
to simulate locations where a radiographer’s body might be
while manually restraining varying sizes of patients during
a radiographic exposure. Personnel exposure was measured
10 times for each image acquisition and each technique. The
exposure values were then normalized from the center of
each phantom using the inverse square law and a standard
distance of 50 cm. As personnel exposure was our primary
concern, this exposure value was considered the figure of
merit, and was calculated using the equation below 1 :
FOM = (Sd NR)2/mR (4)
where FOM represents the figure of merit, Sd NR equals
the signal difference-to-noise ratio determined from equa-
tion 3, and mR is the personnel exposure measured by the
dosimeter in milliroentgen. Calculated figure of merit val-
ues were then plotted as a function of each test kVp. The
kVp that produced the highest figure of merit was defined
as optimal. The hypothesis was accepted regarding opti-
mization based on figure of merit if techniques other than
the standard were found to be optimal for different patient
thicknesses.

Discussion
Herein we evaluated two alternative objective approaches
to optimizing kVp for an indirect flat panel digital de-
tector. The first focused solely on image quality quanti-
fied by the signal difference-to-noise ratio. The second was
based on the balance between image quality and person-
nel exposure. When image quality is of greatest importance
and no personnel are present during image acquisition, a
kVp higher than 100 results in a higher signal difference-
to-noise ratio for all but the smallest patients. The high-
est signal difference-to-noise ratio maximizes image qual-
ity without regard to personnel exposure. For larger pa-
tients, images are underexposed if a fixed kVp of 100 is
used, resulting in image noise and degradation of image
quality.
Using our figure of merit, a kVp lower than 100 was
found to be optimal for small patients. A kVp of 100 was
optimal for most medium-sized patients. Using the test kVp
of 100 in small patients resulted in an overexposed image
and unnecessary personnel exposure.
It is important to realize that the figure of merit-based
techniques proposed in this study lead to images with a
lower signal difference-to-noise ratio. Essentially, reduced
image quality is accepted to decrease radiation exposure
to technical staff. It is clear that, based on this principle
a standard technique of 100 kVp/2.5 mAs would have a
high figure of merit in the majority of patients, due to de-
creased overall exposure and smaller measured exposure
to personnel. A digitally underexposed image would result
from increased noise and decreased signal difference-to-
noise ratio in these instances. For very large patients, a kVp
higher than the standard 100 should be used regardless of
the optimization method.
We focused on image quality by measuring quantifiable
characteristics of the image, namely the signal-to-noise ra-
tio. Classifying an image as being of diagnostic quality may
not always mean that it is a high quality image based on
objective criteria. If a diagnosis can be made from a noisy
image then it might be considered of diagnostic quality.
The diagnostic quality of the images produced with this
experiment was not evaluated as the images were not re-
viewed subjectively to determine lesion detectability. While
expected to provide a better balance between personnel
dose and image quality, the techniques proposed in this
study would require validation via clinical trials in order to
confirm this theory.
An important finding was that the detector response
to radiation was not linear. It was assumed that most
detectors would have a linear response to exposure, and
therefore allow a wide exposure latitude.5 Making the as-
sumption of a linear response rather than quantifying it
would have resulted in different conclusions since detec-
tor noise would not have been accounted for. To ensure
our data were linearized and properly calculated, the de-
tector’s response to exposure needed to be incorporated
into the experimental calculations. We conclude that the
detector’s response to exposure has to be known before ex-
posure optimization is quantified. This novel approach sets
a standard for future studies in veterinary medicine that
address technique optimization of a digital detector based
system.

Multiple factors affect exposure optimization when
based on personnel exposure. These include the size of the
phantom (i.e. the patient), kVp, mAs, distance, and loca-
tion of the radiation detector as well as the type of image
detector. In a study evaluating personnel exposure to those
assisting during medical, dental, and veterinary examina-
tions, it was determined that dose levels to assistants of
both small and large animal studies were strongly depen-
dent on the angle of the person relative to the scattered
radiation.11 Not all of these variables could be addressed in
this study. Optimizing the mAs portion of the radiographic
technique would have required complex calculations, an ex-
ponential number of images and resulted in overwhelming
tube load. This study relied upon using a clinically relevant
mAs for each patient thickness and not varying for each
kVp, therefore mAs was not optimized. It has been con-
sidered feasible to lower the kVp in digital radiographs of
the human chest as structural contrast would be improved
and the detective quantum efficiency of the digital detector
would increase.12 As the contrast resolution and sensitivity

Since kVp has minimal influence on the contrast of
a digital radiographic image, technique charts for digi-
tal radiography systems have been overly simplified com-
pared to those of screen-film systems. 13 Based on our re-
sults, when using a Canon CXDI-50G indirect flat panel
detector, a more sophisticated approach to selecting an
exposure would be necessary. Others have evaluated or
discussed the amount of personnel exposure during re-
straint of patients during small animal imaging studies,
though technique optimization was not a goal.11, 14 Sim-
ilar to our results, it was concluded that personnel dose
was extremely low, in the range of 2–49 microsievert. In
this range, personnel exposure would not be expected to
exceed the annual occupational dose for adults of 0.5 siev-
ert set forth by the institution’s state Radiation Protection
Commission,15 especially when compared to a recent study
of operator exposure during hand holding of portable x-
ray units for equine imaging.16 In conclusion, by incor-
porating the principles of ALARA into technique opti-
mization using an objective endpoint, a single technique
is not appropriate for all imaging studies of small animal
patients.
