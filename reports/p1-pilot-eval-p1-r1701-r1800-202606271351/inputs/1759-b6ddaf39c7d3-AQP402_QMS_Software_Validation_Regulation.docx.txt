Purpose

These regulations apply to the software (hereinafter referred to as "QMS software") used for our quality management system, production, service provision, monitoring, and measurement. The purpose is to register, manage, and validate QMS software that impacts the quality of these products (medical devices).

Applied Area

This regulation applies to the validation of QMS software and covers, but is not limited to, the following.

1) Software used in the quality management system.

Examples: ERP (limited to electronic approval, lot and material entry, etc.),

cleanroom monitoring programs, vision equipment for specific products,

software included in CNC equipment, etc.

2) Software used in production and service provision.

Examples: Software used for process management, software purchased,

developed, maintained, or modified for process management purposes or

automated production, software that automatically controls certain

processes, etc.

3) Software used for monitoring and measuring specified requirements.

Examples: Software for measuring products on a coordinate measuring

machine, software that analyzes sterilization process parameters and

determines whether the process meets process requirements, etc.

Work Procedures

General

QMS software must be validated before initial use and managed throughout its life cycle [Development phase - Maintenance phase - Retirement phase].

Risk analysis and change management should be repeated throughout the life cycle.

If any of the following situations occur in relation to the use of QMS software, use of the software must be stopped immediately and revalidation of the QMS software must be performed.

1) Changes to QMS software (e.g., version updates)

2) Changes to the application of QMS software

3) QMS software errors

4) Other problems related to QMS software

Determination of QMS software

The Quality Management Team considers the following questions to determine whether the software is QMS software. If the answer to any of the questions below is “yes,” it is determined that the software requires validation.

1) Do defects or potential defects in the software affect the quality or safety

of the medical device?

2) Does the software automate or perform activities required by regulatory

requirements (specifically, requirements for a medical device quality

management system)?

Examples: capturing electronic signatures and/or records, maintaining

product traceability, performing and capturing test results, maintaining

data logs such as CAPA, non-conformances, complaints, calibrations, etc.

Software Registration and Distribution Archive

The quality management team registers the software in the software registration ledger (402-3) before distributing the software, and ensures that the latest version status, etc. are known at the time of distribution.

After software distribution, register the computer of the receiving department to ensure that the latest version is continuously supplied, and if a revised version is distributed, indicate this and identify it.

The original and license of the software are managed by specifying a storage location for identification.

Each software is managed by assigning a management number.

QMS Software Risk Management

Through the ISO 14971 risk management process, risk factors that may occur in software are identified and analyzed, evaluated, controlled, and residual risk assessed.

Depending on the intended use, the identification and estimation of hazardous situations must be identified.

1) Risks to medical devices

2) Risks to the manufacturing process

3) Risks to manufacturing personnel or the environment

4) Risks to regulatory compliance

Conduct an assessment to determine whether the software's risks are acceptable.

In cases where it is difficult to estimate the likelihood of occurrence of a hazard, the acceptance criteria are based solely on severity.

Acceptance criteria utilize a tool box in relation to the selection of predefined software process control methods.

Establish a software validation plan

Initial validation is based entirely on input from the process failure risk analysis.

Validation planning activities identify when critical thinking should be applied, use input from the process risk analysis to establish criteria for the level of effort required for documentation, and guide tool selection in the toolbox definition section.

The toolbox should be utilized appropriately as a confidence-building activity to be performed to meet the intent of the validation requirements.

Process

Development Phase - Definition: This includes defining process requirements, analyzing process failure risks, implementing intended use, conducting validation planning, reviewing formal software requirements, selecting a software development lifecycle model, developing a risk management plan, and identifying risk control measures within the manufacturing or business process.

Development Phase - Implementation: This includes software failure analysis, documentation and review of the architecture, design specifications, development and design reviews, identifying risk control measures within the design, code review or verification, traceability analysis, and vendor audits.

Development Phase - Testing: This includes optional software test plans, unit testing, data verification, integration testing, use case testing, interface testing, regression testing, vendor-supplier testing, software system testing, use case testing, normal case testing, robustness (stress) testing, output force testing, input combination testing, beta testing, and performance testing.

Development Phase - Deployment: Optionally, user procedure review, in-house training for implementation, installation qualification, operational and performance qualification, final acceptance testing, and operator certification may be implemented.

Maintenance Phase: Optionally, software maintenance planning, known issue analysis, compatibility testing, infrastructure compatibility analysis, system monitoring, backup and recovery processes, operational control, and regression analysis may be implemented.

Maintenance phase

It starts after the software is released and put into use.

Maintenance phase activities are activities that ensure that the software remains in a validated state.

Corrective maintenance: Changes to correct software errors and defects.

Complete maintenance: Changes to improve performance, maintainability, and other software properties.

Adaptive maintenance: Changes performed to update the software operating environment.

If the intended use of the software changes, validation must be performed for the new intended use, and even if there is no change, maintenance must be evaluated and managed.

Retirement Phase

A method for accessing relevant electronic records during the required retention period when software is disposed of must be established.

Software disposal is determined by considering the following and is recorded and managed.

Is there a replacement software for the deprecated software?

Can the data be migrated to new software?

Does the data need to be migrated to a portable format for long-term preservation?

What are the data retention requirements for each data type?

Is the data stored on durable media?

What are the storage guidelines or procedures, and is the data retrievable with all relevant requirements?

What are the procedures for maintaining durable media and the software that can read it?

Is the archived hardware platform stored for use and retrieval of deprecated applications?

How is the stored hardware managed?

Is access to deprecated software required as part of a complaint or CAPA investigation?

Documenting Software Validation

Validation Plan

Using the inputs from the process risk analysis, determine the level of documentation effort required and select a toolbox.

Using the inputs from the software risk analysis, select implementation, testing, and deployment tools from the toolbox.

Definition

Definition Block Requirements: Define the activities completed within the definition block as processes and define the intended use of the software.

Process Requirements: Define the overall purpose and function of the process, as well as the purpose and function of the controlled portions.

Process Failure Risk Analysis: The relationship between the ultimate safety and effectiveness of the software and medical device is considered during the risk analysis process, including human, regulatory, and environmental risks.

Risk of human harm: This includes direct harm to patients and users, as well as indirect harm resulting from the malfunction of software that controls the manufacturing or quality of medical devices.

Regulatory risk: Consider the risk of software failure resulting in the loss of records required by regulatory agencies (e.g., CAPA, complaints, medical device history files), and deviations from quality systems and manufacturing procedures.

Environmental risk: This includes risks to the environment in which the software operates, both physical and virtual.

Validation Plan: This plan is based on input from the process failure risk analysis. The plan should provide objective evidence demonstrating the application of critical thinking.

Intended Use of the Software: The purpose and intent must be clear, including the intended use and regulatory use of the software. The software requirements must be defined in detail.

Purpose and Intent of the Software: This plan should include information on the intended use, regulatory use, and boundaries of the software.

Software Use: Consider what, why, how, who, where, and when the software will be used. This can be determined through questions.

Regulatory Use: Identify potential harm to people (other than medical device users) and the environment caused by the software. Consider regulations such as public health, safety, and the validity of electronic records and signatures.

Software Boundaries: Consider boundaries within the process and with other software.

Boundaries within the process: The software's role within the process

Boundaries with other software: It is important to check all interfaces.

Requirements for software use: Consideration should be given to both the user's perspective and the product requirements.

Software requirements: Considered as input to the system's design, configuration, and testing activities.

Implementation, Testing, and Deployment

The level of rigor of design validation should be considered in planning, developing and configuring, building the software, and testing the software based on identified risks.
