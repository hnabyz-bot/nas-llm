Overview

Document Overview

This document provides cybersecurity risk analysis with a structured threat modeling analysis for the Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector. The STRIDE methodology is used to identify and mitigate potential threats to the device’s security and integrity.

Medical Device Overview

Product name: Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector

Model name: G1417CW/G1717CW/G1417FW/G1717FW/

G1417CWP/G1717CWP/G1417FWP/G1717FWP

Manufacturer: H&abyz Co., Ltd.

This Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector (Model: G1417CW/G1717CW/G1417FW/G1717FW/

G1417CWP/G1717CWP/G1417FWP/G1717FWP) consists of a scintillator-based digital X-ray imaging panel, an image processing board, a wireless communication module, batteries and a protective enclosure.

The detector is designed to capture X-ray images and transmit them to an image acquisition workstation via wired or wireless communication.

This device is a battery-powered Digital Flat Panel X-ray Detector, designed and manufactured by H&abyz Co., Ltd. Compared with conventional imaging methods, this device offers a lightweight, compact design, high image resolution, and fast image acquisition, making it suitable for use in various clinical and mobile environments.

The major components of the detector include the Scintillator Panel, Image Processing board, Batteries, Communicatiuon Module, and Protective Enclosure.

The detector operates in conjunction with an X-ray generator, image acquisition software, and a computer workstation for image processing and analysis. The cybersecurity risks associated with the G Series Detectors have been evaluated to ensure no new or increased risks are introduced. Identified risks have been mitigated through risk management measures outlined in the system’s cybersecurity framework.

Assets

A1: Configuration - Receiving X-ray exposure settings (kV, mAs, APR values) from the generator

A2: Calibration - Gain Correction, offset correction, and pixel sensitivity adjustment

A3: Data acquisition - Capturing and converting X-ray signals into digital data

A4: Image storage - Storing acquired images in internal memory before transmission

A5: Wireless Communication – Data Transfer via Wi-Fi module for remote image transmission

A6: Wired Communication – Ethernet-based data transmission to workstations

A7: Battery Management – Monitoring charge levels, voltage, and power efficiency

A8: Error Monitoring – Detecting abnormal voltage, overheating, and communication failures

A9: Synchronization – Coordinating exposure timing with the X-ray generator

A10: Display Interface – LED indicators for power, battery status, and connection status

A11: SID (Source-to-Image Distance) – Calibration reference for accurate distance measurements

A12: Hardware Interfaces - Physical connection ports

Threat modeling Summary

The threat modeling document for the Medical Device, G Series Detector (Manufacture: H&abyz Co., Ltd) aims to evaluate potential cybersecurity threats and pinpoint vulnerabilities within the device. This comprehensive assessment identifies possible attack vectors, evaluates the likelihood and impact of various threats, and proposes mitigations to enhance the security posture. By systematically analyzing the device's architecture, data flow, and interfaces, the document ensures that security measures are robust and aligned with industry standards. In total NN initial risks have been identified during the threat modeling process:

The key findings and outcomes of each test are summarized below:

a) IEC 60601-4-5 Compliance Testing (RT): Ensures medical devices meet requirement for cybersecurity (NN initial risks)

b) CWE Vulnerability Assessment (VT): Detects and analyzes weaknesses to prevent cyber threats (NN initial risks)

c) Penetration Testing (PT): Simulates attacks to identify and fix security vulnerabilities in systems. (N initial risks)

Data-Flow Diagram

A Data-Flow Diagram (DFD) in medical device cybersecurity is a graphical representation that maps out the flow of data within a medical system. It highlights how data moves between devices, systems, and users, identifying points where data is input, processed, stored, and output. By visualizing these flows, DFDs help identify potential vulnerabilities in data handling, transmission, and storage, ensuring the security and integrity of medical imaging data.

The report's diagram contains the following views:

Global System View - Provides an overview of how data flows across the entire system, including connections between the detector, workstation, and PACS.

Multi-Patient Harm View - Assesses cybersecurity risks that could impact multiple patients, such as unauthorized access to stored X-ray images.

Updateability/Patchability View - Analyzes how firmware and software updates are managed to mitigate vulnerabilities.

Security Use Case View(s) - Focuses on specific security scenarios, such as data encryption during image transmission or access control mechanisms for the detector.

These views are visually represented in the diagram and are explained in detail through the accompanying legend. The legend helps to clarify the purpose and scope of the depicted views in the diagram.

Threat Identification and Methodology

Threat Methodology: The STRIDE methodology is selected for its comprehensiveness in identifying Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and Elevation of Privilege, which are relevant to the G Series Detector security needs.

Rationale for STRIDE: STRIDE provides a structured approach to identifying threats that could compromise the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the system, making it suitable for complex systems where multiple interaction points exist, such as medical devices.
